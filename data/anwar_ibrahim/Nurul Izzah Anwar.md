'''Nurul Izzah binti Anwar'''{{Efn|[[Jawi script|Jawi]]: {{lang|ms-arab|{{Script|Arab|نور العزة أنور}}}}<ref>{{Cite news |date=2023-01-30 |title=أنور يدافع عن تعيين ابنته مستشارة في وزارة المالية رغم الانتقادات |trans-title=Anwar defends the appointment of his daughter as an adviser to the Ministry of Finance despite criticism |url=https://aswaqpress.com/anwar-defends-appoitning-his-daughter-as-advisor/ |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230130030036/https://aswaqpress.com/anwar-defends-appoitning-his-daughter-as-advisor/ |archive-date=2023-01-30 |access-date=2025-10-17 |work=[[Aswaq]] |language=ar}}</ref>}} (born 19 November 1980) is a Malaysian politician who served as the [[Parliament of Malaysia|Member of Parliament]] for [[Permatang Pauh (federal constituency)|Permatang Pauh]] from 2018 to 2022. A member of the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]] and the eldest daughter of the 10th prime minister, [[Anwar Ibrahim]], she also represented [[Lembah Pantai (federal constituency)|Lembah Pantai]] in the Parliament of Malaysia from 2008 to 2018.

Born in [[Kuala Lumpur]] into politician family of Anwar Ibrahim and [[Wan Azizah Wan Ismail]], Nurul Izzah graduated from [[Johns Hopkins University]] in 2007 with a [[master's degree]] in [[international relations]]. She became publicly active in 1998 during her father Anwar's [[Anwar Ibrahim sodomy trials|dismissal and imprisonment]], participating in the {{Lang|ms|[[Reformasi (Malaysia)|Reformasi]]}} movement and co-founding the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]] in 1999. She entered parliament in 2008, winning the Lembah Pantai seat, and campaigned on [[Multiracialism|multiracial inclusivity]], [[government accountability]], and [[social justice]]. In parliament, she advocated [[open government]], [[equitable development]], increased representation of [[Women in Malaysia|women]] and youth, and policies based on need rather than [[Bumiputera (Malaysia)|ethnicity]].

In 2010, two years after joining parliament, Nurul Izzah was elected as the PKR's youngest vice president. She retained her Lembah Pantai seat in the [[2013 Malaysian general election|2013 general election]] and was active in promoting [[political reform]], transparency, and parliamentary oversight. She faced several detentions and investigations related to her activism, including under the [[Sedition Act 1948|Sedition Act]], and engaged in advocacy on [[Sabah]] sovereignty and electoral matters. In 2018, she won the Permatang Pauh seat, served on [[parliamentary committees]], and led initiatives such as the [[TVET (Technical and Vocational Education and Training)|TVET]] Empowerment Committee and the [[Multidimensional Poverty Index]] report. She resigned as PKR vice-president in 2018 but was reappointed for the 2022–2025 term. After losing her parliamentary seat in 2022, she served as an adviser on [[Economic policy|economic]] and [[social policy]] and chaired the [[think tank]] Social & Economic Research Initiative. In 2025, she was elected deputy president of PKR, focusing on party unity and engagement with [[grassroots]] members.

Nurul Izzah has advocated for greater political participation by women and strengthened legal protections for women and families in Malaysia. She has engaged in [[civil rights]] and policy reform, including [[Malaysian Prison Department|national prison]] reform, parliamentary reforms such as establishing a central authority for TVET, and introducing [[private member's bill]]s on issues including hate crimes, the Sedition Act, the Petroleum Development Act, and [[Mass media in Malaysia|media]] regulation. As MP for Permatang Pauh, she contributed to [[Health in Malaysia|public health]], women's [[Vocational education|vocational development]], [[Poverty in Malaysia|poverty studies]], and recognition of technical and vocational skills. She has also supported Palestinian rights, calling for an end to the [[Israeli occupation]] and submitting protest notes to multiple embassies with parliamentary and civil society backing. Domestically, she has emphasised the need for locally rooted leadership in Sabah, accountability over the [[Gaza Freedom Flotilla#2025|Freedom Flotilla]] incident, and careful review of [[corporal punishment]] in schools.

Nurul Izzah advocated [[open government]], [[equitable development]], and putting an end to race-based patronage following her entry into the parliament. She supported broadcasting parliamentary sittings and greater [[public scrutiny]] and investment, describing her experience in the [[Dewan Rakyat]] as demanding but essential to ensuring government accountability. She promoted the representation of [[Women in Malaysia|women in politics]] to correct gender imbalance, emphasised [[Pakatan Rakyat]]'s commitment to democratic change and [[social justice]] despite federal-level resistance, and demanded increased political participation by youth. She also supported transparency in [[public spending]], community participation in governance, and policies determined by need rather than ethnicity.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2008-06-11 |title=Face to Face: Nurul Izzah Anwar |url=https://www.malaysia-today.net/2008/06/11/face-to-face-nurul-izzah-anwar/ |archive-url=https://archive.today/20251019020012/https://www.malaysia-today.net/2008/06/11/face-to-face-nurul-izzah-anwar/ |archive-date=2025-10-19 |access-date=2025-10-19 |website=[[Malaysia Today]]}}</ref>

=== Vice President of PKR (2010–2018) ===
Nurul Izzah became the youngest person to hold the position of vice-president of the PKR at the age of 30, two years after she joined the Dewan Rakyat in 2008.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Gooch |first=Liz |date=2010-12-24 |title=A Daughter of Malaysia Finds Her Political Star Rising |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2010/12/25/world/asia/25malay.html |url-access=subscription |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20101226072319/https://www.nytimes.com/2010/12/25/world/asia/25malay.html |archive-date=2010-12-26 |access-date=2025-08-04 |work=[[The New York Times]]}}</ref><ref name=":5" /> Her appointment was officially announced on 28 November 2010 during the PKR's [[2010 People's Justice Party leadership election|7th National Congress]]. === Deputy President of PKR (2025–present) ===
[[File:Muhammad Yunus Presents Book To Nurul Izzah Anwar 2025-07-27 (PID-0000698).jpg|thumb|[[Chief Adviser of Bangladesh|Chief Advisor]] [[Muhammad Yunus]] presents ''Art of Triumph'' to Nurul Izzah at State Guest House Jamuna, 27 July 2025]]
Following the announcement that the office would be contested in the [[2025 People's Justice Party leadership election|PKR leadership election]], Nurul Izzah ran for the PKR deputy presidency in May 2025. Her candidacy emerged amid intra-party tensions, particularly after Rafizi threatened to resign following divisional election losses. She was reportedly supported by over two-thirds of PKR divisions and became Rafizi's strongest challenger. Rafizi's HIRUK campaign criticised the party's treatment of original reformists and leadership accountability, while Nurul Izzah's DAMAI platform emphasised [[party cohesion]] and unity. The contest attracted national attention, and she won with 9,803 votes compared to Rafizi's 3,966. She reportedly offered him a role in the new leadership, which he declined. Her position was further consolidated as supporters of her faction retained other senior party roles.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Yeoh |first=Tricia |date=2025-09-22 |title=The 2025 PKR Party Election: Feuding, Factionalism and the Future of the People's Justice Party |url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/ISEAS_Perspective_2025_71.pdf |archive-url=https://archive.today/20251019032539/https://www.iseas.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/ISEAS_Perspective_2025_71.pdf |archive-date=2025-10-19 |access-date=2025-10-19 |website=[[ISEAS – Yusof Ishak Institute]] |pages=9–10 |issn=2335-6677}}</ref>

Nurul Izzah formally assumed the PKR deputy presidency on 23 May 2025. Her familial connection to Anwar sparked public discourse and responses regarding nepotism and dynastic politics in Malaysia.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Amir Yusof |date=2025-05-23 |title=Malaysia PM Anwar's daughter Nurul Izzah elected as PKR's deputy president, becomes his new heir-apparent |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-pm-anwars-daughter-nurul-izzah-elected-pkrs-deputy-president-becomes-his-new-heir-apparent-5149786 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20250724142948/https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-pm-anwars-daughter-nurul-izzah-elected-pkrs-deputy-president-becomes-his-new-heir-apparent-5149786 |archive-date=2025-07-24 |access-date=2025-08-04 |work=CNA}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last=Mohamad Fadli |date=2025-05-22 |title=Win or lose, Rafizi's forever a friend, says Nurul Izzah |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2025/05/22/win-or-lose-rafizis-forever-a-friend-says-nurul-izzah |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20250527162322/https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2025/05/22/win-or-lose-rafizis-forever-a-friend-says-nurul-izzah |archive-date=2025-05-27 |access-date=2025-08-04 |work=Free Malaysia Today}}</ref> Both Nurul Izzah and Anwar dismissed these claims, emphasising that she had secured the position through intra-party elections. As deputy president, she was preoccupied with speaking to PKR [[grassroots]] and failed candidates in party polls, calling for unity and involvement by the party for national election results.<ref name=":11" />

== Activism and advocacy ==
Nurul Izzah advocated for greater political participation by women to strengthen legal protections for women and families in Malaysia.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web |last=Anis Hazim |date=2022-11-15 |title=Top women in Malaysian politics |url=https://themalaysianreserve.com/2022/11/15/top-women-in-malaysian-politics/ |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231226092416/https://themalaysianreserve.com/2022/11/15/top-women-in-malaysian-politics/ |archive-date=2023-12-26 |access-date=2025-08-04 |website=The Malaysian Reserve}}</ref> She engaged in [[civil rights]] and policy reform campaigns, including [[Malaysian Prison Department|national prison]] reform as a member of the All Party Parliamentary Group for the Reform of All Places of Detention,<ref>{{Cite web |last=Augustin |first=Sean |date=2021-02-12 |title=Only 500 of 11,000 eligible detainees freed, says Nurul Izzah |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2021/02/12/only-500-of-11000-eligible-detainees-freed-says-nurul-izzah |access-date=2025-08-04 |website=Free Malaysia Today}}</ref> and promoted parliamentary reforms such as a central authority for TVET<ref name=":12" /> with emphasis on public accountability and feedback from lower- and middle-income groups.<ref name=":13" /> She introduced seven [[private member's bill]]s covering racial and religious [[hate crimes]], the repeal of the Sedition Act, amendments to the Petroleum Development Act, and media regulation. As Permatang Pauh MP, she participated in projects on [[Health in Malaysia|public health]], women's [[Vocational education|vocational development]], and [[Poverty in Malaysia|poverty studies]], while also advocating for greater recognition of technical and vocational skills and qualifications.<ref name=":0" />

=== Interests ===
Outside of politics, Nurul Izzah likes to study books about politics and society, including ''Democracy in Iran: History and the Quest for Liberty'' and ''Memerdekakan Rakyat Memerdekakan Sendiri''. She also likes music and movies, and she says she likes bands like [[Radiohead]], [[The Strokes]], and [[The Killers]], as well as performers like [[Julianne Moore]] and [[Johnny Depp]]. For spiritual grounding, Nurul Izzah enjoys [[rock music]] and [[Quran]]ic recitations. She loves simplicity and genuineness in her familial ties and looks up to strong, resilient people like [[Aung San Suu Kyi]]. She enjoys swimming in her spare time and maintains that self-esteem inevitably leads to attractive appearance.<ref name=":8" />
