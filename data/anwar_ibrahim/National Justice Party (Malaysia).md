The '''National Justice Party''' ({{Langx|ms|Parti Keadilan Nasional}}'','' '''KeADILan''' or '''PKN''') was a [[Reformism|reformist]] [[political party]] in [[Malaysia]]. Founded in 1999, it was the electoral vehicle of the [[Reformasi (Malaysia)|Reformasi]] movement that followed the [[Anwar Ibrahim sodomy trials|dismissal and arrest]] of [[Deputy Prime Minister of Malaysia|Deputy Prime Minister]] [[Anwar Ibrahim]] in 1998. The party was established under the leadership of [[Wan Azizah Wan Ismail]] and drew support from [[Activism|activist]]s and [[Civil society organization|civil society group]]s demanding [[judicial independence]], [[anti-corruption|anti-corruption measures]] and [[political reform]].

The party contested its first [[Elections in Malaysia|general election]] in [[1999 Malaysian general election|1999]], entering [[Politics of Malaysia|national politics]] as a newly organised [[Opposition (Malaysia)|opposition]] force and winning [[Members of the Dewan Rakyat, 10th Malaysian Parliament|parliamentary representation]] as part of the broader post-1998 [[Barisan Alternatif|opposition realignment]]. The party's founding and early mobilisation are widely credited with institutionalising Reformasi into [[Parliamentary system|parliamentary politics]] and providing a platform for [[Multiracialism|multi-ethnic]] [[Reformism|reformist]] campaigning.

After several years of negotiations with [[Left-wing politics|left-wing]] groups, the party merged with the [[Socialism|socialist]] [[Parti Rakyat Malaysia|Malaysian People's Party]] (''Parti Rakyat Malaysia'', PRM) on 3 August 2003 to form the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]] (PKR), a broader national party that carried forward KeADILan's reformist agenda and became a major component of later [[Pakatan Rakyat|opposition]] and [[Pakatan Harapan|governing coalition]]s.

== History ==
=== Background ===
{{Main|Reformasi (Malaysia)|Anwar Ibrahim sodomy trials}}

The [[Economy of Malaysia|Malaysian economy]] was badly hit by the [[1997 Asian financial crisis|Asian financial crisis]] in 1997 when [[Minister of Finance (Malaysia)|Finance Minister]] [[Anwar Ibrahim]], concurrently serving as [[Deputy Prime Minister of Malaysia|Deputy Prime Minister]], implemented several [[Microeconomic reform|economic reform]]s and [[austerity]] measures in response. He instituted an austerity package that cut government spending by 18%, cut ministerial salaries, and deferred major projects. This was despite it being a cornerstone of the government's development strategy, and the reforms were thus seen as challenging the administration's policies. The situation was exacerbated when he introduced controversial amendments to the [[Prevention of Corruption Act]] that sought to increase the powers of the [[Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission|Anti-Corruption Agency]] (ACA). 

Prime Minister [[Mahathir Mohamad]] strongly disagreed with the measures, and towards the end of the 1990s, their relationship began to deteriorate, triggered by their conflicting views on governance.<ref name="split">{{cite web |author=Wong Chin Huat |date=17 August 2007 |title=Splits in Umno and Opposition unity |url=https://www.malaysianbar.org.my/article/news/legal-and-general-news/general-news/splits-in-umno-and-opposition-unity |access-date=29 September 2021 |work=[[The Sun (Malaysia)|The Sun]] |via=[[Malaysian Bar]]}}</ref> Eventually, Mahathir dismissed Anwar from his cabinet positions on 2 September 1998 before expelling him from [[UMNO]] the day after. This incident eventually led to Anwar's subsequent arrest and [[detention without trial]] on September 20 on what many believed to be politically motivated [[Anwar Ibrahim sodomy trials|allegations]] of [[sexual misconduct]] and [[corruption]]. While he was in police custody, Anwar was beaten by the then [[Inspector-General of Police (Malaysia)|Inspector-General of Police]], [[Abdul Rahim Mohd Noor]]. The incident and the circumstances in which it occurred led to [[Protest|public outcry]] in what became known as the [[Reformasi (Malaysia)|Reformasi]] movement.

=== Early years ===
During the autumn of 1998 and early 1999, as waves of street protests and civil society organising swept Malaysia, new civic groups coalesced around demands for [[judicial independence]], an end to [[cronyism]], and [[political reform]]. These developments created the preconditions for a new political vehicle to channel the Reformasi movement into electoral politics.<ref name="ISEAS1998">{{cite web |last=Chai |first=James |date=2024 |title=Party of Hardship: The Evolution of Malaysia's Parti Keadilan Rakyat |url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/TRS15_24.pdf |access-date=15 December 2025 |website=ISEAS - Yusof Ishak Institute |quote=10 December 1998 ADIL was officially launched, with Wan Azizah as its first president; 4 April 1999 Parti Keadilan Nasional (PKN) was officially launched.}}</ref><ref name="PIIE1999">{{cite web |last=Hunt |first=Michael |title=Crisis, Political Change, and Economic Reform |url=https://www.piie.com/publications/chapters_preview/107/3iie2830.pdf |access-date=15 December 2025 |website=Peterson Institute for International Economics |quote=Pergerakan Keadilan Sosial (ADIL) was founded in December 1998 and then the National Justice Party (Keadilan) the following April.}}</ref> At that time, two people's movements were established:

* The [[Malaysian Islamic Party|Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party]] (PAS), under the leadership of President [[Fadzil Noor]], together with political parties such as the [[Democratic Action Party]] (DAP), the [[Parti Rakyat Malaysia|Malaysian People's Party]] (PRM), and Islamic NGOs such as the [[Malaysian Islamic Youth Movement]] (ABIM), established the Malaysian People's Justice Movement Council (''Majlis Gerakan Keadilan Rakyat Malaysia'', GERAK).

* [[Suara Rakyat Malaysia]] (SUARAM), in collaboration with political parties such as PAS, PRM, and DAP, along with other human rights organisations, also established a multiracial movement known as ''Gagasan Demokrasi Rakyat'' (GAGASAN).

The establishment of GERAK and GAGASAN was not solely to support the release of Anwar Ibrahim, but also draw attention to alleged injustices committed by the Malaysian government at the time, led by Prime Minister [[Mahathir Mohamad]].<ref>{{Cite book|title=Personalized Politics: The Malaysian State Under Mahathir|last=In-wŏn Hwang|first=|publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies|year=2003|isbn=9812301852|location=Singapura|pages=314-315}}</ref>

After Anwar's arrest, his wife, [[Wan Azizah Wan Ismail]], decided to build upon the momentum of Reformasi by convening [[Civil society organization|civil society actor]]s and [[Activism|activist]]s to form the Social Justice Movement (''Pergerakan Keadilan Sosial'', ADIL) on 10 December 1998. Initially planned to serve as a [[non-governmental organisation]] vehicle for the Reformasi movement,<ref name="JSTOR_ADIL">{{cite web |title=Malaysia's Tenth Elections: Status Quo, "Reformasi" or Islamization? |url=https://www.jstor.org/stable/25798478 |access-date=15 December 2025 |website=JSTOR |quote=Pergerakan Keadilan Sosial (ADIL) was launched on 10 December 1998 as the specific vehicle of the reform movement.}}</ref> ADIL faced obstacles to formal party registration from the [[:ms:Jabatan Pendaftaran Pertubuhan Malaysia|Registrar of Societies]] (RoS), which was under the jurisdiction of the [[Ministry of Home Affairs (Malaysia)|Ministry of Home Affairs]] whose minister happened to be Mahathir Mohamad.<ref>{{Cite web |date=10 September 1999 |title=Lim Kit Siang |url=http://www.limkitsiang.com/archive/1999/Oct99/sg2012.htm |access-date=11 July 2011 |publisher=Lim Kit Siang}}</ref>

Due to difficulties in becoming a registered political party, Reformasi organisers instead made the decision to take over an existing minor party, the [[Ikatan Masyarakat Islam Malaysia|Malaysian Islamic Community Alliance]] (''Ikatan Masyarakat Islam Malaysia'', IKATAN), and reestablished it as the National Justice Party (''Parti Keadilan Nasional'', KeADILan) on 4 April 1999.<ref name="ISEAS1999">{{cite web |last=Chai |first=James |date=2024 |title=Party of Hardship: The Evolution of Malaysia's Parti Keadilan Rakyat |url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/TRS15_24.pdf |access-date=15 December 2025 |website=ISEAS-YusofIshakInstitute |quote=4 April 1999 Parti Keadilan Nasional (PKN) was officially launched at the Renaissance Hotel, taking over Ikatan Masyarakat Islam Malaysia.}}</ref> The new party leadership installed Wan Azizah as its inaugural president and positioned the organisation as a multi-ethnic, [[Reformism|reformist]] alternative to the ruling [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN).<ref>Amnesty International. [http://web.amnesty.org/library/Index/ENGASA280271998?open&of=ENG-381 The arrest of Anwar Ibrahim and his political associates.] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20060607215526/http://web.amnesty.org/library/Index/ENGASA280271998?open&of=ENG-381|date=7 June 2006}}</ref> The timing allowed the party to contest the 1999 general election.

=== Arrests ===
Between 27 and 30 September 1999, seven activists, including KeADILan leaders; Vice President [[Chua Tian Chang|Tian Chua]], [[Gobalakrishnan Nagapan|N. Gobalakrishnan]], Youth Chief [[Mohamad Ezam Mohd Nor]], [[Azmin Ali|Mohamed Azmin Ali]], Fairus Izuddin and Badrul Amin Baharun; were arrested and consequently barred from contesting the election. Further arrests were made on 10 April 2001, and those arrested were later charged and imprisoned under the [[Internal Security Act 1960|Internal Security Act]]. They became known as the '''Reformasi 10'''.

=== 1999 general election ===
The National Justice Party entered electoral politics immediately, contesting the [[1999 Malaysian general election|1999 general election]]. The party also joined with other opposition parties in the [[Barisan Alternatif]] (BA) coalition to present a united front against Barisan Nasional in multiple constituencies. In the election, KeADILan won several parliamentary seats, most notably in [[Permatang Pauh (federal constituency)|Permatang Pauh]], the former seat of [[Anwar Ibrahim]] where his wife, Wan Azizah, was elected. The 1999 results consolidated KeADILan's position as a new centre of opposition politics despite the ruling coalition retaining overall parliamentary dominance.<ref name="Amnesty19992">{{cite web |last=Amnesty International |title=Malaysia: Human rights developments and political background, 1999 |url=https://www.amnesty.org/en/documents/asa28/006/1999/en/ |access-date=15 December 2025 |website=Amnesty International |quote=Wan Azizah called for fairer policing and transparency in 1999 as the new party sought to translate Reformasi into electoral gains.}}</ref><ref name="FreeWorld20012">{{cite web |last=Refworld |title=Freedom in the World 2001 - Malaysia |url=https://www.refworld.org/reference/annualreport/freehou/2001/en/21470 |access-date=15 December 2025 |website=Refworld |quote=Anwar's wife, Wan Azizah Ismail, responded by founding Keadilan (the National Justice Party), which entered into a new, opposition Alternative Front coalition.}}</ref>

=== Merger into Parti Keadilan Rakyat ===
After the 1999 general election, there was a growing consensus that KeADILan and the [[Parti Rakyat Malaysia|Malaysian People's Party]] (PRM) needed to merge based on their shared commitment to Reformasi and opposition to injustice and abuse of power. Following the 1999–2002 period of consolidation, negotiations were held for a potential merger between KeADILan and PRM. Thus, on 3 August 2003, the two parties formally merged to establish the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]] (PKR) in [[Kuala Lumpur]].<ref name="Cambridge20252">{{cite web |last=Chai |first=James |date=2025 |title=The transformation of Malaysia's Parti Keadilan Rakyat |url=https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/southeast-asian-affairs-2024/party-of-hardship-the-evolution-of-malaysias-parti-keadilan-rakyat/9AF3D11FD7CCC1F92AE6970EB51D5360 |access-date=15 December 2025 |website=Cambridge University Press |quote=The 2003 merger created a sixty-two-member transitional supreme council and positioned Anwar as the party's life member and de facto opinion-maker.}}</ref> However, approval for the merger and PKR's subsequent establishment was only granted in 2004 after the [[2004 Malaysian general election|general election]]. The merger combined KeADILan's urban reformist base with PRM's socialist legacy, producing a broader national party structure under Wan Azizah's leadership with PRM president [[Syed Husin Ali]] serving as her deputy. Meanwhile, Anwar Ibrahim was designated as an advisor and symbolic figurehead while he served his prison sentence. The merger and the party's subsequent evolution were noted by scholars as successful in establishing PKR as a durable multi-ethnic reformist party in Malaysia's changing party system.<ref name="ISEAS20032">{{cite web |last=Chai |first=James |date=2024 |title=Party of Hardship: The Evolution of Malaysia's Parti Keadilan Rakyat |url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/TRS15_24.pdf |access-date=15 December 2025 |website=ISEAS - Yusof Ishak Institute |quote=After two years of painful negotiations, on 3 August 2003, Parti Keadilan Rakyat (PKR) came into being as a result of a merger between PKN and PRM.}}</ref>

=== Legacy ===
The National Justice Party represents the immediate institutionalisation of the Reformasi movement into party politics. Its establishment under Wan Azizah, rapid mobilisation in 1999, and the later merger to form PKR are widely regarded as key developments in Malaysia's post-1998 political realignment. The party's multi-ethnic, reformist orientation and its linkage to the broader Reformasi agenda laid the foundations for PKR's subsequent role in coalition politics and eventual participation in the federal government.

== Election results ==
The National Justice Party (KeADILan) participated in two general elections before the [[:ms:Jabatan Pendaftaran Pertubuhan Malaysia|Registrar of Societies]] (RoS) approved the name change to the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]] (PKR), namely in [[1999 Malaysian general election|1999]] and [[2004 Malaysian general election|2004]]. In the 2004 election, candidates from the [[Parti Rakyat Malaysia|Malaysian People's Party]] (PRM) contested on the KeADILan ticket as the merger of the two parties had yet to be approved. 

=== 1999 Malaysian general election ===
In the 1999 general election, KeADILan fielded 59 candidates for parliamentary seats and 94 candidates for state assembly seats. Of these, 5 candidates were successfully elected to the [[Dewan Rakyat]], while 4 were elected to the state assemblies, one each from [[Penang]], [[Perak]], [[Pahang]], and [[Selangor]].
