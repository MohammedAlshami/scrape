'''Wan Azizah binti Wan Ismail'''{{Efn|{{langx|ms-Arab|وان عزيزة وان إسماعيل|label=[[Jawi alphabet|Jawi]]}}}} (born 3 December 1952) is a Malaysian politician who served as the [[spouse of the prime minister of Malaysia]] as the wife of [[Anwar Ibrahim]]. A member of the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]], she served as [[Parliament of Malaysia|Member of Parliament]] (MP) for [[Bandar Tun Razak (federal constituency)|Bandar Tun Razak]] since 2022. She is the first woman to serve as [[Deputy Prime Minister of Malaysia|deputy prime minister of Malaysia]], a position she held from 2018 to 2020.

Born in the [[Colony of Singapore]], Wan Azizah graduated from the [[Royal College of Surgeons in Ireland]]. She practised medicine, specialised in ophthalmology, and served as a government doctor for 14 years before leaving in 1993. Entering politics in 1998 after her husband's dismissal as the [[Deputy Prime Minister of Malaysia|7th deputy prime minister]] of Malaysia, she led the {{Lang|ms|[[Reformasi (Malaysia)|Reformasi]]}} movement and founded the National Justice Party (PKN), later [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]] (PKR), becoming the second woman to lead a parliamentary party in Malaysia. She won the [[Permatang Pauh (federal constituency)|Permatang Pauh]] seat in [[1999 Malaysian general election|1999]], retained it in [[2004 Malaysian general election|2004]], and briefly stepped aside in [[2008 Malaysian general election|2008]] for her husband's return, but continued to lead PKR as it grew into an opposition force.

Wan Azizah became Malaysia's first female [[Leader of the Opposition (Malaysia)|leader of the Opposition]] in April 2008, but resigned later that year to allow her husband to succeed her. During her time in opposition, she spoke out on [[Human rights in Malaysia|human rights issues]], and was active in domestic debates on [[Malay supremacy]], governance, and land issues. She continued her political career, winning the [[2014 Kajang by-election|Kajang by-election]] in 2014 and later reclaiming [[Permatang Pauh (federal constituency)|Permatang Pauh]] in [[2015 Permatang Pauh by-election|2015]]. She won the [[Pandan (federal constituency)|Pandan]] seat in [[2018 Malaysian general election|2018]] and became Malaysia's first female deputy prime minister, while also serving as [[Minister of Women, Family and Community Development (Malaysia)|minister of women, family and community development]]. She left office during the [[2020–2022 Malaysian political crisis|2020 political crisis]] but returned to the [[Dewan Rakyat]] in [[2022 Malaysian general election|2022]] after winning the [[Bandar Tun Razak (federal constituency)|Bandar Tun Razak]] seat. Following her husband's appointment as [[Prime Minister of Malaysia|prime minister]] that year, she became the spouse of the prime minister.

On 30 April 2008, Wan Azizah became the first woman to hold the post of [[Leader of the Opposition (Malaysia)|Leader of the Opposition]].<ref>{{cite news |last=Lee |first=Yuk Peng |last2=Loh |first2=Foon Fong |date=2008-03-20 |title=PKR president poised to make history as first woman Opposition Leader |url=http://thestar.com.my/news/story.asp?file=/2008/3/20/nation/20702955&sec=nation |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110521101825/http://thestar.com.my/news/story.asp?file=%2F2008%2F3%2F20%2Fnation%2F20702955&sec=nation |archive-date=2011-05-21 |access-date=2025-07-28 |work=The Star}}</ref> Her position was acknowledged during coalition negotiations by the [[Malaysian United Indigenous Party]], led by [[Mahathir Mohamad]] and [[Muhyiddin Yassin]]. This recognition helped sustain the PKR's leadership role within the opposition despite internal divisions and uncertainty over succession.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Chai |first=James |url=https://www.google.com.au/books/edition/Party_of_Hardship_The_Evolution_of_Malay/8TQVEQAAQBAJ?hl=en&gbpv=1&dq=Wan+Azizah+opposition&pg=PA26&printsec=frontcover |title=Party of Hardship: The Evolution of Malaysia's Parti Keadilan Rakyat |date= |publisher=[[ISEAS – Yusof Ishak Institute]] |year=2024 |isbn=978-981-5203-62-2 |pages=26 }}</ref> That same year, Wan Azizah sent a letter to [[Tin Latt (diplomat)|Myanmar's ambassador]] expressing concern over the [[State Peace and Development Council|military junta]]'s decision to proceed with a [[2008 Myanmar constitutional referendum|constitutional referendum]] despite the ongoing [[Cyclone Nargis]] crisis. She submitted a motion urging [[Speaker of the Dewan Rakyat|Speaker]] [[Pandikar Amin Mulia]] to call for a postponement of the vote, to prioritise relief efforts and to press the regime to grant greater access to aid workers. She also called on the [[Third Abdullah cabinet|Malaysian government]] to use its influence to advocate for a delay, though the motion was rejected on the grounds of Malaysia's policy of neutrality and noninterference.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Rüland |first=Anchalee |url=https://www.google.com.au/books/edition/Norms_in_Conflict/I8IxEAAAQBAJ? |title=Norms in Conflict: Southeast Asia's Response to Human Rights Violations in Myanmar |date= |publisher=[[University Press of Kentucky]] |year=2022 |isbn=978-0-8131-8372-5 |pages=147 }}</ref> Her tenure as opposition leader ended on 28 August 2008,<ref>{{Cite news |date=2015-02-10 |title=Anwar Ibrahim: a timeline of political turmoil |url=https://www.abc.net.au/news/2015-02-10/anwar-ibrahim/6083644 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20240418102820/https://www.abc.net.au/news/2015-02-10/anwar-ibrahim/6083644 |archive-date=2024-04-18 |access-date=2025-07-28 |work=[[Australian Broadcasting Corporation]] |language=en-AU}}</ref> when she resigned to make way for her husband,<ref>{{Cite book |last=Bennett |first=Clinton |author-link=Clinton Bennett |url=https://www.google.com.au/books/edition/Muslim_Women_of_Power/zRhhvNHZRX8C? |title=Muslim Women of Power: Gender, Politics and Culture in Islam |date= |publisher=[[Continuum International Publishing Group|Continuum]] |year=2010 |isbn=978-0-8264-0087-1 |pages=5 }}</ref> who succeeded her.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2008-08-28 |title=Anwar sworn in as Malaysian MP |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2008/8/28/anwar-sworn-in-as-malaysian-mp |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210927000406/https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2008/8/28/anwar-sworn-in-as-malaysian-mp |archive-date=2021-09-27 |access-date=2025-08-19 |work=[[Al Jazeera]] }}</ref>
