The '''Democratic Action Party''' ('''DAP'''; {{langx|ms|Parti Tindakan Demokratik}}) is a [[social democracy|social democratic]] [[political party]] in [[Malaysia]], sitting on the [[Centre-left politics|centre-left]] of the political spectrum.<ref name="DAP leadership">{{cite web|url=http://www.dapmalaysia.org/newenglish/au_l_n.htm|title=About Us: Leadership|publisher=Democratic Action Party|access-date=12 February 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100612212743/http://dapmalaysia.org/newenglish/au_l_n.htm|archive-date=12 June 2010|url-status=dead}}</ref> As one of three component parties of the [[Pakatan Harapan]] (PH) coalition, it formed the federal government after defeating [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN) in the [[2018 Malaysian general election]], ending the party's 53 year-long stay in the opposition. However, before the coalition finished its first term, defections from partnering parties caused it to lose power after 22 months, culminating in the [[2020 Malaysian political crisis]]. At the [[2022 Malaysian general election]], the PH coalition which the DAP was part of was returned to power again, albeit without a majority, leading it to form a [[Anwar Ibrahim cabinet|unity coalition government]] with its traditional political rivals.

The DAP was founded in 1965 by Malaya–based members of the [[Singapore]]an [[People's Action Party]] (PAP), [[Chen Man Hin]] and [[Devan Nair]], shortly after [[independence of Singapore|Singapore's expulsion from Malaysia]]. Singapore's expulsion was in part due to intense [[PAP–UMNO relations|ideological differences]] between the PAP and the federal government, led by the [[United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO). The UMNO which favoured the idea of ''[[Ketuanan Melayu]]'' and [[Early Malay nationalism|Malay nationalism]] for the country. In contrast, the PAP favoured a more [[egalitarian]] and [[civic nationalist]] [[Malaysian Malaysia]], which the DAP would continue to espouse. Following the expulsion, the PAP was elected as the ruling government of a newly sovereign Singapore and would continue to operate on a platform of civic nationalism. However, unlike the DAP, the PAP became more [[conservative]] and moved towards the [[Centre-right politics|centre-right]].

Historically a [[Democratic socialism|democratic socialist]] party, the DAP draws much of its support from [[Progressivism|progressive]] voters with a stable electorate from voters of [[City|cities]], [[Coast|coastal regions]], the [[middle class]], and the [[working class]].<ref>{{Cite web|date=2021-05-12|title=Can Malaysia's DAP capture the Muslim and non-Chinese vote?|url=https://www.scmp.com/week-asia/politics/article/3133110/can-malaysias-dap-capture-muslim-and-non-chinese-vote-anthony|access-date=2021-11-26|website=South China Morning Post|language=en}}</ref><ref name=":0">{{Cite web|date=2021-09-07|title=2021/118 "Malaysia's Democratic Action Party (DAP): Background and Inner Workings" by Francis E. Hutchinson and Kevin Zhang|url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/articles-commentaries/iseas-perspective/2021-118-malaysias-democratic-action-party-dap-background-and-inner-workings-by-francis-e-hutchinson-and-kevin-zhang/|access-date=2021-11-26|website=ISEAS-Yusof Ishak Institute|language=en-US}}</ref> The party's strongholds are primarily in the [[List of cities and towns in Malaysia by population|urban and semi-urban areas]] of [[Penang]], [[Perak]], [[Selangor]], [[Negeri Sembilan]], [[Johor]], [[Malacca]] and the Federal Territory of [[Kuala Lumpur]].<ref name=":0" /> In the [[Malaysian general election, 2018|2018 Malaysian general election]], the party contested in 47 federal and 104 state [[List of Malaysian electoral districts|constituencies]] under the banner of its ally the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]],{{refn|group=nb|The party contested under the banner of the People's Justice Party as Pakatan Harapan was yet to be officially registered by the government, meaning its logo could not be used.}} winning 42 and 102 seats respectively, except in Sarawak, where the party's state branch chose to contest under its own banner.

=== Formation by ex-PAP members ===
The party is the direct successor of the [[Peninsular Malaya]]n branch of the [[Singapore]]–based [[People's Action Party]] (PAP), which was then known as the '''People's Action Party of Malaya (PAP–M)'''. [[Singapore in Malaysia|Singapore's expulsion]] from Malaysia led to its deregistration in 1965 by the Registrar of Societies (RoS).<ref name="History">Goh, Cheng Teik (1994). Malaysia: Beyond Communal Politics, p. 51. Pelanduk Publications. {{ISBN|967-978-475-4}}.</ref> The deregistered party's leadership consisted of [[Devan Nair]] (secretary-general), [[Chen Man Hin]] (chairman), D. P. Xavier (assistant secretary-general), [[Goh Hock Guan]] (vice-chairman) and Seeveratnam Sinnathamby (treasurer), who was the younger brother of Singapore minister [[S. Rajaratnam]], with Zain Azahari bin Zainal Abidin, Chin Chan Sung, Michael Khong Chye Huat, Tan Chong Bee and Too Chee Cheong as committee members.<ref>{{cite web |date=5 September 1965 |title=The 4 reasons why PAP Malaya should not be struck off |url=http://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/Digitised/Article/straitstimes19650905-1.2.17.30?ST=1 |access-date=24 May 2018 |work=The Straits Times |publisher=National Library Board |page=7}}</ref>

On 11 October that year, the name "Democratic Action Party (DAP)" was chosen and officially formed by the remnants of the PAP–M.<ref name=":2">{{Cite journal |last=Chew |first=Huat Hock |date=1980 |journal=[[The Australian National University]]|title=The Democratic Action Party in post-1969 Malaysian politics : the strategy of a determined opposition |url=https://openresearch-repository.anu.edu.au/handle/1885/128740 |language=en |doi=10.25911/5d7396292f6f4|hdl=1885/128740 }}</ref> However, the registration of the party was delayed to 18 March 1966 while the ruling [[Alliance Party (Malaysia)|Alliance Party]] laid out new conditions for the registration of political parties in the [[Societies Act 1966]]. While awaiting the party's registration, party chairman Chen Man Hin won the state constituency of Rahang, Negeri Sembilan as an independent in [[1965 Rahang by-election|December 1965]].<ref name=":2" /> In August that year, the official party organ, ''The Rocket'', was first published.

The party adopted the "Setapak Declaration" at the first DAP National Congress held on 29 July 1967, declaring itself "irrevocably committed to the ideal of a free, democratic and socialist Malaysia, based on the principles of racial and religious equality, social and economic justice and founded on the institution of [[parliamentary democracy]]", and later joined the [[Socialist International]] (SI), participating in the organisation's International Council in [[Zurich, Switzerland]].<ref name="pilihanraya">{{cite web |title=DAP Details |url=http://www.pilihanraya.com.my/English/DAP.asp |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100810235329/http://www.pilihanraya.com.my/english/DAP.asp |archive-date=10 August 2010 |access-date=7 November 2005 |publisher=11th General Election of Malaysia}}</ref>

=== Early years, 1969 election and crackdown ===
In 1967, the party was involved in discussions to form a united front of opposition parties against the Alliance coalition ahead of the [[1969 Malaysian general election|1969 general election]], although these efforts were eventually unsuccessful.<ref>{{Cite news |date=1967-04-18 |title=Opposition parties plan summit |pages=7 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19670418-1.2.55 |access-date=2023-11-09}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |date=1967-11-17 |title=Leaders to talk on big united front today |pages=24 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19671117-1.2.132 |access-date=2023-11-09}}</ref> In March 1968, the party reached an agreement with the [[People's Progressive Party (Malaysia)|People's Progressive Party]] (PPP) not to contest against each other in the state of [[Perak]] for the upcoming general election.<ref>{{Cite news |date=1968-03-18 |title=DAP and PPP 'no contest' pact in six places |pages=4 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19680318-1.2.25 |access-date=2023-11-09}}</ref> The DAP attempted to achieve a similar arrangement with the [[United Democratic Party (Malaysia)|United Democratic Party]] (UDP), whose base was in [[Penang]], but its leader [[Lim Chong Eu]] rejected the proposal and sarcastically thanked DAP secretary general [[Goh Hock Guan]] for "his very smug condescension."<ref>{{Cite news |date=1969-04-14 |title=DAP gets a firm 'no' from Dr. Lim |pages=18 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19680404-1.2.121 |access-date=2023-11-09}}</ref> Later that same month, the newly formed [[Parti Gerakan Rakyat Malaysia]] (Gerakan) led by [[Syed Hussein Alatas]] suggested that the DAP merge into the new party, an offer that was declined.<ref>{{Cite news |date=1968-03-29 |title=Devan: Why we won't merge with new party |pages=5 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19680329-1.2.24 |access-date=2023-11-09}}</ref> 

[[Devan Nair]], the co-founder of the DAP, returned to [[Singapore]] in 1969 after being withdrawn as a candidate for the general election that year.<ref>{{Cite news |date=1969-04-24 |title='Stop those insinuations about S'pore, says Dr. Chen |pages=7 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19690424-1.2.42 |access-date=2023-11-09}}</ref> [[Lee Kuan Yew]], at the time the [[Prime Minister of Singapore]], explained in 1981 that "the Cabinet decided that [[Singapore–Malaysia relations]] would always be bedevilled if Devan Nair remained a DAP leader. I persuaded him to come back". Nair eventually became the [[President of Singapore]] from 1981 to 1985.<ref>{{cite web |title=Speech by Prime Minister Lee Kuan Yew moving the motion on the election of Mr Chengara Veetil Devan Nair as President of the Republic of Singapore on 23 October 1981 at Parliament |url=http://www.nas.gov.sg/archivesonline/data/pdfdoc/lky19811023.pdf |access-date=24 May 2018 |work=Government of Singapore |publisher=National Archives of Singapore |page=4}}</ref> Eventually, the DAP contested a general election for the first time in 1969 together with both the PPP and Gerakan in an electoral pact but under separate banners.<ref>{{Cite news |date=1969-02-22 |title=DAP and GRM announce pact to contest the General Election |pages=1 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19690222-1.2.8 |access-date=2023-11-09}}</ref> In its campaign, the party opposed ''[[Bumiputera (Malaysia)|Bumiputera]]'' privileges guaranteed under [[Article 153 of the Constitution of Malaysia|Article 153 of the Constitution]] and continued Lee Kuan Yew's call for a "[[Malaysian Malaysia]]", which irked many Malay [[Ultra (Malaysia)|ultras]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.nas.gov.sg/archivesonline/data/pdfdoc/lky19650606.pdf|title=Text of Mr. Lee Kuan Yew's speech at the Malaysian Solidarity Convention at the National Theatre on 6th June 1965|work=Government of Singapore|publisher=National Archives of Singapore|access-date=24 May 2018|page=3|archive-date=25 April 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190425085720/http://www.nas.gov.sg/archivesonline/data/pdfdoc/lky19650606.pdf|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |date=1969-01-26 |title=DAP warns of the change to come |pages=5 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19690126-1.2.32?qt=dap&q=DAP |access-date=2023-11-09}}</ref> The "Malaysian Malaysia" idea was originally conveyed by Lee in the [[Dewan Rakyat]] prior to Singapore's expulsion: "Malaysia – to whom does it belong? To Malaysians. But who are Malaysians? I hope I am, Mr Speaker, Sir. But sometimes, sitting in this chamber, I doubt whether I am allowed to be a Malaysian".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.jeffooi.com/archives/2005/11/i_went_into_act.php|title=Perils of the sitting duck|author=Jeff Ooi|publisher=JeffOoi.com|date=6 November 2005|access-date=11 November 2005|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20051228132802/http://www.jeffooi.com/archives/2005/11/i_went_into_act.php|archive-date=28 December 2005|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|author=Alex Josey|title=Lee Kuan Yew: The Crucial Years|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=gQ-JAAAAQBAJ&pg=PA84|date=15 February 2013|publisher=Marshall Cavendish International Asia Pte Ltd|isbn=978-981-4435-49-9|pages=84–}}</ref>

The election results of 1969 saw the DAP winning 13 [[Parliament of Malaysia|parliamentary]] seats and 31 state assembly seats, securing 11.96% of the valid votes cast. Gerakan also made significant gains, campaigning on a similar platform in [[Penang]] in which the ''Bumiputeras'' had made up a minority of the state. That election represented the largest breakthrough for an opposition party in Malaysia prior to [[2008 Malaysian general election|2008]] and came close to unseating the ruling [[Alliance Party (Malaysia)|Alliance]]. However, political rallies that followed triggered racial violence particularly between the [[Malaysian Chinese|Chinese]] and the [[Malaysian Malays|Malays]], resulting in the [[13 May incident]] which saw hundreds of deaths. [[Parliament of Malaysia|Parliament]] was suspended, and the [[National Operations Council]] (NOC) was established to govern the country until 1971.<ref>Goh, pp. 19, 39.</ref> When Parliament reconvened, the ruling Alliance passed new laws such as the [[Sedition Act (Malaysia)|Sedition Act]], which criminalised any attempt to challenge constitutional provisions relating to ''Bumiputera'' privileges, including Article 153. The DAP and the PPP were the only parties that opposed the Act, which was passed by 125 votes to 17.<ref>Means, Gordon P. (1991). ''Malaysian Politics: The Second Generation'', pp. 14, 15. Oxford University Press. {{ISBN|0-19-588988-6}}.</ref> After the 1969 general election, the DAP would never come close to repeating its past successes for the next 38 years. Although the DAP remained a major opposition party, the ruling Alliance coalition and later its successor [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN) would clung solidly to its two-thirds parliamentary majority. The DAP, however, continued campaigning on its platform of abolishing the ''Bumiputera'' privileges, securing equal rights for all Malaysians and to establishing a [[democratic socialist]] state in Malaysia.<ref>{{cite book|author1=Lars Willnat|author2=Annette Aw|title=Political Communication in Asia|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=8aqTAgAAQBAJ&pg=PT197|date=23 March 2009|publisher=Routledge|isbn=978-1-135-89510-5|pages=197–}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|author=Ooi Keat Gin|title=Historical Dictionary of Malaysia|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=hEdADwAAQBAJ&pg=PA131|date=18 December 2017|publisher=Rowman & Littlefield Publishers|isbn=978-1-5381-0885-7|pages=131–}}</ref>

=== 1970–2007 ===
The party boycotted the National Consultative Council, a body formed in the aftermath of the 1969 racial riots to "establish positive and practical guidelines for inter-racial co-operation and social integration", in protest of the government's continued detention of its secretary-general, [[Lim Kit Siang]], under the Internal Security Act.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2016-09-23 |title=Revisiting the National Consultative Council |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/356653 |access-date=2023-11-08 |website=Malaysiakini}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |date=1970-01-12 |title=Tun hits out at DAP over boycott of NCC |pages=8 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/digitised/article/straitstimes19700112-1.2.64 |access-date=2023-11-08}}</ref>

In 1978, the party experienced a split when founding member and Penang state chairman [[Yeap Ghim Guan]] left and formed the [[Social Democratic Party (Malaysia)|Socialist Democratic Party]] after attempts by the national leadership to take over the party's state leadership. Yeap was joined by the entire state committee and member of parliament for [[Petaling (federal constituency)|Petaling]] Oh Keng Seng. They were later joined by party stalwart [[Fan Yew Teng]], who had served as acting secretary-general while Lim was detained under the Internal Security Act<ref>{{Cite web |title=Fan Yew Teng – ZeitZug.com - Literarische Online-Sammlung |url=https://www.zeitzug.com/index.php/component/content/article.html?id=973:fan-yew-teng&catid=110:timetrain |access-date=2023-11-06 |website=www.zeitzug.com}}</ref> and was the member of parliament for [[Menglembu (federal constituency)|Menglembu]]. Those who had left the party all labelled Lim's leadership as "dictatorial".<ref name=":1">{{Cite book |last=Kassim |first=Ismail |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=gwX121HjHJ4C&q=Fan+Yew+Teng+Social+Democratic+Party+SDP&pg=PA35 |title=The Politics of Accommodation: An Analysis of the 1978 Malaysian General Election |date=2018-06-11 |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian |language=en}}</ref>

The party suffered more defections in the lead-up to the [[1978 Malaysian general election]], when one of its most prominent Malay members, Daing Ibrahim Othman, left the party in protest of the leadership's decision to move him to another constituency, which was followed by a number of resignations in Negeri Sembilan for similar reasons.<ref name=":1" />

During the [[Mahathir Muhammad|Mahathir]] administration in 1987, several DAP leaders, including Parliamentary Opposition Leader [[Lim Kit Siang]], were detained by the government without trial during [[Operation Lalang]], under the accusation of being a national security threat. It is widely believed they were arrested for protesting the expansion of the [[Malaysian New Economic Policy|New Economic Policy]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.limkitsiang.com/archive/2005/sep05/lks3643.htm|title=Hisham – gonna say sorry for UMNO Youth keris episodes?|author=[[Lim Kit Siang]]|publisher=LimKitSiang.com|date=6 September 2005|access-date=11 November 2005|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20051218093046/http://www.limkitsiang.com/archive/2005/sep05/lks3643.htm|archive-date=18 December 2005|url-status=dead}}</ref>

In 1995, the party ran what has become widely known as the "Robocop" campaign to wrest Penang from the [[Barisan Nasional]], led by [[Teoh Teik Huat]] and Gooi Hock Seng. Despite the hype, the campaign was a failure as the party only won one state and three parliamentary seats. Government personalities and state media used the campaign to ridicule Lim for being a "robot" and "soulless".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.malaysiakini.com/news/11537|title=Ex-DAP leader assumes Keadilan post, urges opposition unity|author=Susan Loone|publisher=Malaysiakini|date=23 May 2002|access-date=15 February 2010}}</ref>

Following the ousting of Deputy Prime Minister [[Anwar Ibrahim]] in September 1998, DAP co-founded the [[Barisan Alternatif]] coalition along with [[Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party]] and the newly formed [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]]. However, the coalition did not work out very well for the DAP, with two of its top leaders, [[Lim Kit Siang]] and [[Karpal Singh]] losing their Parliamentary seats in the [[Malaysian general election, 1999|1999 election]]; the DAP managed to win only 5% (10 out of 193) of the seats in Parliament. PAS became the leading opposition party in Parliament. It left the coalition in 2001 due to a disagreement with PAS over the issue of an [[Islamic state]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.atimes.com/se-asia/AL10Ae02.html|archive-url=https://wayback.archive-it.org/all/20120211034419/http://atimes.com/se-asia/AL10Ae02.html|url-status=unfit|archive-date=11 February 2012|title=A wake-up call for ho-hum Malaysian politics|author=Anil Netto|publisher=Asia Times|date=10 December 1999|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.malaysia-today.net/MMblues/2005_06_06_MT_MMblues_archive.htm|title=The crossroads for PAS: whereto from here?|author=[[Raja Petra Kamaruddin]]|publisher=Malaysia Today|date=6 June 2005|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20060218114635/http://www.malaysia-today.net/MMblues/2005_06_06_MT_MMblues_archive.htm|archive-date=18 February 2006|url-status=dead}}</ref>{{unreliable source?|date=May 2018}}

In the [[Malaysian general election, 2004|2004 general election]], the DAP managed to capture 12 seats in Parliament, while PAS and Keadilan suffered major setbacks, with PAS losing 20 of the 27 seats it had held after the 1999 elections, and Keadilan lost all seats except one returned after a recount. The eventual outcome saw Lim Kit Siang, who had been elected in his constituency of Ipoh Timur with a majority of 10,000 votes, formally elected as the leader of the opposition in Parliament, a post he had lost to the president of PAS in 1999.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thestar.com.my/election2004/results/results.html|title=Malaysia Election 2004|work=The Star|date=5 April 2004|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20051112215851/http://thestar.com.my/election2004/results/results.html|archive-date=12 November 2005|url-status=dead}}</ref>

In the [[Sarawak state election, 2006|2006 Sarawak state election]], the Democratic Action Party won 6 of the 12 seats it contested and narrowly lost three other seats with small majorities.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://semak.spr.gov.my/spr/laporan/5_KedudukanAkhir.php|title=Keputusan Pilihan Raya Umum Parlimen/Dewan Undangan Negeri|publisher=[[Election Commission of Malaysia]]|access-date=18 May 2018}} Percentage figures based on total turnout (including votes for candidates not listed).</ref> Up til then it was the party's best showing ever in the history of [[Sarawak]]'s state elections since 1979.

=== 2008–2015 ===
[[Pakatan Rakyat]] was formed in 2008 by DAP, PKR and PAS. In the [[Malaysian general election, 2008|2008 general election]], the DAP won 13% (28 out of 222) of the seats in the [[Dewan Rakyat]], with PAS and PKR making substantial gains as well with 23 seats and 31 seats respectively. In total, the taking of 82 seats (37%) by the opposition to Barisan Nasional's 140 seats (63%), makes it the best performance in Malaysian history by the opposition, and denied Barisan Nasional the two-thirds majority required to make constitutional changes in the Dewan Rakyat.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thestar.com.my/election/results/results.html|title=MALAYSIA DECIDES 2008 > General Election 2008 Results|work=The Star|access-date=10 March 2008|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080409214419/http://thestar.com.my/election/results/results.html|archive-date=9 April 2008|url-status=dead}}</ref> DAP advisor [[Lim Kit Siang]] expressed surprise at the election results but declared it to be the true power of the voice of the Malaysian people for the leaders of the country to hear them.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thestar.com.my/election/story.asp?file=/2008/3/9/election2008/20080309161402&sec=election2008|title=DAP leaders also surprised|author1=M. Mageswari|author2=Loh Foon Fong|work=The Star|date=9 March 2008|access-date=9 March 2008|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090215120857/http://thestar.com.my/election/story.asp?file=%2F2008%2F3%2F9%2Felection2008%2F20080309161402&sec=election2008|archive-date=15 February 2009|url-status=dead}}</ref> In addition, DAP, having secured all its contested seats in the state of Penang, formed the Penang state government with its alliance partners PKR and PAS, the Chief Minister being DAP's [[Lim Guan Eng]], son of [[Lim Kit Siang]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.nst.com.my/Current_News/NST/Saturday/Frontpage/20080308210430/Article/index_html|title=Election 2008 Results: Lim Guan Eng is next Penang CM, promises fair & just administration|publisher=New Straits Times|access-date=12 March 2008|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080312230510/http://www.nst.com.my/Current_News/NST/Saturday/Frontpage/20080308210430/Article/index_html|archive-date=12 March 2008|url-status=dead}}</ref>

In the [[Sarawak state election, 2011|2011 Sarawak state election]], DAP furthered its gains from the previous election, winning 12 out of the 70 state assembly seats, with PR winning a total of 15 state seats and 41% of the popular vote. The PR's success was further enhanced in the [[Malaysian general election, 2013|2013 general election]] when DAP went on to win 17% (38 out of 222) of the seats in the Dewan Rakyat and the PR coalition won the popular vote, giving the BN government its worst election showing since independence. In 2015, the PR alliance broke up after a PAS Muktamar (General Assembly) motion unanimously approved the breaking of ties with DAP due to disagreements over PAS's decision to propose a [[private member's bill]] to implement "hudud" (Islamic penal code).<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.todayonline.com/world/asia/pas-dap-ties-no-more-syura-council-confirms-final-decision|title=PAS-DAP ties no more, Syura Council confirms in final decision|work=The Malay Mail|publisher=Today Online|date=11 July 2015|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180524080241/https://www.todayonline.com/world/asia/pas-dap-ties-no-more-syura-council-confirms-final-decision|archive-date=24 May 2018|url-status=dead}}</ref> Following PAS's decision to cut ties with DAP, DAP announced that PR had "ceased to exist".<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/news/asia/malaysia-s-opposition-pakatan-rakyat-alliance-ceases-to-exist-da-8277608|title=Malaysia's opposition Pakatan Rakyat alliance ceases to exist: DAP|author=Sumisha Naidu|publisher=Channel NewsAsia|date=16 June 2015|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180524151822/https://www.channelnewsasia.com/news/asia/malaysia-s-opposition-pakatan-rakyat-alliance-ceases-to-exist-da-8277608|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://themalaysianreserve.com/2017/03/31/dap-declares-pakatan-rakyat-ceases-to-exist/|title=DAP declares Pakatan Rakyat ceases to exist|publisher=The Malaysian Reserve|date=31 March 2017|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref>

At the DAP election in December 2012, Vincent Wu, who was initially declared to have secured the sixth spot with 1,202 votes, dropped to 26th place because he had actually secured only 669. [[Zairil Khir Johari]] was elected to the central executive committee (CEC) with 803 votes to secure the 20th spot. The glitch, reportedly because of a vote tabulation error due to the copy-and-paste method in [[Microsoft Excel]], had raised suspicion.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.nst.com.my/nation/general/dap-cec-member-zairil-not-a-malay-1.201030|title=DAP CEC member Zairil not a Malay|author=Looi Sue-chern|publisher=New Straits Times|date=14 January 2013|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130325040445/http://www.nst.com.my/nation/general/dap-cec-member-zairil-not-a-malay-1.201030|archive-date=25 March 2013|url-status=dead}}</ref> The DAP admitted to the counting error after discovering the mistake. The DAP election fiasco had caused unease among party members and led to protests to the Registrar of Societies (RoS). Two dissatisfied life members of the DAP then lodged reports with the RoS on the party elections following the revelations.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2013/01/14/two-dap-members-lodge-reports-with-ros-alleging-fraud-in-party-elections/|title=Two DAP members lodge reports with ROS, alleging fraud in party elections|work=Bernama|publisher=The Star|date=14 January 2013|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref> Following the report the RoS had informed DAP of the dispute by its members and in turn as provided for under Section 3A of the Societies Act 1966 did recognise the office-bearers of the committee formed in the party elections on 15 December 2012, the point of contention.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2013/04/18/dap-to-contest-under-pkr-pas-banners-if-ros-does-not-revoke-letter-derecognising-partys-cec/|title=DAP to contest under PKR, PAS banners if RoS does not revoke letter derecognising party's CEC|author=Regina Lee|work=The Star|date=18 April 2013|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref>

DAP chairperson [[Karpal Singh]] said DAP will contest under the PAS logo for the Peninsula and PKR logo in Sabah and Sarawak in the 13th general election, following the Registrar of Societies' (RoS) failure to respond on the withdrawal letter of RoS informing that it does not recognise the party's top leadership line-up. DAP had appealed to the RoS to withdraw its letter to suspend the party's existing central executive committee (CEC) but the department was silent on the matter.<ref name="rocket">{{cite web|url=http://www.malaysiakini.com/news/227304|title=Use rocket first during nomination, says Guan Eng|author1=Hafiz Yatim|author2=Susan Loone|publisher=Malaysiakini|date=19 April 2013|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref>

On 19 April 2013, DAP secretary-general Lim Guan Eng informed all its 51 parliament and 103 state candidates to use the rocket symbol first during nomination tomorrow, and show the Election Commission the letter of authorisation signed by secretary-general Lim Guan Eng. If the rocket symbol is rejected, then use the letter of authorisation signed by PAS secretary-general Mustafa Ali for Peninsula Malaysia and PKR letter of authorisation for Sabah and Sarawak. This came after the DAP decided to use PAS and PKR symbols for the coming general election on 5 May.<ref name="rocket"/> He said the DAP headquarters in [[Kuala Lumpur]] received a letter by hand from the RoS at 10 p.m. on 19 April, stating that it had no objections to the DAP using the logo, and that the Election Commission (EC) had informed all returning officers to accept nominations from the DAP.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.themalaysianinsider.com/malaysia/article/dap-gets-to-ride-the-rocket-for-ge13/|title=DAP gets to ride the rocket for GE13|author1=Opalyn Mok|author2=Emily Ding|publisher=The Malaysian Insider|date=20 April 2013|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130423092413/http://www.themalaysianinsider.com/malaysia/article/dap-gets-to-ride-the-rocket-for-ge13|archive-date=23 April 2013|url-status=dead}}</ref>

On 29 September 2013, DAP held a [[2013 Democratic Action Party leadership election|special congress]] to vote for a new Central Executive Committee.<ref>{{Cite web|date=2013-09-29|title=Keputusan Pemilihan Semula CEC Kongres Khas DAP 2013|url=https://www.roketkini.com/2013/09/29/keputusan-pemilihan-semula-cec-kongres-khas-dap-2013/|access-date=2021-12-08|website=roketkini.com|language=en-US}}</ref>

=== 2015–2019 ===
On 22 September 2015, the [[Pakatan Harapan]] coalition was formed by DAP, PKR and the [[National Trust Party (Malaysia)|National Trust Party]] to succeed Pakatan Rakyat. In the [[Sarawak state election, 2016|2016 Sarawak state election]], the party lost its gains from the previous election, retaining only 7 of the 12 seats it had won in 2011, while Pakatan Harapan as whole won 10 state seats and 30.16% of the popular vote.

On 12 February 2017, [[Kota Melaka]] MP, [[Sim Tong Him]], along with three other DAP state assemblymen from [[Melaka]], Goh (Duyong), [[Lim Jack Wong]] (Bachang), and [[Chin Choong Seong]] (Kesidang), announced their resignation from the party, citing a lack of trust in the party's leadership.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.themalaymailonline.com/malaysia/article/kota-melaka-mp-three-state-reps-leave-dap|title=Kota Melaka MP, three state reps leave DAP|publisher=The Malay Mail|date=12 February 2017|access-date=15 February 2017}}</ref>

The party participated in the [[Malaysian general election, 2018|2018 general election]] as a part of Pakatan Harapan, now a four-member coalition with the addition of [[Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia]] (BERSATU). During the election, the coalition won 113 seats and was able to form the federal government after securing an alliance with the [[Sabah Heritage Party]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/nation/2018/04/352101/warisan-will-work-only-pakatan-harapan-ge14|title=Warisan will work only with Pakatan Harapan for GE14|author=Suzianah Jiffar|publisher=New Straits Times|date=2 April 2018|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/warisan-and-pakatan-harapan-team-up-in-sabah-for-malaysia-elections|title=Warisan and Pakatan Harapan team up in Sabah for Malaysia elections|publisher=The Straits Times|date=2 April 2018|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref> The DAP won 42 seats of the 47 seats it contested, making it the second-largest party from Pakatan Harapan in the parliament's lower house behind PKR, which had won 47 seats.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://election.thestar.com.my/|title=14th General Election [Results Overview]|work=The Star|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180524082545/https://election.thestar.com.my/|archive-date=24 May 2018|url-status=dead}}</ref> The party was given six ministerial portfolios and secured seven deputy minister positions in the [[Seventh Mahathir cabinet]], with then-secretary-general of the party Lim Guan Eng being appointed Minister of Finance, becoming the first person of Malaysian Chinese descent to hold the position in 44 years since [[Tan Siew Sin]] from the [[Malaysian Chinese Association]], who served in that capacity from 1959 until 1974.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://says.com/my/news/lim-guan-eng-is-the-new-finance-minister|title=Lim Guan Eng Is Our New Finance Minister|author=Sadho Ram|publisher=Says.com|date=12 May 2018|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.worldofbuzz.com/lim-guan-eng-im-sorry-i-dont-consider-myself-chinese-im-malaysian/|title= Malaysia Lim Guan Eng: "I'm sorry, I don't consider myself Chinese. I'm Malaysian"|publisher=World of Buzz|date=12 May 2018|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref> Other ministerial appointments included [[Loke Siew Fook]] as Minister of Transport, who eventually replaced [[Lim Kit Siang]] as DAP parliamentary leader on 11 July 2018,<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/politics/2018/07/389766/anthony-loke-new-dap-parliamentary-leader|title=Anthony Loke is new DAP parliamentary leader|date=11 July 2018}}</ref> [[Kulasegaran Murugeson]] as Minister of Human Resources, [[Yeo Bee Yin]] as Minister of Energy, [[Teresa Kok]] as Minister of Primary Industries, and [[Gobind Singh Deo]] as [[Minister of Communications (Malaysia)|Minister of Communications and Multimedia]].

===2020–2022 Malaysian political crisis===
{{main|2020–2022 Malaysian political crisis}}

On 24 February 2020, the DAP became the largest party in the Dewan Rakyat for the first time after 11 of the People's Justice Party's 50 MPs resigned during the political crisis. UMNO had also lost 16 of its 54 MPs over several months, mostly through defections to Bersatu. Though the political crisis resulted in the collapse of the Pakatan Harapan government, it retained its 42 MPs. However, over the course of the crisis, a number of state assemblymen defected to opposing coalitions. On 9 March 2020, Paul Yong and A Sivasubramaniam left the party to join the new [[Perikatan Nasional]] state government in Perak.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.hmetro.com.my/mutakhir/2020/03/552678/tiga-adun-perak-isytihar-keluar-parti|title=Tiga ADUN Perak isytihar keluar parti|first=Balqis Jazimah|last=Zahari|date=9 March 2020|website=Harian Metro}}</ref> The next day on 10 March, DAP expelled Norhizam Hassan Baktee, assemblyman for Pengkalan Batu, over his support for the new Perikatan Nasional government in Melaka.<ref>{{cite web| url = http://www.astroawani.com/berita-politik/dap-pecat-norhizam-hassan-baktee-233202| url-status = dead| archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20200311001828/http://www.astroawani.com/berita-politik/dap-pecat-norhizam-hassan-baktee-233202| archive-date = 2020-03-11| title = DAP pecat Norhizam Hassan Baktee {{!}} Astro Awani}}</ref> The party also expelled Sabah assemblyman Ronnie Loh for his support of Perikatan's attempt to replace Sabah state government led by [[Shafie Apdal]] and his Warisan Plus coalition.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2020/07/30/dap-sacks-appointed-sabah-rep-who-backed-musas-takeover-bid/1889521|title=DAP sacks appointed Sabah rep who backed Musa's takeover bid &#124; Malay Mail|first=Sulok|last=Tawie|website=www.malaymail.com|date=30 July 2020 }}</ref>

Separately, the party's Sarawak vice-chairman and Padungan assemblyman Wong King Wei resigned on 27 July 2020, claiming the party had deviated from its objectives and citing his disillusionment with party leadership. He continued to hold his seat as an independent until his term ended in 2021.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2020/07/27/sarawak-dap-vice-chief-quits-party-cites-disillusionment-with-leadership/1888376|title=Sarawak DAP vice-chief quits party, cites disillusionment with leadership &#124; Malay Mail|first=Sulok|last=Tawie|website=www.malaymail.com|date=27 July 2020 }}</ref>

== Ethnic diversity ==
DAP was founded by [[Chen Man Hin]] and [[Devan Nair]], who were ethnic Chinese and Indian respectively. The majority of DAP's party membership is of ethnic Chinese and Indian heritage, with most elected positions within the party being held by Chinese members. While the party has fielded [[Malays (ethnic group)|Malay]] candidates in every general election since 1969, only five had ever been elected by 2008.<ref>{{Cite web |last= |date=2011-08-03 |title=UMNO's Muhyiddin possesses neither class nor standard! |url=https://www.therocket.com.my/en/umno%e2%80%99s-muhyiddin-possesses-neither-class-nor-standard/ |access-date=2023-11-06 |website=The Rocket |language=en-US}}</ref> Of the five, two had been elected in 1969 as state legislative assemblymen, while the party's first Malay member of parliament was only elected in 1990.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.dapmalaysia.org/all-archive/English/2003/mar03/bul/bul1981.htm|title=Ahmad Nor – a far-sighted and visionary leader|access-date=15 September 2019}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |last= |date=2018-04-24 |title=DAP cadang 10 calon Melayu, paling ramai sejak 1966 |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/bahasa/2018/04/24/dap-cadang-10-calon-melayu-paling-ramai-sejak-1966/ |access-date=2023-11-06 |website=Free Malaysia Today |language=en-US}}</ref> The DAP also only gained its first native Sabahan ([[Kadazandusun]]) legislator in 2013, [[Edwin Jack Bosi]], who sat in [[Sabah State Legislative Assembly]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2017/08/31/rep-quits-dealing-dap-a-blow-kadazandusun-bosis-resignation-halts-chances-to-make-inroads-in-sabah|title=Kadazandusun Bosi's resignation halts chances to make inroads in Sabah|publisher=TheStar|access-date=15 September 2019}}</ref> The lack of Malay members within the party has led to DAP being viewed as a "racist" or "anti-Malay" party by political opponents in that it is exclusively concerned with the issues of the Chinese or Indian communities.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2016/01/23/why-dap-still-isnt-very-popular-with-malays-analysts-explain/1046433|title=Why DAP still isn't very popular with Malays: Analysts explain|date=23 January 2016 |publisher=MalayMail|access-date=15 September 2019}}</ref>

=== Allegations of racism and chauvinism ===
Despite constant rebuttals by party leaders, DAP has been depicted by their political opponents, especially from UMNO, as a party that favours the [[Malaysian Chinese]] minority above others.<ref>{{Cite news |date=1990-08-20 |title=嘉化： 行动党招揽华团人士捞取选票制造分裂 |work=Nanyang Siang Po}}</ref> This allegation of racial chauvinism culminated{{when|date=August 2024}} in a two-piece television program broadcast on government-controlled TV channel ''[[Radio Televisyen Malaysia]]'' (RTM) entitled "Bahaya Cauvinisme", which translates to "Dangers of Chauvinism". The program forced then party leader Lim Kit Siang to issue a formal media statement to counter the allegations.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.dapmalaysia.org/all-archive/English/2002/nov02/lks/lks1931.htm|title=Media statement by Lim Kit Siang|author=Lim Kit Siang|publisher=Democratic Action Party|year=2002|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref>

On 15 November 2011, [[Ismail Sabri Yaakob]], the Malaysian Minister for Domestic Trade, Co-operatives and Consumerism, accused DAP's publicity chief, [[Tony Pua]] of racism for making repeated attacks against the [[Kedai Rakyat 1Malaysia]], a government initiative to supply cheap retail products to Malaysian consumers. Tony Pua was criticised for singling out Kedai Rakyat 1Malaysia, whose suppliers to the store generally come from the Malaysian Bumiputra community, and for not investigating the quality of products supplied by Malaysian-Chinese suppliers or making similar accusations against independent Malaysian-Chinese stores.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.themalaysianinsider.com/malaysia/article/dap-ismail-sabris-racism-claims-over-kedai-rakyat-exposes-baseless/|title=DAP: Ismail Sabri's racism claims over Kedai Rakyat exposes baseless|author=Shannon Teoh|publisher=The Malaysian Insider|date=17 November 2011|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20111119173926/http://www.themalaysianinsider.com/malaysia/article/dap-ismail-sabris-racism-claims-over-kedai-rakyat-exposes-baseless|archive-date=19 November 2011|url-status=dead}}</ref>

Allegations of racism have forced DAP party leader Lim Guan Eng to issue a formal denial in the Penang High Court.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.thesundaily.my/news/164896|title=Guan Eng testifies he is not a racist|author=Aaron Ngui|work=The Sun|date=4 October 2011|access-date=24 May 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20111106043309/http://www.thesundaily.my/news/164896|archive-date=6 November 2011|url-status=dead}}</ref>

== Party symbols ==
=== Party logo ===
The symbol or logo of the DAP (see above) is the rocket, which it has used since the 1969 general election. Its components are symbolised as follows:

* The red rocket symbolises the Party's aspiration for a modern, dynamic and progressive society
* The four rocket boosters represent the support and drive given to the Party objectives by the three major ethnicities (Malay, Chinese, Indian) and others
* The blue circle stands for the unity of the multi-racial people of Malaysia
* The white background stands for purity and incorruptibility

=== Ubah mascot ===
In 2008, DAP initially introduced "Rocket Kid", a rocket as the party's official mascot during the [[Malaysian general election, 2008|12th Malaysian general election]]. This was then changed to Ubah bird, a [[hornbill]] which was designed by Ooi Leng Hang and was launched during the [[Sarawak state election, 2011|Sarawak state election]] in 2011 and also used as part of their political campaigning during the [[Malaysian general election, 2013|13th Malaysian general election]] in 2013. DAP had adopted this bird as a symbol for change both for its unique characteristics, hardiness and representation of the unity of both East Malaysia and West Malaysia into a Malaysian nation.<ref name="symbol">{{cite web|url=https://limguaneng.com/index.php/2013/07/11/official-launching-of-ubah-inflatable-bird-water-ubah-at-10am-13-july-at-ijm-promenade/#more-3316|title=Official Launching of UBAH inflatable bird (Water Ubah) at 10am, 13 July, at IJM Promenade.|author=[[Lim Guan Eng]]|publisher=LimGuanEng.com|date=11 July 2013|access-date=24 May 2018}}</ref> Its merchandise such as plush toys, buttons and car stickers were very well received by the public.<ref>{{cite tweet|title=Merchandise incl Ubah plush toys selling like hot cakes!|user=dapmalaysia|number=286078600292212737|date=1 January 2013}}</ref> The idea of the mascot came from [[Sarawak]] DAP Secretary and future state party chairman, [[Chong Chieng Jen]], who felt a mascot would boost the spirit of the people. The name "Ubah", which means "change" in [[Malay language|Malay]], is in line with the party's aspirations in changing the ruling party of the Malaysian federal government. In addition to its original Sarawak [[Iban people|Iban]] costume, "Ubah" now comes in a [[Malay people|Malay]] costume for [[Eid al-Fitr|Hari Raya]], [[Indian people|Indian]] costume for [[Diwali|Deepavali]], [[Chinese people|Chinese]] costume for [[Chinese New Year]], [[Santa Claus]] costume for [[Christmas]], and a [[Superman]] costume that depicts the power of the people.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mmail.com.my/story/political-mascots-come-play-52799|title=Political mascots come into play|author=R. The Central Executive Committee (CEC) serves as the party's executive body and its 30 members are elected by party delegates during a national congress held every three years.
