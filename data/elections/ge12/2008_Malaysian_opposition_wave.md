# 2008 Malaysian opposition wave

*Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Malaysian_opposition_wave*

---

{{Short description|Political phenomenon in Malaysia}}
The '''2008 Malaysian Opposition Wave''' refers to a significant political shift that occurred during the [[2008 Malaysian general election]], held on 8 March 2008. This election, often described as a "[[Political tsunami (Malaysian politics)|political tsunami]]", marked a turning point in Malaysia’s political landscape. Both the ruling coalition, [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN), and the opposition coalition, [[Barisan Alternatif]], which mainly comprised the [[Democratic Action Party]] (DAP), the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]] (PKR), and the [[Malaysian Islamic Party]] (PAS), experienced unexpected outcomes. Dissatisfaction with governance, rising crime rates, consumer-price inflation, and corruption highlighted during the election campaign contributed to a decline in BN's support, particularly in urban and minority communities. Adding to the mounting pressure on Prime Minister [[Abdullah Ahmad Badawi]] was public criticism from his predecessor, [[Mahathir Mohamad]], who openly disapproved of Abdullah’s leadership and policy direction, further influencing public perception and party confidence. [[Anwar Ibrahim]] was instrumental in uniting the opposition and coordinating candidate placements to avoid multi-cornered contests, helping the opposition consolidate their votes. Their manifesto focused on reducing BN's two-thirds majority in [[Parliament of Malaysia|Parliament]] and winning control of key northern states such as [[Perlis]], [[Kedah]], [[Penang]], and [[Perak]] at the [[State legislative assemblies of Malaysia|State Legislative Assembly]] (DUN) level, while retaining [[Kelantan]] under PAS’s control.<ref>{{Cite journal |last=Moten |first=Abdul Rashid |date=2009-04-01 |title=2008 General Elections in Malaysia: Democracy at Work |url=https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/japanese-journal-of-political-science/article/abs/2008-general-elections-in-malaysia-democracy-at-work/03EC9FC548FCFA286D7E62CC32B69052 |journal=Japanese Journal of Political Science |language=en |volume=10 |issue=1 |pages=21–42 |doi=10.1017/S1468109908003356 |issn=1474-0060|url-access=subscription }}</ref><ref name="Opposition strategy">{{cite web |author=Whitley, Angus |date=14 February 2008 |title=Anwar, Malaysian Opposition, Aim to Erode Majority (Update1) |url=https://www.bloomberg.com/apps/news?pid=20601080&sid=aCv93ZkPPcRs&refer=asia |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121105055543/http://www.bloomberg.com/apps/news?pid=newsarchive&sid=aCv93ZkPPcRs&refer=asia |archive-date=5 November 2012 |access-date=15 February 2008 |publisher=[[Bloomberg L.P.]]}}</ref><ref name="Reuters Kelantan lost">{{cite news |date=8 March 2008 |title=M'sia PM loses Kelantan state: Islamist party |url=http://www.straitstimes.com/Latest+News/Asia/STIStory_214864.html |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080311200711/http://www.straitstimes.com/Latest%2BNews/Asia/STIStory_214864.html |archive-date=11 March 2008 |access-date=12 March 2008 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |agency=[[Reuters]]}}</ref>

Beyond their initial targets, the opposition achieved significant success, gaining control of five states: Kelantan (retained by PAS), Kedah, Penang, Perak, and [[Selangor]].<ref name="AFP Selangor lost">{{cite news |date=9 March 2008 |title=Malaysian opposition claims Selangor state |url=http://malaysia.news.yahoo.com/afp/20080308/tap-malaysia-vote-selangor-0193655.html |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080312171830/http://malaysia.news.yahoo.com/afp/20080308/tap-malaysia-vote-selangor-0193655.html |archive-date=12 March 2008 |access-date=12 March 2008 |publisher=[[Yahoo! News]] |agency=[[Agence France-Presse]]}}</ref> Additionally, they dominated the [[Kuala Lumpur|Federal Territory of Kuala Lumpur]], winning 10 out of 11 parliamentary seats. The opposition also made inroads in traditionally strong Barisan Nasional territories, securing several parliamentary seats in states such as [[Negeri Sembilan]], [[Malacca]], [[Johor]], and [[Pahang]]. Losing five states and being left with only 140 parliamentary seats, put intense pressure on Prime Minister Abdullah Badawi, who was later asked to resign.<ref name="CNA bad BN results">{{cite news |date=9 March 2008 |title=Malaysia's BN coalition suffers worst electoral defeat |url=http://www.channelnewsasia.com/stories/southeastasia/view/333807/1/.html |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080310234537/http://www.channelnewsasia.com/stories/southeastasia/view/333807/1/.html |archive-date=10 March 2008 |access-date=11 March 2008 |publisher=Channel NewsAsia}}</ref> This electoral shift reduced BN's dominance and altered Malaysia's political landscape, providing the opposition with a stronger platform to promote their reform agenda.<ref name=":1">{{Cite book |last=Welsh |first=Bridget |title=The End of UMNO? Essays on Malaysia’s Dominant Party |publisher=Strategic Information and Research Development Centre |year=2016 |isbn=978-9670960210}}</ref>

== Pre-election events ==

=== Kris wielding incident ===
In 2005, [[Hishammuddin Hussein]]'s act of wielding a [[kris]] during the UMNO Youth General Assembly was perceived as controversial and reportedly offended segments of the [[Malaysian Chinese|Chinese community]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=A decade after Hisham waved the keris... |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/305590 |access-date=24 November 2024 |website=Malaysiakini}}</ref> This incident received widespread coverage in Chinese-language newspapers and criticism from opposition leaders. Despite the backlash, Hishammuddin repeated the act at UMNO General Assemblies in 2006 and 2007.

=== Hindraf protests ===
On 25 November 2007, [[2007 HINDRAF rally|protests]] organized by the [[HINDRAF|Hindu Rights Action Force]] (HINDRAF) erupted in response to grievances against the UMNO-led government.<ref>[https://openresearch-repository.anu.edu.au/items/adecfe4e-fb0b-4d35-a9de-12bb2f128a23 From independence to Hindraf: the Malaysian Indian community and the negotiation for minority rights]. PhD Thesis. [[Australian National University]]. 2011. Arunajeet Kaur.</ref> This movement marked a significant shift in support among the [[Malaysian Indians|Indian community]], who overwhelmingly voted for opposition parties in the election. One notable outcome was the defeat of [[Malaysian Indian Congress]] (MIC) leader [[Samy Vellu]], who lost his parliamentary seat to [[Michael Jeyakumar Devaraj]] of the [[Socialist Party of Malaysia]] (PSM), contesting under the [[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|People's Justice Party]] (PKR) banner during the [[2008 Malaysian general election|2008 general election]], along with the loss of several other MIC-held seats to the opposition.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2008-03-08 |title=Malaysian Indian leader Samy Vellu loses |url=https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/malaysian-indian-leader-samy-vellu-loses/articleshow/2848330.cms |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241127164008/https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/malaysian-indian-leader-samy-vellu-loses/articleshow/2848330.cms |archive-date=2024-11-27 |access-date=2024-11-27 |work=The Times of India |issn=0971-8257}}</ref>

=== Port Klang Free Zone (PKFZ) scandal ===
The [[Port Klang Free Zone]] (PKFZ) scandal, involving RM4.6 billion, exposed alleged corruption within the Barisan Nasional power-sharing framework.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web |date=15 April 2008 |title=Analysis of the 2008 General Election of Malaysia |url=https://www.malaysia-today.net/2008/04/15/analysis-of-the-2008-general-election-of-malaysia/ |access-date=24 November 2024 |website=Malaysia Today}}</ref> ''Kuala Dimensi Sdn. Bhd.'' purchased the land from ''Koperasi Kemajuan Pulau Lumut'' for RM95 million in 1999 and later sold it to the [[Port Klang Authority]] for RM1.1 billion in 2003. Although the government could have acquired the land for RM10 per square foot under the [[National Land Code (Malaysia)|National Land Code]], it opted to purchase it for RM23 per square foot. ''Kuala Dimensi Sdn. Bhd.'' was subsequently appointed as the project contractor, with costs escalating to RM3.3 billion. Politicians from [[UMNO]], [[Sarawak Progressive Democratic Party|SPDP]], and a former [[Malaysian Chinese Association|MCA]] parliamentary secretary were implicated in various stages of the project.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Fernandez |first=Terence |date=2017-03-21 |title=PKFZ: A scandalous end |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/opinion/2017/03/21/pkfz-a-scandalous-end/1339375 |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201101081858/https://www.malaymail.com/news/opinion/2017/03/21/pkfz-a-scandalous-end/1339375 |archive-date=2020-11-01 |access-date=2024-11-27 |website=www.malaymail.com}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=2009-12-27 |title=Story Of The Year: PKFZ: The headline-grabbing scandal |url=https://theedgemalaysia.com/article/story-year-pkfz-headline-grabbing-scandal?utm_source=chatgpt.com |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241127170847/https://theedgemalaysia.com/article/story-year-pkfz-headline-grabbing-scandal |archive-date=2024-11-27 |access-date=2024-11-27 |website=The Edge Malaysia}}</ref>

== Opposition strategies ==

=== PAS strategy ===
PAS, traditionally supported by the [[Malays (ethnic group)|Malay]] community, demonstrated adaptability by fielding prominent leaders to safeguard its stronghold in Kelantan. Leaders such as [[Mahfuz Omar]] and [[Salahuddin Ayub]] were strategically placed in [[Kedah]] and [[Kelantan]] respectively, while [[Nasharudin Mat Isa]] contested in [[Bachok District|Bachok]] against the Barisan Nasional candidate, Awang Adek.<ref name=":0" /> Independent candidate [[Ibrahim Ali (politician)|Ibrahim Ali]], previously dismissed by UMNO, was backed by PAS in [[Pasir Mas (federal constituency)|Pasir Mas]]. However, PAS miscalculated by fielding [[Mohamad Sabu]] in [[Kuala Terengganu]], where he faced a strong local opponent.

=== DAP strategy ===
DAP capitalized on dissatisfaction among Chinese and Indian communities with certain UMNO leaders accused of making racially charged statements.<ref name=":0" /> Their campaign slogan, "A vote for [[Malaysian People's Movement Party|Gerakan]] is a vote for UMNO," resonated with voters in Chinese-majority constituencies. DAP also strategically fielded candidates in areas with minimal Malay-majority voters.

=== PKR strategy ===
[[People's Justice Party (Malaysia)|PKR]] adopted slogans such as "People's Supremacy" ("Ketuanan Rakyat") to attract urban and moderate Malay voters. As a multiethnic party with Chinese and Indian members, PKR successfully appealed to non-Malay voters. The party also highlighted issues of corruption within Barisan Nasional, including the [[Royal Commission of Inquiry into the Lingam Video Clip|VK Lingam case]] and the PKFZ scandal, as key campaign points.<ref name=":0" />
