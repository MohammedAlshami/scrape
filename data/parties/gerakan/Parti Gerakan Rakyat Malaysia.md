The '''Parti Gerakan Rakyat Malaysia''' ({{Langx|en|'''Malaysian People's Movement Party'''}}; [[Abbreviation|abbrev]]: '''GERAKAN''' or less commonly as '''PGRM''') is a [[Liberalism|liberal]] political party in [[Malaysia]]. Formed in 1968, Gerakan gained prominence in the [[1969 Malaysian general election|1969 general election]] when it defeated the ruling [[Alliance Party (Malaysia)|Alliance Party]] in [[Penang]] and won the majority of seats in [[Penang State Legislative Assembly|Penang's state legislature]]. However, in 1972, Gerakan joined the Alliance Party, which later evolved into [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN) and contested the [[1974 Malaysian general election|1974 general election]] and subsequent elections as part of the ruling coalition. The party maintained representation in the [[Dewan Rakyat]] until the [[2018 Malaysian general election|2018 general election]], when it lost all its seats amid BN's historic defeat. Gerakan subsequently left BN and has been a member of [[Perikatan Nasional]] (PN) since 2021.

Gerakan also held a dominant position in the [[Penang State Legislative Assembly]] from 1969 to 2008 and also achieved some success nationally as part of the ruling BN coalition, particularly in the [[2004 Malaysian general election|2004 general election]]. However, its fortunes declined sharply in the [[2008 Malaysian general election|2008 election]], and left the coalition following BN's defeat in the 2018 general election.<ref name="Gerakan leaves Barisan Nasional">{{cite web |date=23 June 2018 |title=Gerakan leaves Barisan Nasional |url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/politics/2018/06/383177/gerakan-leaves-barisan-nasional |access-date=23 June 2018 |publisher=New Straits Times}}</ref> While it joined PN in 2021,<ref>{{Cite news |author=Minderjeet Kaur |date=February 11, 2021 |title=Gerakan joins PN |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2021/02/11/gerakan-joins-pn/ |access-date=February 12, 2021 |publisher=[[Free Malaysia Today]] |language=en-GB}}</ref> it had no representation at the national or state-level until the [[2023 Kedah state election]], where the party achieved its first electoral victory since [[2014 Telok Intan by-election|2014]]. Despite clashes with its coalition allies, the party is perceived to be dependent on continued membership within PN.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Gerakan in tough position within Perikatan over brewery donation issue |date=29 July 2024 |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2024/07/29/gerakan-in-tough-position-within-perikatan-over-brewery-donation-issue}}</ref>

The party is assisted by an affiliated think tank called SEDAR Institute (Socio-Economic Development And Research Institute). The party is a member of the [[Council of Asian Liberals and Democrats]]. At its height, the party's primary source of support came from the country's [[Malaysian Chinese|ethnic Chinese]] who constitute 80% of the party's membership, with 15% being [[Indian Malaysian|ethnic Indian]] and the remaining spread between the [[Malaysian Malays|Malays]] and other groups.<ref name="merger">{{cite news |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070311092203/http://malaysia-today.net/blog2006/newsncom.php?itemid=850 |archive-date=11 March 2007 |author= Suthakar, K. |date=20 November 2006 |url=http://malaysia-today.net/blog2006/newsncom.php?itemid=850 |title=PPP offers Gerakan merger |work=The Star }}</ref>

== History ==
=== Formation ===
In 1959, the leader of [[Malaysian Chinese Association]] (MCA) [[Lim Chong Eu]] resigned his position as president after conflict with UMNO leader [[Tunku Abdul Rahman]] over the allocation of seats in the [[Malayan general election, 1959|1959 general election]]. Dr Lim requested that MCA be given 40 seats to contest (MCA was allocated 30 seats). He left MCA, and later set up the [[United Democratic Party (Malaysia)|United Democratic Party]] in 1962.  In 1968, UDP was dissolved and its former members, together with the Labour Party and others, joined force to form a new party Gerakan.<ref name="a to z">{{cite book |author=Ooi |first=Keat Gin |author-link=Keat Gin Ooi |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=YVViEJmN99kC&pg=PA106 |title=The A to Z of Malaysia |publisher=Scarecrow Press |year=2010 |isbn=978-0-8108-7641-5 |page=106}}</ref> The six founders of the party were Professor [[Syed Hussain Alatas]], Dr. [[Tan Chee Khoon]], Dr. J.B.A. Peter, Lim Chong Eu, Professor [[Wang Gungwu]], and [[Veerappen Veerathan|V. Veerapan]].<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.gerakan.org.my/about-us/history/the-early-years |title=The Early Years, 1968–1972 |work=Parti Gerakan Rakyak Malaysia }}</ref> Although the majority of its supporters were Chinese, the party positioned itself as a Malaysian, non-communal party that received some support from the Indians and Malays.  Its [[pro tem]] president was Professor [[Syed Hussein Alatas]], and later Lim took over the presidency which lasted until 1980.<ref name="lim chong eu">{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=v9QEBAAAQBAJ&pg=PA603 |title=Southeast Asian Personalities of Chinese Descent: A Biographical Dictionary |editor= Leo Suryadinata |author=Cheah Kooi Guan |pages=603–605 |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |year= 2012 |isbn=978-981-4345-21-7 }}</ref>

=== 1969–2007 ===
The party won the state of Penang in the [[Malaysian general election, 1969|1969 general election]] by taking 16 out of 24 seats, winning control from the MCA. Lim Chong Eu became Penang's Chief Minister.  Gerakan and the [[Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party]], which won in Kelantan, were the only parties not then affiliated with the Alliance Party to form a state government in Malaysia.  However, due to internal disputes within the party, for example over plan for closer ties with Alliance, the party was split. Many of its members, such as Syed Hussein Alatas, [[V. David]], Veerappen, and [[Tan Chee Khoon]], left to form [[Parti Keadilan Masyarakat Malaysia]] (PEKEMAS).<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=TeExjdWUmJYC&pg=PA259 |title=Indian Communities in Southeast Asia |editor= K S Sandhu, A Mani |page=259 |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |year= 1993 |isbn=978-981-210-017-7 }}</ref> The PEKEMAS party however proved to be short-lived, and Gerakan itself became reduced and largely Chinese as many non-Chinese left the party. In 1972, Gerakan joined the Alliance which became [[Barisan Nasional]] in 1973.<ref>{{cite book |url= https://books.google.com/books?id=BkUORUDN4ysC&pg=PR66 |title=The A to Z of Malaysia|author= Keat Gin Ooi |publisher=Scarecrow Press |pages=ixvi-ixvii |year= 2010 |isbn= 978-0-8108-7641-5 }}</ref>

In 1973, a number of former MCA members from [[Perak]] and [[Selangor]] who were expelled from the party, including [[Lim Keng Yaik]] and Paul Leong Khee Seong, joined Gerakan.<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=hPi3BAAAQBAJ&pg=PA99 |title=he Influence of the Type of Dominant Party on Democracy: A Comparison Between South Africa and Malaysia |author= Malte Kaßner |page=99|publisher=Springer VS |year= 2014 |isbn=978-3-658-04437-4  }}</ref><ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=v9QEBAAAQBAJ&pg=PA634 |title=Southeast Asian Personalities of Chinese Descent: A Biographical Dictionary |editor= Leo Suryadinata |author=Cheah Kooi Guan |page=634 |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |year= 2012 |isbn=978-981-4345-21-7 }}</ref> In 1980, Lim Chong Eu decided to retire from the post of party president, and Lim Keng Yaik won the presidency over Lim Chong Eu's preferred candidate Paul Leong.<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=EV88Tf0yWqoC&pg=PA12 |title=Malaysia's 1982 General Election|author= Harold A. Crouch |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |page=12|year=1982 |isbn=  978-9971-902-45-2 }}</ref> Lim Keng Yaik stayed as president of Gerakan until he decided to retire in 2007.

Gerakan had continual conflicts with MCA as it challenged MCA's position as the main Chinese party within BN. In the [[Malaysian general election, 1978|1978 general election]], disputes broke out between Gerakan and MCA as seven ex-MCA members (with support from MCA) stood against Gerakan.<ref>{{cite book |url= https://books.google.com/books?id=gwX121HjHJ4C&pg=PA58 |title=The Politics of Accommodation: An Analysis of the 1978 Malaysian General Election|author= Ismail Kassim |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |year=1978 |pages=58–59 }}</ref> The party lost some seats but managed to retain its stronghold Penang despite losing its position as the biggest party in Penang.  Gerakan also sought to expand its influence in others states, especially in Perak, an effort helped by continued defections from MCA. In 1979, Michael Chen stood against [[Lee San Choon]] for the MCA Presidency but lost, then later in 1981 joined Gerakan.<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=EV88Tf0yWqoC&pg=PA9 |title=Malaysia's 1982 General Election |author=Harold A. Crouch |pages=9–11|publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |year= 1982|isbn=978-9971-902-45-2 }}</ref> He was followed by 120,000 dissidents MCA members, thereby allowing Gerakan to create of new branches and become a nationwide organisation. (Chen however later rejoined MCA after a failed challenge to replace Lim Keng Yaik as leader in 1984).<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=UbMkAQAAIAAJ&q=chen+120%2C000 |title=Non-sectarian Politics in Malaysia: The Case of Parti Gerakan Rakyat Malaysia|author=Neil Jin Keong Khor |author2=Kay Peng Khoo |publisher=Trafalgar Publishing House|year= 2008 |pages=84, 90 |isbn=978-983-43245-1-3}}</ref> In the [[Malaysian general election, 1982|1982 general election]], it increased its representation in both the national level (from 4 to 5, out of 7 seats allocated) as well as state level (from 12 to 15).<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=EV88Tf0yWqoC&pg=PA50 |title=Malaysia's 1982 General Election |author=Harold A. Crouch |page=50|publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |year= 1982|isbn=978-9971-902-45-2 }}</ref>

Before the [[Malaysian general election, 1986|1986 general election]], Gerakan came into conflict with UMNO when it was suggested that a Malay, possibly [[Anwar Ibrahim]] should replace Lim Chong Eu as Penang Chief Minister after his term expired.  Gerakan threatened to resign from BN unless it was allocated more seats.<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=X14hOFCw_wAC&pg=PA29 |title=Malaysia's 1986 General Election: The Urban-rural Dichotomy|author= Sankaran Ramanathan|author2= Mohd. Hamdan Adnan |year=1990 |isbn=978-981-3035-12-6 |page=29 |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian }}</ref> It was then allocated 9 seats, it nevertheless lost 4 of these, thereby ending with the same number of seats (5).  It also saw increased pressure from [[Democratic Action Party (Malaysia)|Democratic Action Party]] which increased its representation in the [[Penang State Legislative Assembly|Penang state assembly]] and overtook Gerakan as the second largest party in the state.<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=X14hOFCw_wAC&pg=PA58 |title=Malaysia's 1986 General Election: The Urban-rural Dichotomy|author= Sankaran Ramanathan|author2= Mohd. Hamdan Adnan |year=1990 |isbn=978-981-3035-12-6 |page=58 |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian }}</ref>

In the [[Malaysian general election, 1990|1990 general election]], it suffered a setback when Lim Chong Eu was defeated by [[Lim Kit Siang]] of [[Democratic Action Party (Malaysia)|Democratic Action Party]] in his Padang Kota constituency, after which Lim Chong Eu retired from politics.<ref name="lim chong eu"/> Nevertheless, Gerakan retained the post of Chief Minister of Penang with Dr. [[Koh Tsu Koon]] taking over the position.  In the [[Malaysian general election, 1995|1995 general election]], Gerakan bounced back, winning 7 parliamentary seats and 23 state seats. The same year defecting members from [[Liberal Democratic Party (Malaysia)|LDP]] founded the party's [[Sabah]] chapter in 1995, gaining the party's first foothold in [[East Malaysia]]. Representation in the [[Sabah State Legislative Assembly|Assembly]] was brief as Kong Hong Ming, the sole representative there defected again to [[United Sabah Party|PBS]] in 1996.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Chin |first=Ung-Ho |url=https://openresearch-repository.anu.edu.au/server/api/core/bitstreams/525486e8-9520-43d2-a6d2-79c5e6d507ff/content |title='Kataks', Kadazan-Dusun nationalism and development: The 1999 Sabah state election. |date=1999 |publisher=Department of Political and Social Change Research, School of Pacific and Asian Studies, [[Australian National University]] |isbn=07315 2678 3}}</ref>

For nearly four decades, from 1969 to 2008, although not necessarily the biggest party (UMNO was the biggest party in Penang for many years from the late 1970s onward), Gerakan played a dominant role in the [[Penang State Legislative Assembly|Penang State Legislature]], and became closely associated with the fortune of Penang.  In that time there had been only two chief ministers in Penang, Lim and Koh Tsu Koon, both from Gerakan.<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=-oIyKT1MOA4C&pg=PA124 |title=The City in Southeast Asia: Patterns, Processes and Policy |author= Peter James Rimmer |author2= Howard W. Dick |publisher=Univ of Hawaii Pr|year= 2009 |pages=123–124|isbn= 978-0-8248-3313-8 }}</ref> In 1996, in an attempt to win broader support, the party shifted its headquarter to [[Kuala Lumpur]].<ref name="a to z"/>

In the [[Malaysian general election, 2004|2004 general election]], Gerakan achieved its best electoral result, winning 10 parliamentary seats and 30 state seats.  On 27 August 2005, its president [[Lim Keng Yaik]] was challenged by the Deputy [[Kerk Choo Ting]] in its party election. Lim retained his party President post after winning with 983 votes against 628 votes for Kerk. [[Koh Tsu Koon]] became the new Deputy President which he won unopposed after the nomination day for the party election.

=== 2008–2017 ===
In 2008, Lim Keng Yaik retired from politics after relinquishing the post of party president in 2007. [[Koh Tsu Koon]] took over as acting president in 2007, and won the presidency uncontested in the 2008 party elections.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.gerakan.org.my/about-us/former-presidents/item/3867-sdr-tan-sri-dr-koh-tsu-koon-president-from-2007-present/3867-sdr-tan-sri-dr-koh-tsu-koon-president-from-2007-present |title=Sdr Tan Sri Dr Koh Tsu Koon (President from 2007 to 2013) |work=Parti Gerakan Rakyat Malaysia }}</ref>

In the [[Malaysian general election, 2008|2008 general election]], the party suffered its worst electoral defeat. The party retained only two parliament seats, compared to the 10 seats it had before the election. As a result, the party lost its only cabinet post in the ensuing cabinet shuffle. In addition, Gerakan also lost power in Penang after governing the state for almost 39 years.<ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=hPi3BAAAQBAJ&pg=PA107 |title=he Influence of the Type of Dominant Party on Democracy: A Comparison Between South Africa and Malaysia |author= Malte Kaßner |pages=106–108|publisher=Springer VS |year= 2014 |isbn=978-3-658-04437-4  }}</ref> Soon after the defeat, a number of prominent members, such as [[Tan Kee Kwong]] and Lee Kah Choon, went over to the opposition coalition [[Pakatan Rakyat]] to take up posts with Opposition-led state governments in Penang and Selangor.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.thestar.com.my/story/?file=%2F2008%2F5%2F29%2Fnation%2F21392333&sec=nation |date=29 May 2008 |title=Gerakan's Tan 'joins' Pakatan |author=Dharmender Singh |author2=Christina Tan |work=The Star |access-date=17 March 2015 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171209044425/https://www.thestar.com.my/story/?file=%2F2008%2F5%2F29%2Fnation%2F21392333&sec=nation |archive-date=9 December 2017  }}</ref>

After the dismal performance in the 12th general election, Gerakan launched a party rejuvenation campaign in conjunction with the party's 40th anniversary celebrations on 25 May 2008 in its bid to rebound in mainstream politics and to regain people's confidence. It outlined three main thrusts, namely to voice Gerakan's ideology, policy position and advocate Malaysian solutions for various major issues, to rebuild, rebrand and re-empower the party at all levels, and to regain people's confidence.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.therakyatpost.com/news/2013/10/29/will-a-party-rejuvenation-program-save-gerakan-from-its-impending-doom/ |title=Will a party rejuvenation program save Gerakan from its impending doom? |work=Rakyat Post |date=29 October 2013 |access-date=17 March 2015 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180115003354/http://www.therakyatpost.com/news/2013/10/29/will-a-party-rejuvenation-program-save-gerakan-from-its-impending-doom/ |archive-date=15 January 2018  }}</ref> The party also launched a new slogan, "Forward Together with One Heart" (Satu Hati Gerak Bersama), and set out its area of concerns and strategies.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.thesundaily.my/node/165982 |title=Gerakan still has a role to play: Koh |work=The Sun Daily |date= 26 May 2008}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.gerakan.org.my/news-media/gerakan-matters/item/download/7_0377b3057c676354dd061936575ea772 |title=Gerakan Matters |work=Gerakan }}</ref>

In the [[Malaysian general election, 2013|2013 general election]], the party failed to improve its position and losing one its two remaining parliamentary seats. Later in the year, [[Mah Siew Keong]] took over as president after beating Penang Gerakan chairman [[Teng Chang Yeow]] for the post.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.thestar.com.my/News/Nation/2013/10/26/Gerakan-Mah-Siew-Keong-is-the-new-party-president/ |title=Gerakan: Mah Siew Keong is the new party president |work=The Star |author= L. Suganya |author2= D. Kanyakumari |date=26 October 2013  }}</ref> In 2016, for the first time in history, the President of BN did not attend the annual general meeting of Gerakan and instead briefly appeared on video for 2 minutes.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.themalaymailonline.com/malaysia/article/do-more-to-win-people-over-najib-urges-gerakan|title=Do more to win people over, Najib urges Gerakan|date=9 October 2016|website=The Malay Mail|access-date=2 April 2018}}</ref>

=== 2018–present ===

In the [[Malaysian general election, 2018|2018 general election]], Gerakan failed to win any seat and therefore had no representation in Parliament for the first time. On 23 June 2018, the party unanimously decided to leave BN in the aftermath of the election.<ref name="Gerakan leaves Barisan Nasional"/> Following the party's departure from [[Barisan Nasional]], the party contested in the [[2019 Tanjung Piai by-election]], which it lost. It joined the [[Perikatan Nasional]] coalition in February 2021.<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/former-bn-party-gerakan-is-now-part-of-perikatan-nasional |title=Former BN party Gerakan joins Malaysia's ruling Perikatan Nasional coalition |work=The Straits Times |date= 12 February 2021 }}</ref>

Gerakan contested the [[2022 Malaysian general election]] as part of the [[Perikatan Nasional]] coalition.<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2022/10/26/gerakan-to-contest-under-perikatan-logo|title=Gerakan to contest under Perikatan logo |author=Lo Tern Chern |date=26 October 2022 |work=The Star}}</ref>  However, it again failed to win any seat in the parliamentary or state elections. However, the party won a seat in the [[2023 Kedah state election]], which is the party's first electoral victory since the [[2014 Telok Intan by-election]].

GERAKAN's sole state representative joined Kedah state government as junior partner to PAS. 

* '''[[Kedah]]''' (''1972–1990, 1995–2008'', ''2023–present'')
* '''[[Penang]]''' ('''1969–2008''')
* '''[[Perak]]''' (''1972–2008, 2020–2021'')
* '''[[Johor]]''' (''1982–2018'')
* '''[[Pahang]]''' (''1972–1978, 1982–2013'')
* '''[[Selangor]]''' (''1972–1986, 1995–2008'')
* [[Negeri Sembilan|'''Negeri Sembilan''']] (''1995–2008'')
* '''[[Sabah]]''' (''1995–1996, 2008–2018'')
* '''[[Malacca]]''' (''1972–1974, 2004–2008'')
