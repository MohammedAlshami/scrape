'''Green Wave''' ([[Malay language|Malay]]: ''Gelombang Hijau''),<ref name="Will a second ‘green wave’ soon sweep Malaysia?">{{cite web |last=Hassan |first=Hazlin |date=27 March 2023 |title=Will a second 'green wave' soon sweep Malaysia? |url=https://www.straitstimes.com/opinion/will-a-second-green-wave-soon-sweep-malaysia |accessdate=23 July 2023 |work=The Straits Times}}</ref> also known as '''Green Tsunami''' ([[Malay language|Malay]]: ''Tsunami Hijau''),<ref name="‘Green wave’ sweeps over voters">{{cite web |last1=Teng |first1=Alex |last2=Hilmy |first2=Imran |date=9 December 2022 |title='Green wave' sweeps over voters |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2022/12/09/green-wave-sweeps-over-voters |accessdate=21 July 2023 |work=[[The Star (Malaysia)|The Star]]}}</ref> is a political phenomenon that took place in [[Malaysia]] during the [[2022 Malaysian general election]] and onwards. This political phenomenon involves [[Malaysian Islamic Party]] (PAS) and its [[ultraconservative]] voters, who mainly originate from the northeastern and northwestern parts of [[Peninsular Malaysia]].

In 2020, [[Mahathir Mohamad]] resigned as prime minister. The decision contributed to the [[2020–2022 Malaysian political crisis]] and the formation of the [[Perikatan Nasional]] coalition, consisting of the [[Malaysian United Indigenous Party]] (BERSATU) and [[Malaysian Islamic Party]] (PAS) among others, which formed the federal government alongside the [[UMNO|United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO)-led Barisan Nasional.<ref>{{Cite web |date=5 March 2020 |title=How Malaysia's government collapsed in two years |url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-51716474 |access-date=28 April 2026 |website=[[BBC News]] |language=en-GB}}</ref>

All three parties share Malay nationalist tendencies, which heavily influences the Malaysian Islamic Party alongside [[Islamism]].<ref>{{Cite book |last=Farish Ahmad Noor |title=The Malaysian Islamic Party PAS, 1951-2013: Islamism in a mottled nation |date=2014 |publisher=Amsterdam University Press |isbn=978-90-8964-576-0 |series=Religion and society in Asia |location=Amsterdam}}</ref><ref>{{Citation |last1=Yilmaz |first1=Ihsan |title=Seven Decades of PAS in Malaysian Politics |date=2024 |work=Islamist Parties and Power in Democratic Nation-States: A Comparative Analysis of Six Muslim-Majority Countries |pages=49–90 |editor-last=Yilmaz |editor-first=Ihsan |url=https://doi.org/10.1007/978-981-97-4343-8_3 |access-date=30 April 2026 |place=Singapore |publisher=Springer Nature |language=en |doi=10.1007/978-981-97-4343-8_3 |isbn=978-981-97-4343-8 |last2=Shukri |first2=Syaza |editor2-last=Shukri |editor2-first=Syaza|url-access=subscription }}</ref>

The Malaysian Islamic Party's [[Political base|electoral base]] is largely centered around Peninsular Malaysia's rural and conservative northern and eastern coasts, known as the Malay-Muslim heartland,<ref>{{Cite news |date=31 March 2026 |title=What gives happiness in Malaysia's east coast heartland of Terengganu |url=https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/the-mosque-the-river-the-sea-why-terengganu-folk-say-they-need-little-else-to-be-happy |access-date=29 April 2026 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |language=en |issn=0585-3923}}</ref> particularly in the states of [[Kelantan]] and [[Terengganu]]. The party is led by [[Abdul Hadi Awang]], a member of the ''ulama'' (religious scholar) faction, who has been its president since 2002. [[Muhammad Sanusi Md Nor]], a member of the party's professional faction, is the election director of Perikatan Nasional as well as Chief Minister of [[Kedah]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=PAS leader pulls out of race for key post in Malaysia Islamist party - what are the political implications? |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-pas-sanusi-election-vice-president-5330136 |access-date=2026-04-28 |website=CNA |language=en}}</ref>

The United Malays National Organisation is seen and often presents itself as the champion of [[Ketuanan Melayu]], translated as Malay overlordship or supremacy, as well as the protector of Malay interests.<ref>{{Cite journal |last=Wade |first=Geoff |date=November 2009 |title=The Origins and Evolution of Ethnocracy in Malaysia |journal=[[Asia-Pacific Journal]] |language=en |volume=7 |issue=47 |article-number=e4 |doi=10.1017/S1557466009008948 |doi-access=free |issn=1557-4660}}</ref> It was the predominant party in Malaysian politics from independence up until [[2018 Malaysian general election|2018]], and leads the Barisan Nasional coalition.<ref name=":1" /><ref>{{Cite report |url=https://www.jstor.org/stable/resrep40184 |title=Late Malaysian Politics: From Single Party Dominance to Multi Party Mayhem |last=Case |first=William |date=2021 |publisher=S. Rajaratnam School of International Studies}}</ref> Following [[Najib Razak]]'s imprisonment for corruption, the party has been led by [[Ahmad Zahid Hamidi]].

The Malaysian United Indigenous Party was founded by former UMNO members who left the party over Najib's leadership and the [[1MDB scandal]]. Initially led by Mahathir, it was a member of Pakatan Harapan. Following its exit from that coalition, the party was led by [[Muhyiddin Yassin]], who formed Perikatan Nasional and became prime minister.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Lopez |first=Leslie |date=12 November 2025 |title=Under growing pressure to hand over Bersatu reins, former Malaysia PM Muhyiddin fights for political survival |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-bersatu-muhyiddin-yassin-politics-5459846 |access-date=29 April 2026 |website=[[Channel News Asia]] |language=en}}</ref>{{multiple image

== Broad contributing factors ==
=== Identitarian politics, social media, and the youth vote ===
Three elements, ''race, religion, and royalty'' — collectively referred to as the 3Rs — have dominated political and social discourse within Malaysia.<ref>{{Citation |last1=Abdul Hamid |first1=Ahmad Fauzi |title=Race, Religion and Royalty in Malaysian Politics |date=2025 |work=Race, Religion, Royalty in Malaysia: Discursively Reproduced, Resisted, Renegotiated |pages=23–37 |editor-last=Rajandran |editor-first=Kumaran |url=https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-031-94985-2_2 |access-date=28 April 2026 |place=Cham |publisher=Springer Nature Switzerland |language=en |doi=10.1007/978-3-031-94985-2_2 |isbn=978-3-031-94985-2 |last2=Che Mohd Razali |first2=Che Hamdan |editor2-last=Mohamad Jamil |editor2-first=Siti Nurnadilla|url-access=subscription }}</ref> Rural and conservative Malay voters in particular are more likely to vote for Malay-based parties as they are seen to better represent Malay interests, fight for the supremacy of Islam, and support royal institutions. Conversely, many perceive [[Pakatan Harapan]] as insufficiently protective of Malay rights.<ref>{{Cite web |last1=Arrifin |first1=Afifah |last2=Koh |first2=Fabian |date=16 November 2022 |title=Courting the Malay vote: Race-based politics still centrestage in Malaysian politics |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-general-election-malay-vote-race-based-politics-umno-3076591 |access-date=28 April 2026 |website=[[Channel News Asia]] |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |last=Yusof |first=Amir |date=22 October 2022 |title=Malaysia election: Why the ethnic Malay votes matter and who has the upper hand |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-general-election-malay-urban-rural-voters-3017431 |access-date=28 April 2026 |website=[[Channel News Asia]] |language=en}}</ref>

In a pre-election survey conducted by [[Merdeka Center]] in conjunction with [[Sisters in Islam]] in 2022, religion was primary identity marker for Muslim youth at 45%, followed by nationality at 28%. Less than a third believed that Islam and politics, as well as Islam and matters of public life should be kept separate. 68% of respondents agreed that [[Sharia]] should have equal standing to common law or replace it entirely, while 75% agreed that [[Hudud]] should be implemented.<ref>{{Cite web |date=17 June 2022 |title=Muslim youth outlook increasingly pessimistic amidst strong retention of Islamic idealism |url=https://sistersinislam.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/06/Muslim-Youth-Survey-2022-News-Release.pdf |access-date=29 April 2026 |website=[[Sisters in Islam]] |language=en-GB}}</ref>

The 2022 general election was the first to be held following the constitutional amendment that lowered the voting age from 21 to 18 was passed in 2019, resulting in 6 million new voters. Of the 21.1 million eligible voters in total, 1.4 million were aged between 18 and 20.<ref>{{Cite web |last1=Solhi |first1=Farah |last2=Nizam |first2=Fuad |date=8 November 2022 |title=GE15: Young voters cast as kingmakers {{!}} New Straits Times |url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/politics/2022/11/848134/ge15-young-voters-cast-kingmakers |access-date=26 November 2025 |website=[[New Straits Times]] |language=en}}</ref> Social media is generally understood to have played a considerable role in the election, being employed by political parties as a method of courting first-time voters,<ref name=":0">{{Cite web |last=Ooi |first=Kok Hin |date=22 December 2022 |title=How Political Parties Used TikTok in the 2022 Malaysian General Election - RSIS |url=https://rsis.edu.sg/rsis-publication/rsis/how-political-parties-used-tiktok-in-the-2022-malaysian-general-election/ |access-date=26 November 2025 |website=S. Rajaratnam School of International Studies |language=en-US}}</ref> with analysts crediting its effective employment by Perikatan Nasional, particularly [[TikTok]], for their success.<ref>{{Cite web |title=The Big Read: With split in youth vote, will Malaysia politics see a new dawn post-GE or yesterday once more? |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/today/big-read/malaysia-ge15-split-youth-vote-politics-election-2022-3119936 |access-date=2025-11-26 |website=CNA |language=en|date=5 December 2022|last=Ong|first=Justin Guang-Xi}}</ref> Political scientist Ooi Kok Him noted that Perikatan Nasional relied on content ostensibly coming from third-parties more so than other parties.<ref name=":0" />

According to a study conducted by Iman Research, the 2022 election was dominated by Malay-Muslim issues, with political rhetoric shifting further rightwards and becoming more [[Identity politics|identitarian]]. It also found that Malay nationalist rhetoric amplified through social media was especially effective in aiding Perikatan Nasional capture first-time voters.<ref name="Iman Research: PAS' 'green wave' long time coming, came from lack of Pakatan presence and winning over first-time voters">{{cite web |last=Noorshahrizam |first=Shahrin Aizat |date=20 January 2023 |title=Iman Research: PAS' 'green wave' long time coming, came from lack of Pakatan presence and winning over first-time voters |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2023/01/20/iman-research-pas-green-wave-long-time-coming-came-from-lack-of-pakatan-presence-and-winning-over-first-time-voters/51161 |accessdate=21 July 2023 |work=Malay Mail}}</ref>

A post-election study by [[Malaysiakini]] found that young voters aged between 18 and 39 were more supportive of Perikatan Nasional at 37%, than Pakatan Harapan at 35%, and Barisan Nasional at 23%, and attributed the Green Wave to youth votes.<ref>{{Cite web |date=20 July 2023 |title=PRU15: Gelombang Hijau Penangan Pengundi Muda |trans-title=GE15: The Green Wave a result of youth votes |url=https://newslab.malaysiakini.com/greenwave-generational-gap/ms/ |website=[[Malaysiakini]]}}</ref>

A study released in 2024 conducted on Malay youth aged 10-35 from [[Peninsular Malaysia]] showed that [[multiculturalism]] was considered acceptable as long as Islam and Malay rights remained unaffected.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Ariff |first=Syed Umar |date=22 September 2024 |title=What Malay youths want: Yes to multiculturalism, but... |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/focus/2024/09/22/what-malay-youths-want-yes-to-multiculturalism-but |access-date=28 April 2025 |website=[[The Star (Malaysia)|The Star]] |language=en}}</ref> Individuals who identified themselves as supporters of the Malaysian Islamic Party and Barisan Nasional generally believed that the country must be led by a Malay Muslim.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Ariff |first=Syed Umar |date=22 September 2024 |title='Orang kita': Grounded and open |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/focus/2024/09/22/039orang-kita039-grounded-and-open |access-date=28 April 2026 |website=[[The Star (Malaysia)|The Star]] |language=en}}</ref>

=== Malay dissatisfaction with UMNO ===
UMNO leader [[Ahmad Zahid Hamidi]] was ranked the least popular Malay leader in a poll conducted by [[Merdeka Center]] entering the 2022 election,<ref>{{Cite web |date=30 October 2022 |title=Report: Muhyiddin more popular than Zahid |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/641621 |access-date=29 April 2026 |website=[[Malaysiakini]]}}</ref> by which time UMNO had already failed to win a majority of the Malay electorate in four of the five preceding elections.<ref name=":1">{{Cite journal |last=Sani |first=Mohd Azizuddin Mohd |date=4 December 2023 |title=2023/94 "UMNO's Prospects: Oblivion, Survival or Recovery" by Mohd Azizuddin Mohd Sani |url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/articles-commentaries/iseas-perspective/2023-94-umnos-prospects-oblivion-survival-or-recovery-by-mohd-azizuddin-mohd-sani/ |journal=[[ISEAS – Yusof Ishak Institute|ISEAS Perspective]] |language=en-US |volume=2023 |issue=94}}</ref> The party's image was also marred by corruption allegations, which Perikatan Nasional exploited by running on a "clean" and "religious" campaign.<ref name="Malaysia’s ‘Green Wave’ Was a Long Time Coming">{{cite web |last=Rahman |first=Serina |date=26 January 2023 |title=Malaysia's 'Green Wave' Was a Long Time Coming |url=https://fulcrum.sg/malaysias-green-wave-was-a-long-time-coming/ |accessdate=21 July 2023 |work=Fulcrum Singapore}}</ref>

Its poor performance with the Malay electorate continued into the 2022 election, where the UMNO-led [[Barisan Nasional]] won just 32% of the Malay vote, whereas [[Perikatan Nasional]] won 52%.<ref>{{Cite web |last= |date=11 August 2023 |title=Ilham Centre: 3:3 status quo expected with high likelihood of vote swing from UMNO members to PN |url=https://focusmalaysia.my/ilham-centre-33-status-quo-expected-with-high-likelihood-of-vote-swing-from-umno-members-to-pn/ |access-date=28 April 2026 |website=Focus Malaysia |language=en-US}}</ref> The ratio shifted further in favour of Perikatan Nasional in the 2023 state elections despite Barisan Nasional entering into an alliance with Pakatan Harapan, where the combined ticket secured just 29% of the Malay vote. UMNO itself won just 19 of the 108 seats it contested.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Loheswar |first=R. |date=25 August 2023 |title=Ilham Centre survey found Perikatan's 2R tactics scared ethnic Chinese voters to turn out in droves for six state elections |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2023/08/25/ilham-centre-survey-found-perikatans-2r-tactics-scared-ethnic-chinese-voters-to-turn-out-in-droves-for-six-state-elections/87209 |access-date=28 April 2026 |website=[[Malay Mail]] |language=en}}</ref>

UMNO's poor performance in 2023 in particular were driven by a combination of dissatisfaction with party leader [[Ahmad Zahid Hamidi]] and disapproval of co-operation with the Chinese-dominated [[Democratic Action Party]], which is seen as anti-Malay, as part of the wider "[[Anwar Ibrahim cabinet|unity government]]" formed following the 2022 election.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Bunyan |first=John |date=11 August 2026 |title=Umno grassroots' 'silent protest' vote to cost party dearly in state elections, says Ilham Centre |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2023/08/11/umno-grassroots-silent-protest-vote-to-cost-party-dearly-in-state-elections-says-ilham-centre/84754 |access-date=28 April 2026 |website=[[Malay Mail]] |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |last=Loheswar |first=R. |date=25 August 2023 |title=Ilham Centre: Malay voters rejected Umno in state polls due to disdain towards Zahid |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2023/08/25/ilham-centre-malay-voters-rejected-umno-in-state-polls-due-to-disdain-towards-zahid/87203 |access-date=28 April 2026 |website=[[Malay Mail]] |language=en}}</ref>

== Impact on 2022 general election and state elections==
{{Main|2022 Malaysian general election|2022 Malaysian state elections}}

[[File:2022_Malaysian_general_election_results_map.svg|thumb|500px|Results for 2022 general election. PN swept all the seats in northeast and northwest Peninsular Malaysia except Sungai Petani.]]
The [[Perikatan Nasional]] (PN) coalition, comprising the [[Malaysian Islamic Party]] (PAS), [[Malaysian United Indigenous Party]] (BERSATU) and [[Malaysian People's Movement Party]] (GERAKAN), won 74 seats in the Malaysian [[Dewan Rakyat|lower house]], becoming the second largest coalition in Dewan Rakyat after PH. PAS had the most number of elected members in the Dewan Rakyat with 43 members, while BERSATU had 31 members. Most of the seats that had been captured by PN have more than 70% registered Malay voters.

In Perlis, [[2022 Perlis state election|the election]] saw the incumbent [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN) coalition failed to win a single seat. The PN coalition won 14 of the 15 seats, achieving a [[supermajority]] and formed the first ever non-BN government in the state's history. PH won the remaining seat and became the sole opposition.

In Pahang, [[2022 Pahang state election|the election]] resulted in a hung assembly with PN winning 17 seats, the same number of seats won by the incumbent BN coalition. It ended BN's dominance in the state. PH won the remaining 8 seats.

In Perak, [[2022 Perak state election|the election]] resulted in a [[hung assembly]]. Although PN won 26 seats, it did not have enough seats to form a majority. PH won 24 seats and the incumbent BN coalition won the remaining 9 seats.

The [[Perikatan Nasional]] (PN) coalition won 146 seats out of 245 seats that were contested in 2023 state elections.

In Selangor, the [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN)–[[Pakatan Harapan]] (PH) electoral pact won the [[2023 Selangor state election|election]] by capturing 34 of 56 seats, with PH winning 32 of those seats and BN winning 2 seats. The Perikatan Nasional (PN) coalition won the remaining 22 seats to become the main opposition in the state assembly.

In Kelantan, the governing PN coalition led by [[Malaysian Islamic Party]] (PAS) won 43 of 45 seats in the [[2023 Kelantan state election|election]], achieving a supermajority and continuing PAS's dominance in the state since 1990. The BN–PH electoral pact won the remaining two seats, with BN and PH each winning one seat and becoming the main opposition in the state assembly.

In Terengganu, the PN coalition led by PAS won all 32 seats, recording the first clean sweep in the state's history since 1978. BN lost all the 10 seats it had prior to the [[2023 Terengganu state election|election]]. This left the state assembly with no elected opposition representatives.

In Negeri Sembilan, the BN–PH electoral pact won 31 of 36 seats, achieving a supermajority, with PH winning 17 of those seats and BN winning 14 seats in the [[2023 Negeri Sembilan state election|election]]. The PN coalition won the remaining 5 seats and became the main opposition in the state assembly.

In Kedah. the governing PN coalition led by PAS won 33 of 36 seats, achieving a supermajority. PH won the remaining three seats for the BN–PH electoral pact. This meant for the first time in the state's history, BN would have no representation in the state assembly. PH took BN's place as the state's main opposition following the [[2023 Kedah state election|election]].

In Penang, the BN–PH electoral pact won 29 of 40 seats in the [[2023 Penang state election|election]], with PH winning 27 of those seats and BN winning 2 seats. The PN coalition won the remaining 11 Malay-majority seats to become the main opposition in the state assembly.

== Impact on 2023 by-elections ==

In the [[2023 Kuala Terengganu by-election]], the PAS candidate managed to defend their victory in the [[2023 Kuala Terengganu by-election]] with a larger majority than in the [[2022 Malaysian general election|2022 general election]].

In the [[2023 Pulai by-election|2023 Pulai]] and [[2023 Simpang Jeram by-election|2023 Simpang Jeram by-elections]], Perikatan Nasional failed to win the seats but managed to reduce the majority of the Pakatan Harapan-Barisan Nasional candidates. The victory was framed as a successful halting of the Green Wave.<ref>{{Cite web |date=10 September 2023 |title=Kemenangan PRK Pulai, Simpang Jeram bukti rakyat Johor matang politik|url=https://www.bharian.com.my/berita/nasional/2023/09/1151007/kemenangan-prk-pulai-simpang-jeram-bukti-rakyat-johor-matang-politik}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=10 September 2023 |title=PH sekat gelombang hijau di Johor|url=https://www.sinarharian.com.my/article/278183/berita/nasional/ph-sekat-gelombang-hijau-di-johor}}</ref>

In the [[2023 Pelangai by-election]], the Barisan Nasional candidate managed to defend their victory in the [[Pelangai (state constituency)|seat]] with a smaller majority than in the [[2022 Pahang state election|2022 state election]].<ref>{{Cite web |date=7 October 2023 |title=Pelangai win puts a dent to PN's green wave aspiration, says Pahang Umno chief|url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/politics/2023/10/964234/pelangai-win-puts-dent-pns-green-wave-aspiration-says-pahang-umno-chief}}</ref>

In the [[2023 Kemaman by-election]], the [[Menteri Besar of Terengganu|Chief Minister of Terengganu]] [[Ahmad Samsuri Mokhtar]] from the Malaysian Islamic Party defeated former [[Chief of Defence Forces (Malaysia)|Chief of Defence Forces]] [[Raja Mohamed Affandi]] of Barisan Nasional in a landslide.
