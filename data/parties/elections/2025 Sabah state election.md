The '''2025 Sabah state election''', formally the '''17th Sabah general election''',<ref>{{Cite web |date=16 October 2025 |title=Pilihan Raya Umum Dewan Undangan Sabah ke-17 |url=https://spr.gov.my/prudusabahke17/ |website=Suruhanjaya Pilihan Raya (SPR)}}</ref>{{Efn|The Election Commission of Malaysia officially used the term PRU Sabah 2025 to denote the state-level electoral process in Sabah. In English, this is translated as the 2025 Sabah General Election.}} was held on 29 November 2025. All 73 seats were contested to elect members of [[17th Sabah State Legislative Assembly]]. No constituencies were added or removed in a [[Redistribution (election)|redistribution]] from the previous [[2020 Sabah state election|election]].

Incumbent Chief Minister [[Hajiji Noor]] led his [[Gabungan Rakyat Sabah]] majority coalition government to seek a second five-year term. GRS was primarily challenged by [[Heritage Party (Malaysia) | WARISAN]], led by state opposition leader [[Shafie Apdal]]. Other parties such as [[Barisan Nasional]], [[Pakatan Harapan]],[[Social Democratic Harmony Party | KDM]], [[Perikatan Nasional]], [[Homeland Solidarity Party |STAR]], [[Sabah Progressive Party |SAPP]], [[United Progressive Kinabalu Organisation |UPKO]], [[Parti Impian Sabah]], and other minor parties and independents also contested in this election.

The state election resulted in a [[hung assembly]] for the second time since the [[2018 Sabah state election]]. GRS won the most seats in the [[Sabah State Legislative Assembly]] with 29 seats.

== Background ==
Traditionally, state elections are held simultaneously with the parliamentary election but each state can decide when to hold its election. This is because state assemblies are dissolved by their respective [[Conference of Rulers|ruler]] or governor on the advice of the chief minister of the state.

The pre-election state government led by Hajiji was formed following the [[2023 Sabah political crisis]], where [[United Malays National Organisation of Sabah|UMNO Sabah]] had attempted to engineer the formation of a new government by retracting its support for Hajiji in the state legislative assembly. The attempt failed and resulted in the exclusion of UMNO and [[Malaysian United Indigenous Party]] (BESATU) from GRS and the state government. Pakatan Harapan, with whom Hajiji's GRS was allied [[Anwar Ibrahim cabinet|at the federal level]], was instead brought into the state government.

In the lead-up to the election, GRS and Pakatan Harapan agreed to an electoral pact to avoid contesting against each other. Unhappy with the decision, GRS component parties STAR and SAPP left the coalition and contested the election together.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Two parties going solo|url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2025/10/03/two-parties-going-solo|website=[[The Star (Malaysia)|The Star]]|date=3 October 2025|access-date=1 December 2025|language=en|last=Vanar|first=Muguntan}}</ref> Separately, UPKO exited Pakatan Harapan, citing differences in understanding, particularly over issues of state rights and autonomy, as well as frustrations surrounding the political leadership of Pakatan Harapan's Sabah chapter.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Upko quits Pakatan, citing Sabah rights disputes and lack of autonomy|url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2025/11/10/upko-quits-pakatan-citing-sabah-rights-disputes-and-lack-of-autonomy/197896|website=[[Malay Mail]]|date=10 November 2025|access-date=1 December 2025|language=en}}</ref>

The state election was held against a backdrop of increased nationalist or regionalist sentiment, encapsulated by the "Sabah for Sabahans" slogan, related to the state's fight for autonomy,<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|title=Sabah polls: Runner-up Warisan coy on next step, says no talks with BN on forming state government|url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-sabah-elections-shafie-apdal-warisan-opposition-5498141|website=[[Channel News Asia]]|access-date=1 December 2025|language=en|date=30 November 2025|last=Norman|first=Izzah Aqilah}}</ref> including over the issue of revenue sharing. Other prominent issues included a mining license corruption scandal, which implicated members of the ruling GRS alliance as well as the federal government through a series of secret recordings made by businessman Albert Tei released over the course of a year.<ref>{{Cite news|title=Sabah corruption scandal hits PM Anwar’s office as videos and claims emerge|url=https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/sabah-corruption-scandal-hits-pm-anwars-office-as-videos-and-claims-emerge|work=[[The Straits Times]]|date=26 November 2025|access-date=1 December 2025|issn=0585-3923|language=en|last=Azmi|first=Hadi|last2=Mustafa|first2=Muzliza}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=6 things to know as Sabahans head to the polls in high-stakes election|url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-sabah-election-polling-day-anwar-who-will-win-5495526|website=[[Channel News Asia]]|access-date=1 December 2025|language=en|date=29 November 2025|last=Norman|first=Izzah Aqilah|last2=Mahmud|first2=Aqil Haziq}}</ref>

== Electoral system ==
{{main|Elections in Malaysia}}
Elections in Malaysia are conducted at the federal and state levels. Federal elections elect members of the [[Dewan Rakyat]], the lower house of [[Parliament of Malaysia|Parliament]], while state elections in each of the 13 states elect members of their respective [[State legislative assemblies of Malaysia|state legislative assembly]]. As Malaysia follows the [[Westminster system]] of government, the [[head of government]] ([[Prime Minister of Malaysia|Prime Minister]] at the federal level and the [[Chief Ministers in Malaysia|Menteri Besar/Chief Ministers/Premier]] at the state level) is the person who commands the confidence of the majority of members in the respective legislature – this is normally the leader of the party or coalition with the majority of seats in the legislature.

The Legislative Assembly consists of 73 members, known as [[Members of the Legislative Assembly]] (MLAs), that are elected for five-year terms. Each MLA is elected from a [[single-member district|single-member constituencies]] using the [[first-past-the-post voting|first-past-the-post voting system]]; each constituency contains approximately an equal number of voters. If one party obtains a majority of seats, then that party is entitled to form the government, with its leader becoming the Chief Minister. In the event of a [[hung parliament]], where no single party obtains the majority of seats, the government may still form through a [[coalition]] or a [[confidence and supply]] agreement with other parties. In practice, coalitions and alliances in Malaysia, and by extension, in Sabah, generally persist between elections, and member parties do not normally contest for the same seats.

== Preparations ==
On 27 March 2024, State Chairman of [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN) and [[United Malays National Organisation of Sabah|United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO) of Sabah [[Bung Moktar Radin]] dismissed the possibility of Sabah BN to work with [[Gabungan Rakyat Sabah]] (GRS) coalition led by Chief Minister [[Hajiji Noor]].

Hajiji meanwhile, said that GRS is open to collaboration from other parties, but insists that the preference is working together with [[Pakatan Harapan]] (PH), who joined the Sabah government after BN withdrawal during the [[2023 Sabah political crisis]].<ref>{{cite web |title=GRS reaffirms commitment to work with Sabah PH for state election |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2024/12/15/grs-reaffirms-commitment-to-work-with-sabah-ph-for-state-election/ |website=Free Malaysia Today |access-date=17 December 2024 |language=en |date=15 December 2024}}</ref> [[Parti Warisan Sabah]] (WARISAN) also were interested in cooperating with GRS for the elections, but according to its president [[Shafie Apdal]], its invitation to discuss such collaborations were turned down by GRS several times.<ref>{{cite web |title=GRS snubbed Warisan's calls for collaboration, says Shafie |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2025/01/05/grs-snubbed-warisans-calls-for-collaboration-says-shafie/ |website=Free Malaysia Today |access-date=7 January 2025 |language=en |date=5 January 2025}}</ref>

Meanwhile, [[Perikatan Nasional]] (PN) have announced its intention to contest all 73 state seats in its first foray into the state, the move which led to [[Sabah Progressive Party]]'s (SAPP) exit from PN, as announced by party leader [[Yong Teck Lee]] in December 2024, as SAPP insists that only party which has origins in Sabah are allowed to contest the election.<ref>{{cite web |author1=[[Bernama]] |title=SAPP quits Perikatan Nasional, Yong announces |url=https://thesun.my/malaysia-news/sapp-quits-perikatan-nasional-yong-announces-BE13420625 |website=thesun.my |access-date=16 December 2024 |language=en-MY |date=14 December 2024}}</ref> SAPP will contest the election under GRS, the local coalition which it is a component in parallel with PN since 2020. This is the first time PN contesting the election under its own banner, although its component party [[Malaysian United Indigenous Party of Sabah|Sabah BERSATU]] have contested and won seats before, under PH (PKR) and GRS in the 2018 and 2020 state elections respectively. PAS and GERAKAN also have contested in Sabah before, with GERAKAN having won seats under BN banner.

On 15 December 2024, [[Social Democratic Harmony Party]] (KDM), through its President [[Peter Anthony]] also confirmed that the party would contest the election as a party, without being in a coalition and working together with other parties, targeting 43 state seats. Anthony, who is also the [[Melalap (state constituency)|Melalap]] MLA however, announced that he would not contest the election and defend the Melalap state seat in the election, in view of the pending appeal to his court case.<ref>{{cite web |last1=Abdullah |first1=Izwan |title=KDM confirms contesting in Sabah polls, its president skips due to ongoing legal appeal |url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/politics/2024/12/1148744/kdm-confirms-contesting-sabah-polls-its-president-skips-due-ongoing |website=NST Online |access-date=17 December 2024 |language=en |date=15 December 2024}}</ref> After Peter loses his final appeal on 4 March 2025 and sentenced to jail immediately, the party moved on 7 March 2025 to appoint his daughter Priscella Peter as the acting President.<ref>{{cite web |last1=Vanar |first1=Muguntan |title=Peter Anthony's daughter Priscella to lead Parti KDM after his imprisonment |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2025/03/07/peter-anthonys-daughter-priscella-to-lead-parti-kdm-after-his-imprisonment |website=The Star |access-date=21 March 2025 |language=en |date=7 March 2025}}</ref>

On 15 January 2025, PN, through its Deputy Chairman [[Hamzah Zainudin]] launched the slogan 'Kasi Bagus Sabah' (Make Sabah Great Again in [[English language|English]]) as part of its preparations for contesting the election.<ref>{{Cite news |title=Sabah Perikatan launches comeback with "Kasi Bagus Sabah" slogan |last=Vanar |first=Muguntan |date=2025-01-16 |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2025/01/16/sabah-perikatan-launches-comeback-with-kasi-bagus-sabah-slogan |access-date=2025-11-04 |work=[[The Star (Malaysia)|The Star]] |language=en}}</ref>

On 16 January 2025, the [[Malaysian Chinese Association]] (MCA), through its President [[Wee Ka Siong]], confirmed that the party would contest in the election.

On 2 October 2025, the [[Homeland Solidarity Party]] (STAR) and [[Sabah Progressive Party]] (SAPP) has left the ruling [[Gabungan Rakyat Sabah]] (GRS) coalition. However 5 of STAR MLA decided to stay with GRS for the election, and were expelled from STAR.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Edgar |first1=J. A. |title=Two Star deputy presidents, three reps defy Kitingan to back GRS |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/757168 |access-date=8 October 2025 |work=Malaysiakini |date=6 October 2025}}</ref>

On 6 October 2025, Chief Minister Hajiji Noor officially announced the dissolution of the 16th [[Sabah State Legislative Assembly]].<ref>{{Cite news |title=Hajiji announces dissolution of Sabah state assembly, paving way for 17th state election |last=Chan |first=Julia |date=2025-10-06 |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2025/10/06/hajiji-announces-dissolution-of-sabah-state-assembly-paving-way-for-17th-state-election/193625#google_vignette |access-date=2025-11-04 |work=[[Malay Mail]] |language=en}}</ref> 

[[United Progressive Kinabalu Organisation]] (UPKO) announced on 10 November 2025 that it has exited the PH coalition and will contest under its own banner, a day after its leader, [[Ewon Benedick]] resigned as federal cabinet minister.<ref>{{cite web |title=Upko pulls out of PH day after Ewon quits Cabinet |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2025/11/10/upko-leaves-pakatan-harapan |website=Free Malaysia Today |access-date=15 November 2025 |language=en |date=10 November 2025}}</ref>

== Aftermath ==
Shortly after the election results were confirmed, Hajiji was sworn in as chief minister for a second term. While his coalition only won 29 seats in a hung assembly, he reportedly received the support of a number of other parties to form a government.<ref>{{Cite news |date=30 November 2025 |title=Sabah state election: Incumbent beats opposition, Hajiji Noor sworn in as Chief Minister |url=https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/hajiji-sworn-in-as-sabahs-chief-minister |access-date=3 December 2025 |work=[[The Straits Times]] |language=en |issn=0585-3923}}</ref> These included Barisan Nasional, UPKO, STAR, Pakatan Harapan, KDM, as well as five independent assemblymen.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2 December 2025 |title=After Cabinet snub, STAR to take on opposition role in Sabah |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2025/12/02/after-cabinet-snub-star-to-take-on-opposition-role-in-sabah |access-date=3 December 2025 |website=[[Free Malaysia Today]] |language=en}}</ref>

The state cabinet unveiled following Hajiji's swearing in included members of, besides his own coalition, Barisan Nasional (UMNO), UPKO, and Pakatan Harapan. The inclusion of Barisan Nasional in government generated some controversy and pushback in some quarters, including Hajiji's [[Parti Gagasan Rakyat Sabah|own party]]'s youth wing and a number of GRS assemblymen.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2 December 2025 |title='Unreasonable': BN's inclusion in Sabah state government sparks uproar among GRS coalition |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-sabah-election-barisan-nasional-grs-local-party-5522176 |access-date=3 December 2025 |website=[[Channel News Asia]] |language=en}}</ref>

Following its exclusion from government, STAR entered into opposition and reiterated its stance that the state government should only comprise local parties. Nevertheless, one of its two assemblymen was later appointed an assistant minister in a personal capacity.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Miwil |first=Olivia |date=2 December 2025 |title=Jeffrey Kitingan: Star to play opposition role after GRS cabinet formationn |url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/regional/2025/12/1328366/jeffrey-kitingan-star-play-opposition-role-after-grs-cabinet |access-date=3 December 2025 |website=[[New Straits Times]] |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=2 December 2025 |title=Star to remain opposition despite rep becoming assistant minister |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/762387 |access-date=3 December 2025 |website=[[Malaysiakini]]}}</ref>

State opposition and Warisan leader Shafie Apdal criticised the employment of state machinery by the federal and state government as well as the "rampant money politics" during the election, pointing out that his party had nevertheless won the popular vote.<ref name=":0" />

Despite the poor performance of Barisan Nasional as a whole, with UMNO losing six seats, coalition member [[United Sabah People's Party]] managed to regain representation in the assembly. Bung Moktar Radin, Sabah BN chairman and assemblyman-elect for Lamag, died on 5 December 2025, just six days after the election ended.<ref>{{Cite web|title=
Sabah BN chairman Bung passes away|url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2025/12/05/sabah-bn-chairman-bung-passes-away|website=[[The Star (Malaysia)|The Star]]|date=5 December 2025|access-date=5 December 2025|language=en}}</ref>

Pakatan Harapan had a poor showing, winning only one seat. Coalition member [[Democratic Action Party]] (DAP) lost in all eight constituencies it contested, including six it had held going into the election. The six seats were won by Warisan, including three the DAP had won in 2013 and were considered strongholds.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Total wipeout for DAP as Sabah voters send stinging rebuke, party acknowledges need for urgent reset|url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2025/11/29/total-wipeout-for-dap-as-sabah-voters-send-stinging-rebuke-party-acknowledges-need-for-urgent-reset/200147|website=Malay Mail|date=29 November 2025|access-date=1 December 2025|language=en|first=Julia|last=Chan}}</ref> As the party mainly relies on the vote of urban and semi-urban Chinese, the swing against it has been described as a "[[Political tsunami|Chinese tsunami]]".<ref>{{Cite web|title=Sabah polls' Chinese tsunami a symptom of DAP's failure to address local issues, says analyst|url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2025/11/30/sabah-polls039-chinese-tsunami-a-symptom-of-dap039s-failure-to-address-local-issues-says-analyst|website=[[The Star (Malaysia)|The Star]]|date=30 November 2025|access-date=1 December 2025|language=en}}</ref>
