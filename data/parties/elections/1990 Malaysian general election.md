General elections were held in [[Malaysia]] on 20 and 21 October 1990. Voting took place in all 180 parliamentary constituencies of Malaysia, each electing one Member of Parliament to the Dewan Rakyat, the dominant house of Parliament.<ref>[[Dieter Nohlen]], Florian Grotz & Christof Hartmann (2001) ''Elections in Asia: A data handbook, Volume II'', p152 {{ISBN|0-19-924959-8}}</ref> [[1990 Malaysian state elections|State elections]] also took place in 351 state constituencies in 11 (out of 13, except [[Sabah State Legislative Assembly|Sabah]] and [[Sarawak State Legislative Assembly|Sarawak]]) states of Malaysia on the same day.

The result was a victory for the [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN) at the federal level and state except state of [[Kelantan]] with opposition alliance Angkatan Perpaduan Ummah (APU) winning all 39 [[Kelantan State Legislative Assembly|state assembly seats]] with 24 seats going to PAS and 15 for Semangat 46.<ref>{{cite book|title=Democracy in Malaysia: Discourses and Practices|author1=Francis Kok-Wah Loh |author2=Francis Loh Kok Wah |author3=Boo Teik Khoo |author4=Khoo Boo Teik |publisher=[[Routledge]]|year=2002|isbn=0-7007-1161-9|page=95}}</ref>

==Background==
The elections marked the first after [[United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO) party split and the subsequent [[1988 Malaysian constitutional crisis|constitutional crisis]] in 1988. The reconstituted UMNO Baru (New UMNO), led by incumbent Prime Minister [[Mahathir Mohamad]], and the newly formed [[Parti Melayu Semangat 46|Semangat 46]] (S46), led by [[Tengku Razaleigh Hamzah]], contested for the first time in the elections.

It also marked the first time in country general election history when a credible, multi-ethnic coalition have been formed the challenge the dominance of [[Barisan Nasional]]. This also lead the country political scene from [[Dominant-party system|a dominant party system]] into [[Two-party system|two party system]].<ref>Khong, (1991) ''Malaysia's General Election 1990: Continuity, Change, and Ethnic Politics'', p1 {{ISBN|981-3035-77-3}}</ref> The Muslim opposition parties, [[Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party]] (PAS), Semangat 46, [[Barisan Jemaah Islamiah Se-Malaysia]] (BERJASA) and [[Parti Hizbul Muslimin Malaysia]] (HAMIM) teamed up to form the [[Angkatan Perpaduan Ummah]] (APU). On the other hand, Semangat 46, [[Democratic Action Party (Malaysia)|Democratic Action Party]] (DAP) and [[Parti Bersatu Sabah]] (PBS), which withdrew from the Barisan Nasional (BN) at the eleventh hour of the general election, teamed up as [[Gagasan Rakyat]]. However, these two opposition alliances cooperated in the election but not openly due to the sensitivity of the secular DAP and the [[Islamic]] PAS working together.
