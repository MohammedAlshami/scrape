The next general election in [[Malaysia]] is scheduled to be held by 17 February 2028. It will determine the composition of the [[Dewan Rakyat]], which determines the [[government of Malaysia]]. [[Redistribution (election)|Redistribution]] and boundary changes for the constituencies are expected to take place by 2026, with the last taking place before the [[2018 Malaysian general election|2018 general election]].<ref name="Election Commission can consider redelineation of boundaries after March 2026, says deputy minister">{{cite web|url=https://theedgemalaysia.com/node/775817|title=Election Commission can consider redelineation of boundaries after March 2026, says deputy minister|work=The Edge|date=29 October 2025|accessdate=18 December 2025}}</ref>

Prime Minister [[Anwar Ibrahim]], leader of [[Pakatan Harapan]], currently leads a coalition government consisting of [[Pakatan Harapan|PH]], [[Barisan Nasional|BN]], [[Gabungan Parti Sarawak|GPS]], [[Gabungan Rakyat Sabah|GRS]], [[Heritage Party (Malaysia)|WARISAN]] and other minor parties.<ref name="Parliament session on Dec 19, motion of confidence on PM to be tabled - PM Anwar">{{cite web|url=https://pru15.bernama.com/news-en.php?id=2142048|title=Parliament session on Dec 19, motion of confidence on PM to be tabled - PM Anwar|work=Bernama|date=24 November 2022|accessdate=15 December 2023|archive-date=25 November 2022|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221125095427/https://pru15.bernama.com/news-en.php?id=2142048|url-status=dead}}</ref> [[Perikatan Nasional]] (PN) and the [[Malaysian United Democratic Alliance]] (MUDA) sit as the opposition.<ref name="PN to stay as opposition bloc, to provide checks and balances to Anwar’s unity govt">{{cite web|url=https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/pas-to-discuss-with-pn-on-joining-anwar-ibrahim-s-unity-government|title=PN to stay as opposition bloc, to provide checks and balances to Anwar's unity govt|work=The Straits Times|date=25 November 2022|accessdate=15 December 2023}}</ref><ref name="Syed Saddiq: Muda turns opposition as ‘third force’, withdraws from unity govt">{{cite web|url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2023/09/10/syed-saddiq-muda-turns-opposition-as-third-force-withdraws-from-unity-govt/90067|title=Syed Saddiq: Muda turns opposition as 'third force', withdraws from unity govt|work=Malay Mail|date=10 September 2023 |accessdate=15 December 2023}}</ref>

==Electoral system==
{{Further|Elections in Malaysia}}
[[Elections in Malaysia]] are conducted at the federal and state levels. Federal elections elect members of the [[Dewan Rakyat]], the lower house of [[Parliament of Malaysia|Parliament]], while state elections in each of the 13 states elect members of their respective [[State legislative assemblies of Malaysia|state legislative assembly]]. As Malaysia follows the [[Westminster system]] of government, the [[head of government]] ([[Prime Minister of Malaysia|Prime Minister]] at the federal level and the [[Chief Ministers in Malaysia|Chief Ministers]], the so-called {{Em|Menteri Besar}}, at the state level) is the person who commands the confidence of the majority of members in the respective legislature – this is normally the leader of the party or coalition with the majority of seats in the legislature.

The Dewan Rakyat consists of 222 members, known as Members of Parliament (MPs), that are elected for five-year terms. Each MP is elected from a [[single-member district|single-member constituency]] using the [[first-past-the-post voting|first-past-the-post voting system]]. If one party obtains a majority of seats, then that party is entitled to form the government, with its leader becoming the Prime Minister. In the event of a [[hung parliament]], where no single party obtains the majority of seats, the government may still form through a [[coalition]] or a [[confidence and supply]] agreement with other parties. In practice, coalitions and alliances in Malaysia generally persist between elections, and member parties do not normally contest for the same seats.

The party commanding a majority support in the [[Dewan Rakyat]] is called upon by the [[Yang di-Pertuan Agong]] to form a government and present its nominee as [[Prime Minister of Malaysia]], whereas the largest party in the opposition bench nominates a candidate for [[Leader of the Opposition (Malaysia)|Leader of the Opposition]].

=== Main parties ===
The incumbent ruling [[Pakatan Harapan]] (PH) coalition had previously won the [[2018 Malaysian general election|2018 election]] prior to collapsing in the aftermath of the [[2020–2022 Malaysian political crisis|2020 political crisis]]. Following the [[2022 Malaysian general election|2022 election]] that resulted in the country's first [[hung parliament]], the [[multiracialism|multiracial]] and [[reformism|reformist]] coalition formed a negotiated [[Anwar Ibrahim cabinet|unity government]] under the leadership of longtime opposition leader [[Anwar Ibrahim]].<ref name="ISEAS_2020">{{cite web|last=Ostwald|first=Kai|title=Malaysia 2020: The Impasse of Two-Coalition Politics|url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/ISEAS_Perspective_2020_25.pdf|website=ISEAS - Yusof Ishak Institute|date=6 April 2020|access-date=14 December 2025}}</ref><ref name="AlJazeera_2022">{{cite web|title=No Clear Winner as Malaysia Election Ends in Hung Parliament|url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2022/11/19/tight-race-as-votes-counted-in-malaysias-in-general-election|website=AlJazeera|date=19 November 2022|access-date=14 December 2025}}</ref> [[Perikatan Nasional]] (PN) acts as the main opposition at the federal level following its loss in 2022 and the leadership's refusal to participate in the unity arrangement. The [[Far-right politics|far-right]] former ruling coalition was formed amid the 2020 crisis which led to the formation of a PN-led government under [[Muhyiddin Yassin]].<ref name="ISEAS_2020"/> [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN), the country's former ruling coalition of 61 years, continues to retain substantial support and kingmaker capacity. The coalition had undergone a pragmatic shift in the aftermath of the last election, participating in the unity government and allying itself with PH, its longtime historical rival, as a junior coalition partner. The two coalitions also planned an electoral pact for the upcoming election, intending to avoid seat overlaps while uniting their election machineries with the goal of maintaining the pre-election status quo.<ref name="Reuters_BN2022">{{cite web|title=Malaysia's Ruling Coalition Says It Accepts Election Results|url=https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/malaysias-ruling-coalition-says-it-accepts-election-results-2022-11-19/|website=Reuters|date=20 November 2022|access-date=14 December 2025}}</ref><ref name="ISEAS_MalayPolitics">{{cite web|last=Khoo|first=Boo Teik|title=Malay Politics and the Evolution of CoalitionBehaviour|url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2020/11/TRS17_20.pdf|website=ISEAS - Yusof Ishak Institute|access-date=14 December 2025}}</ref> Two allied and component parties of Pakatan Harapan in the previous election has ended their cooperation with PH for various reasons. [[Malaysian United Democratic Alliance|MUDA]] defected into opposition in 2023, citing No Further Action decision on Deputy Prime Minister [[Ahmad Zahid Hamidi|Zahid Hamidi]]'s corruption scandal as trigger,<ref>{{Cite web |last=KOSMO! |date=2023-09-10 |title=Muda umum keluar, bakal duduk di blok pembangkang |url=https://www.kosmo.com.my/2023/09/10/muda-umum-keluar-bakal-duduk-di-blok-pembangkang/ |access-date=2026-05-17 |website=Kosmo Digital |language=ms-MY}}</ref> while [[United Progressive Kinabalu Organisation|UPKO]] quit before the [[2025 Sabah state election|2025 Sabah election]] and decided to join GRS later, citing disagreement with federal government's reaction on court ruling on 40% oil revenue sharing promised to Sabah.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Yamin |first=Essa Abu |date=2025-11-10 |title=UPKO umum keluar PH |url=https://www.bharian.com.my/berita/nasional/2025/11/1470003/upko-umum-keluar-ph |access-date=2026-05-17 |website=Berita Harian |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=2026-05-29 |title=Upko applies to join GRS as component party |url=https://www.dailyexpress.com.my/news/282339/upko-applies-to-join-grs-as-component-party/ |access-date=2026-05-29 |website=www.dailyexpress.com.my |language=en}}</ref>

In addition to the major national coalitions, a number of regional parties and coalitions in [[East Malaysia]] are expected to be key players due to their potential as kingmakers in the post-election government formation. [[Gabungan Parti Sarawak]] (GPS), a [[Sarawak]]-based coalition formed by former BN components, presently dominates politics in Sarawak and commands a significant bloc of parliamentary seats. In [[Sabah]], the two largest parties are [[Gabungan Rakyat Sabah]] (GRS), the ruling state government, and [[Heritage Party (Malaysia)|Parti Warisan]] (WARISAN), the state's main opposition. GRS supports stronger federal-state cooperation and is seen as closer to the unity government,<ref name="GRS_NST">{{cite web|last=NST Staff |title=GRS victory reflects Sabah-centric politics|url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/nation/2025/12/1327416/grs-victory-reflects-sabah-centric-politics|website=New Straits Times|access-date=14 December 2025}}</ref> whereas WARISAN takes a more antagonistic stance against the federal government, utilising 'Sabah for Sabahan' rhetoric while simultaneously sitting in the government bench.<ref name="CNA_Warisan">{{cite web|title=Sabah election: Warisan increases its foothold|url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-sabah-elections-shafie-apdal-warisan-opposition-5498141|website=Channel NewsAsia|access-date=14 December 2025}}</ref> Within GRS, the election would be the first time its main component party [[Parti Gagasan Rakyat Sabah|GAGASAN]] contesting in a federal election after takeover by Chief Minister Hajiji's faction. The performances of these regional parties and their decisions to align with specific national coalitions is seen as key in determining the post-election government formation owing to the country's fragmented party system.

==Timeline==
=== Dissolution of parliament ===
The [[Constitution of Malaysia]] requires that a general election be held in the fifth calendar year after the first sitting unless it is dissolved earlier by the [[Yang di-Pertuan Agong]] following a [[motion of no confidence]], [[loss of supply]] or a request by the [[Prime Minister of Malaysia|prime minister]].

The [[15th Parliament of Malaysia]] first convened on 19 December 2022. If the term of the 15th Parliament reaches its maximum date, it will automatically dissolve on 19 December 2027, paving way for an election within 60 days. The latest date for the 16th General Election will therefore be on 17 February 2028.

===Dissolution of state legislatures===
While any state may dissolve its legislature independently of Parliament, most of them had historically dissolved at around the same time as Parliament such that federal and state elections are held simultaneously. In accordance with Malaysian law, [[Parliament of Malaysia|Parliament]] as well as the [[State legislative assemblies of Malaysia|legislative assemblies of each state]] would automatically expire on the fifth anniversary of the first sitting of a term, unless dissolved prior to that date by the relevant [[State governments of Malaysia#Heads of state|heads of state]] on the advice of their respective [[Chief Ministers in Malaysia|heads of government]]. Elections must be held within sixty days of expiry or dissolution.
