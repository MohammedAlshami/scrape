General elections were held in Malaysia on Saturday, 25 April 1964, to elect members of the expanded [[Dewan Rakyat]], the [[lower house]] of the [[bicameral]] [[Parliament of Malaysia]]. These were the first federal elections since the [[Malaysia Agreement]] of 1963, which transformed the [[Federation of Malaya]] into Malaysia with the addition of [[State of Singapore (Malaysia)|Singapore]], [[Sabah]] and [[Sarawak]]. However, general elections were not held in these new states at the time. Voting took place in 104 of the 159 parliamentary constituencies, each returning one Member of Parliament (MP).<ref name="NohlenGrotz2001">{{cite book|author1=Dieter Nohlen|author2=Florian Grotz|author3=Christof Hartmann|title=Elections in Asia and the Pacific: A Data Handbook: Volume II: South East Asia, East Asia, and the South Pacific|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ceNmuQEACAAJ|date=15 November 2001|publisher=OUP Oxford|isbn=978-0-19-924959-6|page=152}}</ref> [[1964 Malaysian state elections|State elections]] were also held on the same day in 282 state constituencies across 11 of Malaysia's 14 states, each electing one Member of the [[State legislative assemblies of Malaysia|Legislative Assembly]] (MLA).

The elections resulted in a victory for the [[Barisan Nasional|Alliance Party]], which secured 89 of the 104 contested seats with a voter turnout of 80 percent. Two Alliance candidates were returned [[uncontested election|uncontested]]. The outcome is regarded as one of the factors that contributed to the eventual [[independence of Singapore|expulsion of Singapore]] from [[Malaysia]]. The Singapore-based [[People's Action Party]] (PAP) had contested seats in [[Peninsular Malaysia|the peninsula]] in response to the [[United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO) participating in the [[1963 Singaporean general election]], in breach of a prior agreement not to do so. Although the PAP drew large crowds at its rallies, it only won one seat out of nine contested, with [[Devan Nair]] elected at [[Bangsar (federal constituency)|Bangsar]].<ref>{{cite web |last1=Thor |first1=Venessa |title=Flashback Friday: PAP wins 1 seat in Malaysian General Election on April 25, 1964 |url=https://www.straitstimes.com/singapore/flashback-friday-pap-wins-1-seat-in-malaysian-general-election-on-april-25-1964 |website=The Straits Times |access-date=16 September 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210116155801/https://www.straitstimes.com/singapore/flashback-friday-pap-wins-1-seat-in-malaysian-general-election-on-april-25-1964 |archive-date=16 January 2021 |language=en |date=25 April 2014}}</ref> Some historians attribute the results to the [[Malayan Chinese Association]] (MCA) retaining its position as the "undisputed leader of the Chinese in the Malayan peninsula" to then [[Minister of Finance (Malaysia)|Minister of Finance]] and MCA President [[Tan Siew Sin]]'s appeal to the [[Chinese Malaysians|ethnic Chinese]] community to refrain from "challenging [[Ketuanan Melayu|Malay special rights]]" and risking closer alignment with [[Indonesia]]. Nevertheless, Alliance leaders, particularly within UMNO and the MCA, were angered by the PAP's actions and regarded the party, as well as [[Lee Kuan Yew]]'s personal appeal among voters, as a threat to their political dominance.

As the first parliamentary general election held after the formation of Malaysia in 1963, no elections took place in [[State of Singapore (Malaysia)|Singapore]], [[Sabah]] or [[Sarawak]]. Under transitional provisions, voters of these three states are to select their parliamentary representatives at the next election. Together, the three states were allocated 55 seats – 15 for Singapore, 16 for Sabah and 24 for Sarawak – representing 35 percent of the 159 seats in Parliament. This allocation was intended as a [[check and balance]] to prevent the passage of [[constitutional amendment]]s, which required a [[two-thirds majority]], without the consent of representatives from the new states. However, following Singapore's expulsion from Malaysia, Sabah and Sarawak together held only 28 percent of parliamentary seats (40 out of 144), reducing their ability to block legislation that eroded the special rights granted to them as equal partners at the formation of Malaysia. The share fell further to 25 percent after the [[1974 Malaysian general election|1974 general election]] and, despite increases in the total number of seats over the years, has remained nearly constant since.<ref>{{cite web |last1=Haziq Mahmud |first1=Aqil |last2=S Bedi |first2=Rashvinjeet |title=IN FOCUS: Push for greater autonomy by Sabah and Sarawak is stronger than ever, but will they finally succeed? |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-sabah-sarawak-autonomy-ma63-anwar-4595616 |website=CNA |access-date=16 September 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20240914005429/https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-sabah-sarawak-autonomy-ma63-anwar-4595616 |archive-date=14 September 2024 |language=en}}</ref>

==Background==
In the lead-up to the election, Malaysia was still grappling with the political implications of its [[Malaysia Agreement|recent formation in September 1963]]. The merger of [[Federation of Malaya|Malaya]] with [[State of Singapore|Singapore]], [[Sabah]] and [[Sarawak]] introduced complex federal dynamics and heightened ethnic sensitivities, particularly among [[Early Malay nationalism|Malay nationalists]] concerned about the demographic impact of Singapore's predominantly Chinese population. These anxieties were intensified by perceptions that the [[People's Action Party]] (PAP), led by [[prime minister of Singapore]] [[Lee Kuan Yew]], sought to extend its influence into [[Peninsular Malaysia]]. [[United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO) leaders, notably [[prime Minister of Malaysia]] [[Tunku Abdul Rahman]], viewed this as a direct challenge to [[Ketuanan Melayu|Malay political supremacy]] within the federation.<ref name="Means1970">{{cite book |last=Means |first=Gordon P. |title=Malaysian Politics |publisher=New York University Press |year=1970 |pages=231–234 |isbn=9780814704691}}</ref>

The political atmosphere was increasingly polarised along communal lines. The ruling [[Alliance Party (Malaysia)|Alliance]] coalition, although formally multiethnic, was dominated by UMNO and reliant on a formula of ethnic power-sharing that prioritised Malay political leadership. In contrast, opposition parties advanced varied critiques of the Alliance's race-based policies, with Islamist groups appealing to conservative Malay-Muslim sentiment, while leftist movements emphasised class-based inequality and opposed what they perceived as the consolidation of elite interests under the Alliance.<ref name="Vasil1971">{{cite book |last=Vasil |first=R. K. |title=Politics in a Plural Society: A Study of Non-Communal Political Parties in West Malaysia |publisher=Oxford University Press |location=Kuala Lumpur |year=1971 |pages=114–118 |isbn=9780196381275}}</ref> Tensions were further heightened by the ''[[Konfrontasi]]'' with [[Indonesia]], which saw President [[Sukarno]] opposing the formation of Malaysia as a neo-colonial project. This external threat led the Alliance to campaign on a platform of national unity and [[anti-communism]], portraying dissenting voices as threats to stability. The [[Internal Security Act 1960|Internal Security Act]] (ISA) was used to detain political opponents, curtailing the activities of some left-wing and labour-aligned groups.<ref name="Cheah2002">{{cite book |last=Cheah |first=Boon Kheng |title=Malaysia: The Making of a Nation |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |location=Singapore |year=2002 |pages=133–137 |isbn=9789812301543}}</ref>
