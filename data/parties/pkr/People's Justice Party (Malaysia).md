{{Politics of Malaysia}}

The '''People's Justice Party''' ({{langx|ms|Parti Keadilan Rakyat}}, '''PKR''' or '''KEADILAN''')<ref name="Perlembagaan2">{{cite web | title=Perlembagaan Parti Keadilan Rakyat | publisher=Parti Keadilan Rakyat | date=11 March 2025 | url=https://keadilanrakyat.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/PERLEMBAGAAN-PARTI_11-MAC-2025.pdf | language=ms | access-date=11 March 2026}}</ref> is a [[Reformism|reformist]] political party in [[Malaysia]]. It was founded in 2003 through the merger of its predecessor, the [[National Justice Party (Malaysia)|National Justice Party]] (KeADILan), with the socialist [[Malaysian People's Party]] (PRM).<ref name="KaySuhaimi2">{{Cite web | last=Suhaimi | first=Kay | url=https://iluminasi.com/bm/sejarah-penubuhan-parti-keadilan-rakyat-dan-pakatan-harapan.html|title=Sejarah Penubuhan Parti Keadilan Rakyat dan Pakatan Harapan|language=ms|publisher=Iluminasi | date=4 May 2018 | access-date=11 May 2019 | archive-date=26 June 2019 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190626132954/https://iluminasi.com/bm/sejarah-penubuhan-parti-keadilan-rakyat-dan-pakatan-harapan.html | url-status=live}}</ref> The party's predecessor was founded in 1999 by [[Wan Azizah Wan Ismail]] during the height of the [[Reformasi (Malaysia)|Reformasi]] movement triggered by the [[Anwar Ibrahim sodomy trials#1998 allegation|imprisonment of her husband]], former Deputy Prime Minister [[Anwar Ibrahim]]. Following the [[2022 Malaysian general election|2022 election]] that resulted in a [[hung parliament]], the party governs Malaysia through a [[Anwar Ibrahim cabinet|unity government]] led by Anwar Ibrahim as the tenth [[Prime Minister of Malaysia]].

In the first [[Elections in Malaysia|general election]] of its predecessor in [[1999 Malaysian general election|1999]], the party won five seats in the [[Dewan Rakyat]].<ref name="IPU19992">{{cite web|title=Malaysia: parliamentary elections Dewan Rakyat, 1999|url=https://data.ipu.org/election-summary/HTML/2197_99.htm|website=IPU |access-date=20 November 2025}}</ref> A resurgence by the ruling [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN) in [[2004 Malaysian general election|2004]] reduced PKR to a single seat in the traditional stronghold of [[Permatang Pauh (federal constituency)|Permatang Pauh]].<ref name="Aliran20042">{{cite web | last=Loh | first=Francis | title=Malaysia: Understanding the 2004 Election Results |url=https://m.aliran.com/archives/monthly/2004a/3g.html | website=Aliran | date=March 2004 | access-date=20 November 2025}}</ref><ref name="IFES20042">{{cite web|title=IFES Election Guide – Malaysian House of Representatives 2004 |url=https://www.electionguide.org/elections/id/325/|website=IFES|access-date=20 November 2025}}</ref> The [[2008 Malaysian general election|2008 election]] produced a substantial swing to the opposition, with PKR increasing its representation to 31 seats and forming the government in five states.<ref name="ISEAS20242">{{cite web |last=Chai|first=James|title=A Deep Dive into Malaysia's People's Justice Party (PKR) |url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/articles-commentaries/iseas-perspective/2024-21-a-deep-dive-into-malaysias-peoples-justice-party-pkr-by-james-chai/|website=ISEAS‑Yusof Ishak Institute |date=2024 |access-date=20 November 2025}}</ref><ref name="Moten20092">{{cite journal |last=Moten|first=A. R.|title=2008 General Elections in Malaysia: Democracy at Work |journal=Japanese Journal of Political Science |year=2009 |volume=10 |pages=21–42 |doi=10.1017/S1468109908003356 |url=https://doi.org/10.1017/S1468109908003356|access-date=20 November 2025|url-access=subscription }}</ref> The 2008 results also precipitated the resignation of Prime Minister [[Abdullah Ahmad Badawi]] and the lifting of Anwar Ibrahim's five-year political ban on 14 April 2008.<ref name="Chin20232">{{cite journal | last=Chin | first=James |title=Anwar's long walk to power: the 2022 Malaysian general election | journal=Third World Quarterly | volume=112 | pages=1–13 | doi=10.1080/00358533.2023.2165303 | url=https://doi.org/10.1080/00358533.2023.2165303 | date=14 February 2023 | access-date=20 November 2025 | doi-access=free}}</ref>

The [[Pakatan Harapan]] (PH) coalition, of which PKR is a founding and principal member, defeated Barisan Nasional at the [[2018 Malaysian general election|2018 general election]], ending BN's uninterrupted 60-year rule since independence. However, defections from the faction aligned to [[Azmin Ali]] within the party and the withdrawal of [[Malaysian United Indigenous Party|BERSATU]] triggered the collapse of the PH government after just 22 months, leading to the [[2020–2022 Malaysian political crisis|2020–2022 political crisis]].<ref name="Ufen20222">{{cite pdf | last=Ufen | first=Andreas | title=The Downfall of Pakatan Harapan | url=https://www.ssoar.info/ssoar/bitstream/document/80500/1/ssoar-asiansur-2022-2-ufen-The_Downfall_of_Pakatan_Harapan.pdf | year=2022 | access-date=20 November 2025}}</ref> The party returned to government after the [[2022 Malaysian general election|2022 general election]] which resulted in Malaysia's first hung parliament. A unity government arrangement between Pakatan Harapan, former rivals Barisan Nasional, and several [[East Malaysia|Borneo]]-based parties enabled the formation of a federal government led by Anwar Ibrahim.<ref name="AlJazeera20222">{{cite web | title=No clear winner as Malaysia election ends in hung parliament | url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2022/11/19/tight-race-as-votes-counted-in-malaysias-in-general-election | website=Al Jazeera | date=19 November 2022 | access-date=20 November 2025}}</ref>

The [[Multiracialism|multiracial]] party advocates for [[Reformism|institutional reform]] and [[economic justice]], drawing its strongest support from urban and semi-urban constituencies, particularly in the states of [[Selangor]], [[Penang]], [[Perak]], [[Negeri Sembilan]], [[Johor]], and the capital city of [[Kuala Lumpur]]. The party's platform emphasises Reformasi-era priorities such as anti-corruption measures and social justice.<ref name="ISEAS20242" />

== History ==
=== Origins ===
{{See also|Anwar Ibrahim sodomy trials|Reformasi (Malaysia)}}
[[File:2025_Anwar_Ibrahim_(cropped).jpg|thumb|266x266px|Anwar Ibrahim, founder and leader of the party]]
The [[economy of Malaysia]] had been badly affected by the [[1997 Asian financial crisis]].<ref>{{cite web | last=Wong | first=Chin Huat | url=https://www.malaysianbar.org.my/article/news/legal-and-general-news/general-news/splits-in-umno-and-opposition-unity | title=Splits in Umno and Opposition unity | work=[[The Sun (Malaysia)|The Sun]] | date=17 August 2007 | access-date=29 September 2021 | via=[[Malaysian Bar]] | archive-date=28 September 2021 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210928181719/https://www.malaysianbar.org.my/article/news/legal-and-general-news/general-news/splits-in-umno-and-opposition-unity | url-status=live}}</ref> In response, [[Deputy Prime Minister of Malaysia|Deputy Prime Minister]] [[Anwar Ibrahim]] instituted a series of economic reforms and [[Austerity|austerity measures]] under his jurisdiction as [[Minister of Finance (Malaysia)|Minister of Finance]]. The package included reducing government spending by 18%, cutting ministerial salaries, and deferring major projects despite it being the cornerstone development strategy of the [[Fifth Mahathir cabinet|Mahathir government]]. The reforms were seen as challenging the administration's policies, and the situation was later exacerbated when he tabled controversial amendments to the [[Prevention of Corruption Act#Malaysia|Prevention of Corruption Act]] that sought to increase the powers of the [[Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission|Anti-Corruption Agency]] (ACA).<ref>{{cite web | last=Lim | first=Kit Siang | url=http://www.limkitsiang.com/archive/1999/Oct99/sg2012.htm | title=Media statement by Lim Kit Siang | publisher=Democratic Action Party | year=1999 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20061108230207/http://www.limkitsiang.com/archive/1999/Oct99/sg2012.htm | archive-date=8 November 2006 | url-status=dead | author-link=Lim Kit Siang}}</ref> [[Prime Minister of Malaysia|Prime Minister]] [[Mahathir Mohamad]] strongly disagreed with these measures, and towards the end of 1998, their relationship began to heavily deteriorate, triggered by their conflicting views on governance. Eventually, this culminated in Mahathir dismissing Anwar from his cabinet positions on 2 September 1998, before expelling Anwar from [[UMNO]] the day after. This was despite Anwar being his protégé and having deep influence within both the party and government.<ref>{{cite web | last=Symonds | first=Peter | url=https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/1998/10/mal-o03.html|title=Behind the sacking and arrest of Anwar Ibrahim | publisher=World Socialist Web Site | date=3 October 1998 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180526075734/https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/1998/10/mal-o03.html | archive-date=26 May 2018 | url-status=dead}}</ref>

Anwar Ibrahim and sixteen of his political associates were later arrested between 20 and 29 September 1998 under the [[Internal Security Act 1960|Internal Security Act]] (ISA).<ref name="Amnesty2">{{cite web | url=https://www.amnesty.org/en/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/asa280271998en.pdf | title=Malaysia: The arrest of Anwar Ibrahim and his political associates | publisher=Amnesty International | date=3 October 1998 | access-date=26 May 2018 | language=en | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20060607215526/http://web.amnesty.org/library/Index/ENGASA280271998?open&of=ENG-381 | archive-date=7 June 2006}}</ref> [[Amnesty International]] designated the individuals in detention as "prisoners of conscience", stating they were held solely for non-violent political activities. Anwar later appeared in court with visible injuries after nine days in custody, raising concerns of [[prisoner abuse]] and [[Enforced disappearance|incommunicado detention]]. [[Human rights|Human rights organisations]] criticised the use of the ISA to detain political opponents, as well as the violent dispersal of peaceful pro-reform demonstrations and broader restrictions on freedom of assembly and expression by the government. This incident and the circumstances in which it happened led to a public outcry in what became known as the ''[[Reformasi (Malaysia)|Reformasi]]'' movement, but it also resulted in the arrest and subsequent [[detention without trial]] of Anwar on what many believed to be politically motivated [[Anwar Ibrahim sodomy trials|charges]] of [[sexual misconduct]] and [[corruption]].<ref name="Amnesty2" /> The movement, which began as the country [[1998 Commonwealth Games|hosted the 1998 Commonwealth Games]], initially demanded the resignation of Mahathir Mohamad and for the end of alleged [[corruption]] and [[cronyism]] within the [[Barisan Nasional]]-led (BN) government. It would go on to become a [[Reformism|reform movement]] demanding [[social equality]] and [[social justice]] in Malaysia. The movement consisted of [[civil disobedience]], [[Demonstration (protest)|demonstrations]], [[Sit-in|sit-ins]], [[rioting]], [[Occupation (protest)|occupations]] and [[internet activism]].<ref name="KaySuhaimi2" />

=== Founding and early history (1998–1999) ===
Despite Anwar being detained, the ''Reformasi movement'' continued to develop, with  "Justice for Anwar" remaining a potent rallying call. Before his arrest, Anwar had designated his wife, [[Wan Azizah Wan Ismail]], as the successor of the movement.  Wan Azizah went on to develop an enormous following, attracting thousands to her speeches. For a time, these followers held massive weekend street demonstrations, mostly in [[Kuala Lumpur]] but also occasionally in [[Penang]] and other cities, demanding ''"keadilan"'' (justice) and Mahathir's resignation. During Anwar's police custody in September 1998, he was beaten by the [[Inspector-General of Police (Malaysia)|Inspector-General of Police]], [[Abdul Rahim Mohd Noor]], causing the infamous [[black eye]]. The imagery of the black eye was adopted as a rallying symbol of demanded justice by his followers and eventually became the party flag—jocularly known as the ''Bendera Mata Lebam'' ("Black Eye Flag")—designed by artist [[:ms:Syed Ahmad Syed Jamal|Syed Ahmad Syed Jamal]].<ref>{{cite news | last=Khairuddin | first=Nur Hanim | url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/172157 | title=Dari '?' kepada pencipta logo Keadilan | language=ms | access-date=24 October 2025 | work=Malaysiakini | date=6 August 2011}}</ref>

Building on the momentum of ''Reformasi'', a political movement called the Social Justice Movement ({{langx|ms|Pergerakan Keadilan Sosial}}), also known as ADIL, was launched on 10 December 1998 and was led by Wan Azizah.<ref>{{Cite web | url=http://mgrnk.tripod.com/index2.html | title=Pergerakan Keadilan Sosial (ADIL) | language=ms | publisher=Pergerakan Keadilan Sosial (ADIL) | date=10 December 1998 | access-date=11 May 2019 | archive-date=27 June 2019 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190627122406/http://mgrnk.tripod.com/index2.html | url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite news | last=Mahmud | first= Zin | url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/opinion/2018/02/06/di-sebalik-harapan-rakyat-kepada-pkr/ | title=Di sebalik harapan rakyat kepada PKR | language=ms | publisher=[[Free Malaysia Today]] | date=6 February 2018 | access-date=11 May 2019 | archive-date=26 June 2019 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190626134455/https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/opinion/2018/02/06/di-sebalik-harapan-rakyat-kepada-pkr/ | url-status=dead}}</ref> However, facing difficulties in registering ADIL as a political party, the Reformasi movement instead merged with the [[Ikatan Masyarakat Islam Malaysia|Muslim Community Union of Malaysia]] ({{langx|ms|Ikatan Masyarakat Islam Malaysia}}), a minor [[Islamism|Islamic]] political party based in [[Terengganu]], and relaunched it as the [[National Justice Party (Malaysia)|National Justice Party]] ({{langx|ms|[[Parti Keadilan Nasional]]}}), also known as PKN or KeADILan, on 4 April 1999. The registration was just in time for the new party to take part in the [[Malaysian general election, 1999|1999 general election]].<ref>{{cite web | last=Aris | first=Azam | url=http://www.indianmalaysian.com/sound/modules.php?name=News&file=print&sid=1195 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110713025141/http://www.indianmalaysian.com/sound/modules.php?name=News&file=print&sid=1195 | url-status=dead | archive-date=13 July 2011 | title=PKR's watershed election | publisher=Indian Malaysian Online | date=26 February 2008 | access-date=26 May 2018}}</ref> The launch of KeADILan put to rest months of speculation about whether Wan Azizah and Anwar would continute remaining in ADIL, join the [[Malaysian Islamic Party|Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party]] (PAS), or attempt an internal coup within UMNO. Although KeADILan was multiracial, its primary target was middle-class, middle-of-the-road Malays, particularly from UMNO. The party has been noted as having rough similarities with the now-defunct social democratic [[Parti Keadilan Masyarakat Malaysia]] (PEKEMAS).<ref>{{cite web | last=Jasin | first=Abdul Kadir | url=http://www.agendadaily.com/A-Kadir-Jasin-Other-Thots/a-cautionary-tale-of-two-coalitions.html | title=A cautionary tale of two coalitions | publisher=Agenda Daily | date=21 January 2011 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-date=6 January 2016 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160106094034/http://www.agendadaily.com/A-Kadir-Jasin-Other-Thots/a-cautionary-tale-of-two-coalitions.html | url-status=dead}}</ref>

=== Barisan Alternatif era (1999–2004) ===
On 24 October 1999, the party joined together with the [[Democratic Action Party (Malaysia)|Democratic Action Party]] (DAP), the [[Malaysian People's Party]] (PRM), and PAS in a big tent alliance of [[Liberalism|liberals]], [[Socialism|socialists]], and [[Islamism|Islamists]], known as [[Barisan Alternatif]] (BA) to take on the ruling [[Barisan Nasional]] (BN) coalition in the 1999 general election.<ref>{{Cite web | last=Nasir | first=Lyn | url=http://www.keadilandaily.com/selepas-15-tahun-keadilan-kini-bertambah-kuat/ | title=Selepas 15 tahun, KEADILAN kini bertambah kuat | language=ms | publisher=Keadilan Daily | date=4 April 2014 | access-date=11 May 2019 | archive-date=26 June 2019 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190626132957/http://www.keadilandaily.com/selepas-15-tahun-keadilan-kini-bertambah-kuat/ | url-status=live}}</ref>

While parliament's term was set to end in June the next year, an early election was called in November under the pretext of avoiding "undesirable" behaviour during the Islamic holy month of [[Ramadan]] in December. International media speculated that Mahathir wanted an early election to be held before some 650,000 new voters became eligible to participate.<ref>{{Cite web |date=10 November 1999 |title=Mahathir explains snap election |url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/world/monitoring/514574.stm |access-date=16 November 2025 |website=[[BBC News]]}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last=B. Richburg |first=Keith |date=11 November 1999 |title=Malaysian Premier Calls for New Elections |url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/politics/1999/11/11/malaysian-premier-calls-for-new-elections/1 |access-date=16 November 2025 |newspaper=[[The Washington Post]]}}</ref> In the lead-up to the election, seven activists, including Keadilan leaders; Vice-president [[Tian Chua]], [[Gobalakrishnan Nagapan]], Youth leader [[Mohamad Ezam Mohd Nor|Ezam Mohd Nor]], Fairus Izuddin and Dr Badrul Amin Baharun; were arrested between 27 and 30 September and as a result prevented from contesting.<ref>{{cite web | last=Loh | first=Francis |date=22 September 1999 | title=The Rakyat have Awakened and They want Justice | url=https://aliran.com/oldsite/ms990922.html | url-status=live | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190515191109/https://aliran.com/oldsite/ms990922.html | archive-date=15 May 2019 | access-date=26 May 2018 | publisher=Aliran Media}}</ref> Further arrests were made on 10 April 2001 and those arrested were subsequently charged and incarcerated under the [[Internal Security Act (Malaysia)|Internal Security Act]].<ref>{{cite web | date=12 April 2001 | title=Malaysia: Fear of torture or ill-treatment / incommunicado detention / prisoners of conscience | url=http://web.amnesty.org/library/Index/ENGASA280062001?open&of=ENG-MYS | url-status=dead | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20061027033717/http://web.amnesty.org/library/Index/ENGASA280062001?open&of=ENG-MYS | archive-date=27 October 2006 | access-date=26 May 2018 | publisher=Amnesty International}}</ref> They became known as the '''Reformasi 10'''.<ref>{{cite web | year=2004 | title=II. Background: The ISA in Law and Practice | url=https://www.hrw.org/reports/2004/malaysia0504/2.htm | url-status=live | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160305200155/https://www.hrw.org/reports/2004/malaysia0504/2.htm | archive-date=5 March 2016 |  access-date=26 May 2018 | publisher=Human Rights Watch}}</ref>

With parliament dissolving on 11 November, parties were only left with 9 days' time to campaign between candidate nominations on 20 November and voting day on 29 November. The short campaign period drew criticism from the opposition, and the party entered the campaign with many of its key leaders under arrest. It also had to contend with the distribution of pornographic videocassettes implicating Anwar in the villages, as well as a lack of access to written and audiovisual media; the government denied opposition parties access to state-run public broadcaster [[Radio Televisyen Malaysia]].<ref>{{Cite web | last=Ingram | first=Simon | date=16 November 1999 | title=Malaysia's much-maligned media | url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/522848.stm | access-date=16 November 2025 | website=[[BBC News]]}}</ref><ref name="IPU19992" /> Thus, despite the party winning 11.67% of the total votes cast, it only managed to win five parliamentary seats. Notably, Wan Azizah managed to secure victory in [[Permatang Pauh (federal constituency)|Permatang Pauh]]; the seat formerly held by her husband, with a majority of 9,077 votes. The Barisan Alternatif as a whole gained 40.21% of the total votes cast, with PAS winning 27 seats and DAP winning ten seats. The big opposition winner was PAS, which gained 20 seats as well as a majority in two [[State legislative assemblies of Malaysia|state assemblies]] in [[Kelantan State Legislative Assembly|Kelantan]] and [[Terengganu State Legislative Assembly|Terengganu]].

For the first time in Malaysia's history, UMNO, the dominant Malay-based party which had ruled the country for 40 years since independence, received less than half of the total vote of ethnic Malays. Despite losing 14 seats, two of which belonged to federal ministers, the BN coalition still secured a two-thirds majority of 148 seats.

The post election period saw negotiations between Keadilan and [[Parti Rakyat Malaysia]] (PRM) on a possible merger.<ref>{{cite web | === Electoral setback and merger (2004–2008) ===
[[File:Anwar_Ibrahim_speaking.jpg|left|thumb|Anwar Ibrahim speaking in 2005]]
As the new amendments to the party constitution had yet to be approved by the Registrar of Societies, candidates from PRM contested the [[2004 Malaysian general election|2004 general election]] using the symbol of the old National Justice Party.<ref>{{cite web | last=Yap | first=Mun Ching | url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/18439 | title=PRM to contest elections under allys symbol | publisher=Malaysiakini | date=20 January 2004 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-date=12 May 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180512113108/https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/18439 | url-status=live}}</ref> The party fared poorly in the elections and only managed to retain one parliamentary seat, [[Permatang Pauh (federal constituency)|Permatang Pauh]], despite winning 9% of the popular vote. The poor showing was later attributed to [[malapportionment]] and [[gerrymandering]] in the redelineation of constituencies, with one estimate suggesting that on average, a vote for the BN government was worth 28 times the vote of a KeADILan supporter.<ref>{{cite web | last=Surin | first=Jacqueline Ann | url=http://malaysia-today.net/blog2006/newsncom.php?itemid=2028 | title=22/01: German electoral system more democratic | work=The Sun | publisher=Malaysia Today | date=6 June 2005 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071218201013/http://malaysia-today.net/blog2006/newsncom.php?itemid=2028 | archive-date=18 December 2007 | url-status=dead}}</ref>{{unreliable source?|date=May 2018}}

On 2 September 2004, the Federal Court overturned Anwar's sodomy conviction. As he had already served the entirety of his sentence for his corruption conviction, he was set free.<ref>{{Cite news | date=2 September 2004 |title=Malaysia's Anwar Ibrahim set free | url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/3619790.stm | access-date=16 November 2025 | work=[[BBC News]] | language=en-GB}}</ref> This unexpected turn of events came timely for PKR which was facing flagging morale due to its dismal performance in the elections.

In December 2005, PKR organised its second national congress.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.malaysia-today.net/PKR/2005/11/keadilan-akan-adakan-kongres-kedua.htm | title=Keadilan akan adakan kongres kedua | language=ms | publisher=Malaysia Today | date=2 November 2005 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071218193945/http://www.malaysia-today.net/PKR/2005/11/keadilan-akan-adakan-kongres-kedua.htm | archive-date=18 December 2007 | url-status=dead}}</ref> Among the motions passed was the New Economic Agenda<ref>{{cite web | first=Anwar | last=Ibrahim | url=http://www.jahilgoblog.net/image/mea.pdf | title=New Economic Agenda | publisher=jahilgoblog.net | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201025195423/http://www.jahilgoblog.net/image/mea.pdf | archive-date=25 October 2020}}</ref> that envisioned a non-racial economic policy to replace the race-based [[Malaysian New Economic Policy|New Economic Policy]]. PKR managed a breakthrough into [[Sarawak]] politics in May 2006, a state traditionally known as a BN stronghold. In that year's [[Sarawak state election, 2006|Sarawak state elections]], [[Dominique Ng Kim Ho]], a lawyer and activist, won in the [[Padungan (state constituency)|Padungan]] constituency in [[Kuching]], a majority Chinese locale. The party narrowly lost in [[Saribas (state constituency)|Saribas]], a [[Malaysian Malays|Malay]]-[[Melanau]] constituency, by just 94 votes. Afterwards, PKR pursued an aggressive strategy of attracting personalities from within and outside politics. In July 2006, [[Khalid Ibrahim]], a former CEO of [[Permodalan Nasional Berhad]] and [[Guthrie (Malaysia)|Guthrie]], was appointed as treasurer of the party.

=== Pakatan Rakyat era (2008–2015) ===
In the [[2008 Malaysian general election|2008 elections]], PKR contested alongside DAP and PAS in a new coalition known as [[Pakatan Rakyat]] (PR). The party won 31 seats in parliament, while DAP and PAS won 28 and 23 seats respectively. The 82 seats won by the coalition denied BN a two-thirds majority against a backdrop of rises in inflation, crime, and ethnic tension.<ref>{{cite web | url=https://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/7284682.stm | title=Election setback for Malaysia PM | work=BBC News | date=8 March 2008 | access-date=11 July 2011 | archive-date=13 March 2013 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130313215030/http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/7284682.stm | url-status=live}}</ref>

PKR also successfully contested the state legislative elections which saw the loose coalition of PKR, DAP and PAS forming coalition governments in the states of [[Kelantan]], [[Kedah]], [[Penang]], [[Perak]] and [[Selangor]]. The offices of the [[Menteri Besar]] of [[Selangor]] and the Deputy [[Chief Minister]] of [[Penang]] were held by PKR members, [[Khalid Ibrahim]] and [[:ms:Mohammad Fairus Khairuddin|Fairus Khairuddin]], respectively.

Anwar's five-year ban from contesting elections, imposed on anyone sentenced to longer than a year in jail, ended on 14 April 2008.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://afp.google.com/article/ALeqM5hFSMeru-BqR2BIAOAQY_ul-3Klhg | title=Malaysian opposition leader Anwar marks end of political ban | work=Agence France-Presse | date=12 April 2008 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121002110627/http://afp.google.com/article/ALeqM5hFSMeru-BqR2BIAOAQY_ul-3Klhg | archive-date=2 October 2012 | url-status=dead}}</ref> A gathering held by supporters celebrating the end of the ban was dispersed by police.<ref>{{Cite news | date=15 April 2008 | title=Anwar 'could topple Malaysia PM' | url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/7345790.stm | access-date=16 November 2025 | work=[[BBC News]] | language=en-GB}}</ref> Despite the ban, Anwar continued being widely seen as PKR's de facto leader throughout his imprisonment.<ref>{{Cite web | last=Lim | first=Ida | date=27 July 2020 | title=Ahead of Najib's SRC decision, lawyers explain what happens to MPs found guilty of criminal charges | url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2020/07/27/ahead-of-najibs-src-decision-lawyers-explain-what-happens-to-mps-found-guil/1888338 | access-date=16 November 2025 | website=[[Malay Mail]] | language=en}}</ref>

Anwar returned to parliament on 28 August 2008 following a landslide victory in the [[2008 Permatang Pauh by-election]], triggered by Wan Azizah's resignation.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.thestandard.com.hk/breaking_news_detail.asp?id=4470&icid=4&d_str=20080826 | title=Malaysian government declares by-election defeat to Anwar | work=Agence France-Presse | publisher=The Standard (Hong Kong) | date=26 August 2008 | access-date=11 July 2011 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20111220200743/http://www.thestandard.com.hk/breaking_news_detail.asp?id=4470&icid=4&d_str=20080826 | archive-date=20 December 2011 | url-status=dead}}</ref><ref name="landslide vote2">{{cite web | date=26 August 2008 | title=Anwar Ibrahim wins landslide vote | url=https://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/7581446.stm | url-status=live | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110511111215/http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/7581446.stm | archive-date=11 May 2011 | access-date=11 July 2011 | work=BBC News}}</ref> He was quoted as saying, "I'm glad to be back after a decade. The prime minister has lost the mandate of the country and the nation",<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.cnn.com/2008/WORLD/asiapcf/08/27/malaysia.anwar.ap/ | title=Anwar sworn in as member of Malaysian parliament | publisher=CNN | date=28 August 2008 | access-date=11 July 2011 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080924043225/http://www.cnn.com/2008/WORLD/asiapcf/08/27/malaysia.anwar.ap/|archive-date=24 September 2008 | url-status=dead}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | first=Faisal | last=Aziz | url=https://in.reuters.com/article/worldNews/idINIndia-35210120080828 | title=NEWSMAKER – Malaysia Anwar sworn in, ends political exile | publisher=Reuters India | date=28 August 2008 | access-date=11 July 2011 | archive-date=12 January 2009 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090112025057/http://in.reuters.com/article/worldNews/idINIndia-35210120080828 | url-status=dead}}</ref> Despite the claim, Anwar would need at least 30 defections from government lawmakers to form a majority.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://afp.google.com/article/ALeqM5jl2uOGMOucxq_mE5rD3BAogvtmmQ | title=Malaysia's Anwar returns to parliament | publisher=Agence France-Presse | date=27 August 2008 | access-date=11 July 2011 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110520164327/http://afp.google.com/article/ALeqM5jl2uOGMOucxq_mE5rD3BAogvtmmQ | archive-date=20 May 2011 | url-status=dead}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.theedgedaily.com/cms/content.jsp?id=com.tms.cms.article.Article_70d784f0-cb73c03a-11151520-ee17815a | title=Anwar sworn in, appointed as Opposition Leader | publisher=The Edge Daily | date=28 August 2008 | access-date=11 July 2011 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080829114257/http://www.theedgedaily.com/cms/content.jsp?id=com.tms.cms.article.Article_70d784f0-cb73c03a-11151520-ee17815a | archive-date=29 August 2008 | url-status=dead}}</ref>

In June 2010, the party's official newspaper, ''Suara Keadilan'', was suspended over an article alleging that the [[Federal Land Development Authority]] (Felda) had gone bankrupt.<ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.google.com/hostednews/afp/article/ALeqM5iCE_3NhRLnZWOn6HCm3DDPaXTY0w | title=Malaysia suspends main opposition newspaper | publisher=Agence France-Presse | date=29 June 2008 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100705110259/https://www.google.com/hostednews/afp/article/ALeqM5iCE_3NhRLnZWOn6HCm3DDPaXTY0w | archive-date=5 July 2010 | url-status=dead}}</ref> Government officials and Felda itself rejected the assertion as inaccurate.<ref name="MTFelda2">{{cite news |date=25 June 2010 |title=Felda to sue PKR leaders and Suara Keadilan |url=https://www.malaysia-today.net/2010/06/25/felda-to-sue-pkr-leaders-and-suara-keadilan/ |work=Malaysia Today}}</ref> The [[Minister of Home Affairs (Malaysia)|Malaysian Home Ministry]] responded by issuing a [[Order to show cause|show-cause letter]] to the publication demanding an explanation, to which the ministry stated that it was unsatisfied with the response given by the editors.<ref name="Star20102">{{cite news |date=2 July 2010 |title=Suara Keadilan's publication stopped pending Home Ministry decision |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2010/07/02/suara-keadilans-publication-stopped-pending-home-ministry-decision |work=The Star}}</ref> The Home Ministry afterwards declined to renew the newspaper's publishing permit, which had expired on 30 June 2010, thereby halting its regular publication. The decision was made under the [[Printing Presses and Publications Act 1984]], which grants the ministry broad authority to regulate and revoke publication permits.<ref name="MTPermit2">{{cite news |date=2 July 2010 |title=Suara Keadilan loses publishing permit |url=https://www.malaysia-today.net/2010/07/02/suara-keadilan-loses-publishing-permit/ |work=Malaysia Today}}</ref> The suspension drew criticism from civil society organisations and press freedom advocates, who viewed the move as a regulatory pressure on opposition-linked publications in Malaysia.<ref name="HRW20102">{{cite web |date=18 July 2010 |title=Malaysia: Don’t Stop the Presses |url=https://www.hrw.org/news/2010/07/18/malaysia-dont-stop-presses |publisher=Human Rights Watch}}</ref>

On 9 January 2012, Anwar Ibrahim was acquitted of a separate sodomy charge from 2008 by the [[High court (Malaysia)|Kuala Lumpur High Court]], presided over by Judge Mohamad Zabidin Diah. He had been accused by his former aide, [[Saiful Bukhari Azlan]], of sodomising him.<ref>{{Cite web | date=9 January 2012 | title=Anwar Ibrahim found not guilty of sodomy | url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2012/1/9/anwar-ibrahim-found-not-guilty-of-sodomy | access-date=16 November 2025 | website=[[Al Jazeera]] | language=en}}</ref> The court determined that the prosecution had failed to establish elements of the offence beyond a reasonable doubt, leading to Anwar's full discharge from the case.

==== Kajang Move (2014) ====
In early 2014, party strategy director [[Rafizi Ramli]] initiated the [[Kajang Move]] in a failed bid to install Anwar Ibrahim as the [[List of Menteris Besar of Selangor|Menteri Besar of Selangor]]. The move aimed to consolidate opposition control in the state amid perceived weaknesses in the leadership of incumbent Menteri Besar [[Khalid Ibrahim]].<ref>{{cite news | title=For Khalid, end of troubled MB tenure a 'relief' | work=Malay Mail | date=23 September 2014 | url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2014/09/23/for-khalid-end-of-troubled-mb-tenure-a-relief/750945 | access-date=27 March 2026}}</ref> Proponents framed the move as an initiative intended to strengthen the coalition's rule and counter Barisan Nasional's influence by concentrating authority under a more assertive leader.<ref>{{cite web |last=Ramli |first=Rafizi |date=29 January 2014 |title=The Kajang Move: Game Changer in Putrajaya Quest |url=https://rafiziramli.com/2014/01/the-kajang-move-game-changer-in-putrajaya-quest/ |access-date=27 March 2026 |website=rafiziramli.com |publisher= |language=en}}</ref> The move began on 27 January 2014 when [[Kajang (state constituency)|Kajang]] state assemblyman [[Lee Chin Cheh]] resigned from his seat, triggering a by-election. PKR intended for Anwar to contest and win the seat, which would allow his appointment as Menteri Besar through internal party reconfiguration.<ref name="NST-KajangPhase2-20162">{{cite news | last=Buang | first=Salleh | title=Second phase of Kajang Move? | work=New Straits Times | date=9 June 2016 | url=https://www.nst.com.my/news/2016/06/150696/second-phase-kajang-move | access-date=27 March 2026}}</ref>

However, the plan collapsed on 7 March when Anwar's acquittal in 2012 was overturned by the [[Court of Appeal of Malaysia|Court of Appeal]].<ref>{{cite news |date=7 March 2014 |title=Anwar given 5 years’ jail after appellate court reverses sodomy acquittal |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2014/03/07/anwar-acquittal-reversed/630827 |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Malay Mail |publisher=Malay Mail Sdn. Bhd. |language=en}}</ref> He was sentenced to five years in prison on sodomy charges, a verdict which disqualified him from holding public office that critics widely described as politically motivated.<ref>{{cite news |date=7 March 2014 |title=Malaysian court overturns Anwar Ibrahim’s acquittal of sodomy charge |url=https://edition.cnn.com/2014/03/07/world/asia/malaysia-court-anwar-ibrahim |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=CNN |publisher=Turner Broadcasting System |language=en}}</ref> This prevented him from standing as a candidate in the [[2014 Kajang by-election]] and potentially becoming Menteri Besar, although he remained a member of parliament pending the outcome of his appeal. The party subsequently nominated his wife, Wan Azizah Wan Ismail, as their candidate instead.<ref>{{cite web | first=Eileen | last=Ng | url= http://www.themalaysianinsider.com/malaysia/article/2-out-of-3-kajang-move-aims-met-with-the-last-on-the-way-says-pkrs-rafizi | title=2 out of 3 Kajang Move aims met with the last on the way, says PKR's Rafizi | publisher=The Malaysian Insider | date=5 October 2014 | access-date=26 May 2018 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141129212354/http://www.themalaysianinsider.com/malaysia/article/2-out-of-3-kajang-move-aims-met-with-the-last-on-the-way-says-pkrs-rafizi | archive-date=29 November 2014 | url-status=dead}}</ref>

Wan Azizah secured victory in the 23 March by-election with 59.6% of the vote against Barisan Nasional's candidate, but subsequent efforts to appoint her as Menteri Besar encountered resistance from the [[Sultan of Selangor|Selangor Sultan]] and internal Pakatan allies, particularly over constitutional and consensus issues.<ref>{{cite news | title=Selangor Sultan slams Kajang Move as hijack of democratic process | work=Malay Mail | date=12 December 2014 | url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2014/12/12/selangor-sultan-slams-kajang-move-as-hijack-of-democratic-process/800875 | access-date=27 March 2026}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |last=Yin |first=Shao Loong |date=14 February 2014 |title=The Kajang Gambit, Part Two: What does it all mean |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/opinion/2014/02/14/the-kajang-gambit-part-two-what-does-it-all-mean/618225 |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Malay Mail Online |publisher=Malay Mail Sdn. Bhd. |language=en}}</ref> Critics, including former Menteri Besar Khalid Ibrahim, described the move as an undemocratic “fiasco” that bypassed electoral mandates and exposed internal power struggles within PKR.<ref>{{cite web |last=Mohamed Osman |first=Mohamed Nawab |last2=Oh |first2=Ei Sun |last3=Pasuni |first3=Afif |date=October 2014 |title=The Selangor Chief Minister Crisis and the Future of Pakatan Rakyat (PR) |url=https://www.rsis.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/NL141007_Malaysia_Update.pdf |access-date=27 March 2026 |website=RSIS |publisher=S. Rajaratnam School of International Studies, Nanyang Technological University |language=en}}</ref> The political manoeuvre resulted in a nine-month political crisis within the [[Government of Selangor|Selangor state government]] and caused fractures within the [[Pakatan Rakyat]] coalition, requiring the involvement of the [[Sultan of Selangor|Royal Palace of Selangor]].<ref>{{cite news |date=17 August 2015 |title='Kajang Move helped to destroy Pakatan' |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/308831 |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Malaysiakini |publisher=Mkini Dotcom Sdn Bhd |language=en}}</ref> The crisis concluded with the appointment of PKR's Deputy President, [[Azmin Ali]], as the 15th Menteri Besar of Selangor, and the expulsion of Khalid Ibrahim from the party.<ref>{{cite news | title=Khalid Ibrahim expelled from PKR effective immediately | work=AWANI International | date=9 August 2014 | url=https://international.astroawani.com/node/381363 | access-date=27 March 2026}}</ref> Most analysts regarded the Kajang Move as a failure.<ref>{{cite news |date=23 September 2014 |title=With Azmin as MB, the failure of PKR’s Kajang Move, say analysts |url=https://theedgemalaysia.com/article/azmin-mb-failure-pkr%25E2%2580%2599s-kajang-move-say-analysts |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=The Edge Malaysia |publisher=The Edge Media Group |language=en}}</ref>

In 2015, Anwar Ibrahim's conviction was upheld, and he was imprisoned for a second time.<ref>{{Cite web | title=Court upholds five-year jail term for Malaysia's Anwar |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2015/2/10/court-upholds-five-year-jail-term-for-malaysias-anwar | date=10 February 2015 | access-date=16 November 2025 | website=[[Al Jazeera]]  |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news | title=Malaysia's Anwar Ibrahim sentenced to five years' jail for sodomy | News]] | language=en-AU}}</ref> Without his influence, conflict immediately emerged between PAS and DAP regarding the implementation of [[Sharia|sharia law]] in [[Kelantan]] and [[Terengganu]], resulting in the dissolution of the [[Pakatan Rakyat]] coalition in June 2015.<ref>{{cite web| url-status=live}}</ref> The new coalition emphasised multiracial reformism, with a common manifesto pledging anti-corruption measures, electoral reforms, and economic redistribution to counter the long-standing dominance of Barisan Nasional.<ref>{{cite journal |last=Ufen |first=Andreas |date=2020 |title=Opposition in transition: pre-electoral coalitions and the 2018 electoral breakthrough in |access-date=26 March 2026}}</ref>

The earlier split with PAS had left PKR and DAP without a Malay support base. However, this was supplemented by the entry of the [[Malaysian United Indigenous Party]] (BERSATU) in March 2017 as the fourth coalition partner. The UMNO splinter was led by former Prime Minister [[Mahathir Mohamad]], and it brought veteran leadership while bolstering the coalition's Malay appeal against the ruling BN government. The entry of Mahathir's party marked the reconciliation of Anwar Ibrahim and Mahathir Mohamad, two longtime enemies since the former's imprisonment by the latter in 1998.

As the main opposition from 2015 to 2018, PKR challenged BN's rule through the PH coalition by highlighting scandals, such as the [[1MDB scandal|1MDB fund mismanagement]], where investigations revealed over US$4.5 billion in diverted assets linked to the accounts of Prime Minister [[Najib Razak]].<ref>{{cite journal |last=Mohamed Noor |first=Muhamad Nadzri |date=2018 |title=The 14th General Election, the Fall of Barisan Nasional, and Political Development in Malaysia, 1957–2018 |url=https://doi.org/10.1177/186810341803700307 |journal=Journal of Current Southeast Asian Affairs |language=en |publisher=SAGE Publications |volume=37 |issue=3 |pages=139–171 |doi=10.1177/186810341803700307 |access-date=26 March 2026}}</ref> PH leveraged parliamentary scrutiny, public rallies, and protests organised by the [[Bersih]] coalition to erode BN's legitimacy.

==== Government formation (2018–2020) ====
{{See also|Seventh Mahathir cabinet}}
When the [[2018 Malaysian general election|14th general election]] was held on 9 May 2018, Pakatan Harapan (PH) secured 113 of the 222 seats in the [[Dewan Rakyat]], surpassing the 112 seats needed to form the government. The outcome ended 61 years of uninterrupted Barisan Nasional rule since [[Malayan Declaration of Independence|Malaysia's independence]] in 1957.<ref>{{cite news |date=10 May 2018 |title=Malaysia’s opposition pulls off shocking election win |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2018/5/10/malaysias-opposition-pulls-off-shocking-election-win |access-date=26 March 2026 |work=Al Jazeera |publisher=Al Jazeera Media Network |language=en}}</ref> Meanwhile, PKR won 47 seats to become the largest party within the PH coalition. Mahathir Mohamad was sworn in as the seventh prime minister by the [[King of Malaysia|Yang di-Pertuan Agong]] at the [[Istana Negara, Jalan Tuanku Abdul Halim|Istana Negara]] on 10 May, and at the age of 92, became the world's [[Lists of state leaders by age|oldest serving]] head of government at the time.<ref>{{cite news |last=Lee |first=Liz |last2=Latiff |first2=Rozanna |date=11 May 2018 |title=Mahathir, 92, sworn in as Malaysia's seventh prime minister |url=https://www.reuters.com/article/world/mahathir-92-sworn-in-as-malaysias-seventh-prime-minister-idUSKBN1I93E6/ |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Reuters |publisher=Reuters |language=en}}</ref> The cabinet was formed with 25 members, including PKR president Wan Azizah Wan Ismail as deputy prime minister.

During its early period in office, the Pakatan Harapan government focused on implementing pledges from its "Buku Harapan" manifesto.<ref>{{cite web |date=8 March 2018 |title=Buku Harapan: Rebuilding Our Nation, Fulfilling Our Hopes |url=https://dl.dapmalaysia.org/repository/Manifesto_PH_EN.pdf |access-date=27 March 2026 |website=dapmalaysia.org |publisher=Pakatan Harapan |language=en}}</ref> The manifesto outlined ten key actions to be taken within the first 100 days, including the abolition of the [[Goods and Services Tax (Malaysia)|Goods and Services Tax]] (GST) and the recovery of misappropriated public funds. The government also established a special task force to investigate the 1MDB scandal, which led to the seizure of assets belonging to former prime minister Najib Razak and the filing of corruption and money laundering charges against him. Other early measures included a review of major infrastructure projects such as the [[MRL East Coast Rail Link|East Coast Rail Link]] (ECRL) for potential cost inflation, as well as an announcement of the government's intention to ratify the [[International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination]] (ICERD). However, the decision was reversed following high participation in the [[2018 anti-ICERD rally]].<ref>{{cite news |date=17 August 2018 |title=Malaysia: 100 days in power – government still has much to do on human rights |url=https://www.amnesty.org.au/malaysia-100-days-in-power-government-still-has-much-to-do-on-human-rights/ |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Amnesty International |publisher=Amnesty International |language=en}}</ref>

As stipulated in the Pakatan Harapan manifesto, the Yang di-Pertuan Agong, [[Muhammad V of Kelantan]], granted a full royal pardon to imprisoned opposition leader Anwar Ibrahim. He was released from [[Sungai Buloh Prison]] on 16 May after serving time on sodomy charges, which Anwar had maintained were politically motivated.<ref>{{cite news |date=16 May 2018 |title=Malaysia: Anwar Ibrahim released after getting full pardon |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2018/5/16/malaysia-anwar-ibrahim-released-after-getting-full-pardon |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Al Jazeera |publisher=Al Jazeera Media Network |language=en}}</ref> Considered the prime minister–in-waiting, Anwar returned to parliament on 13 October through a [[2018 Port Dickson by-election|by-election]] in [[Port Dickson (federal constituency)|Port Dickson]]. The by-election was triggered by the resignation of the incumbent, [[Danyal Balagopal Abdullah]], a party member who vacated the seat to facilitate Anwar's return.<ref>{{Cite web | title=Anwar Ibrahim, Malaysia's prime minister-in-waiting, expects former premier Najib Razak to be jailed over 1MDB graft | url=https://www.scmp.com/news/asia/southeast-asia/article/2146584/anwar-ibrahim-free-prison-calls-end-race-based-policies | date=17 May 2018 | access-date=16 November 2015 | website=[[South China Morning Post]] | language=en}}</ref> The move became known as the "PD Move".<ref>{{cite news |last=Kamarudin |first=Kurniawati |date=25 September 2018 |title=PD Move A Premier Chance For Anwar |url=https://www.bernama.com/en/news.php/?id=1646117 |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=BERNAMA |language=en}}</ref> Having previously been unable to contest the general election due to his imprisonment, Anwar secured the seat with a majority of over 31,000 votes, defeating six other candidates.<ref>{{cite news |date=13 October 2018 |title=Anwar Ibrahim storms to victory in Port Dickson by-election, wins 72% of vote |url=https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/anwar-ibrahim-storms-to-victory-in-port-dickson-by-election-wins-72-pct-of-vote |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=The Straits Times |publisher=SPH Media |language=en}}</ref>

However, progress on broader structural reforms, including parliamentary redelineation and anti-corruption measures, proved slower amid internal disagreements and bureaucratic resistance.<ref>{{cite news |date=18 August 2018 |title=Malaysia’s new leaders have found their first 100 days tough |url=https://www.economist.com/asia/2018/08/18/malaysias-new-leaders-have-found-their-first-100-days-tough |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=The Economist |publisher=The Economist Group |language=en}}</ref> By August 2018, only a portion of the 100-day pledges had been fully implemented. Critics pointed to delays in economic stabilisation efforts, such as price controls for essential goods.<ref>{{cite news |last=Gartland |first=Annette |date=18 August 2018 |title=Malaysia’s Pakatan Harapan coalition marks its first 100 days in government |url=https://changingtimes.media/2018/08/18/malaysias-pakatan-harapan-coalition-marks-its-first-100-days-in-government/ |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Changing Times |language=en}}</ref> [[Gross domestic product]] growth declined from 5.9 percent in 2018 to 4.3 percent in 2019, driven by global trade tensions and domestic fiscal tightening.<ref>{{cite web |author=Bank Negara Malaysia |date=2018 |title=Outlook and Policy in 2019 |url=https://www.bnm.gov.my/documents/20124/6429370/ar2018_en_ch4.pdf |access-date=27 March 2026 |website=bnm.gov.my |publisher=Bank Negara Malaysia |language=en}}</ref> As a result, the coalition faced difficulties in fully delivering on its manifesto, including partial toll reductions and subsidy rationalisation, due to the economic slowdown during that period.

==== Political crisis and opposition (2020–2022) ====
{{See also|2020–2022 Malaysian political crisis}}
Towards the end of 2019, tensions gradually emerged over the planned transition of power from Mahathir Mohamad to Anwar Ibrahim, which had been scheduled to occur within two years of the coalition taking office.<ref>{{cite news |date=24 November 2019 |title=Mahathir and Anwar: a timeline of Malaysian succession and speculation |url=https://www.scmp.com/week-asia/politics/article/3038996/mahathir-and-anwar-timeline-malaysian-succession-and-speculation |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=South China Morning Post |publisher=South China Morning Post Publishers |language=en}}</ref> Mahathir repeatedly refused to stand down, citing the need to complete reforms and the resolution of Anwar's legal clearance process. This dispute created a rift within PKR and began straining the coalition's unity.<ref>{{cite web |date=28 June 2019 |title=Why Mahathir is unlikely to make Anwar his successor |url=https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/why-mahathir-unlikely-make-anwar-his-successor |access-date=27 March 2026 |website=The Interpreter |publisher=Lowy Institute |language=en}}</ref> Within the party, an internal split developed between Anwar's leadership and the faction supporting Deputy President [[Azmin Ali]], often referred to as the "Azmin cartel".<ref>{{cite news |last=Aiman |first=Amirul |date=11 August 2023 |title='Cartel' claim hints at Azmin's growing influence, says analyst |url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2023/08/11/cartel-claim-hints-at-azmins-growing-influence-says-analyst |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Free Malaysia Today |language=en}}</ref> This faction was seen as aligned with Mahathir, and the conflict led to public disputes and eventually defections that weakened the party's cohesion.<ref>{{cite news | url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/news/asia/malaysia-anwar-mahathir-pakatan-harapan-umno-pas-12462606 | title=Anwar Ibrahim says he has been betrayed by Pakatan Harapan partners amid talk of new ruling coalition | work=[[Channel Newsasia]] | date=23 February 2020 | access-date=26 February 2020 | archive-date=26 February 2020 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200226160122/https://www.channelnewsasia.com/news/asia/malaysia-anwar-mahathir-pakatan-harapan-umno-pas-12462606 | url-status=live }}</ref> The events culminated in the [[2020–2022 Malaysian political crisis]], triggered by Mahathir's sudden resignation as prime minister on 24 February 2020, which brought an end to the Pakatan Harapan government after 22 months. Mahathir did not provide a stated reason for his resignation, though reports indicated the move preempted an imminent parliamentary vote to appoint Anwar as prime minister.<ref>{{cite news |last= |first= |date=24 February 2020 |title=Malaysia’s Mahathir resigns but is asked to stay as interim PM |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2020/2/24/malaysias-mahathir-resigns-but-is-asked-to-stay-as-interim-pm |access-date=26 March 2026 |work=Al Jazeera |publisher=Al Jazeera Media Network |language=en |quote=}}</ref>

The resulting power vacuum led to rapid realignments among MPs in an episode dubbed the "Sheraton Move", named after the [[Sheraton Hotels and Resorts|Sheraton Hotel]] in [[Kuala Lumpur]] where defecting lawmakers met on 23 and 24 February to gather support for an alternative government.<ref>{{cite news |last=Bhattacharjee |first=Rash Behari |last2=Abdullah |first2=Mohsin |date=28 December 2020 |title=Newsmakers 2020: Power shifts from Pakatan Harapan to Perikatan Nasional |url=https://theedgemalaysia.com/article/newsmakers-2020-power-shifts-pakatan-harapan-perikatan-nasional |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=The Edge Malaysia |publisher=The Edge Media Group |language=en |quote=}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |last=Saidin |first=Mohd Irwan Syazli |date=11 June 2023 |title=Malaysia’s crisis of political legitimacy: Understanding the 2020 power transition and ‘Sheraton Move’ polemics through the ‘eyes’ of Malaysian political science graduates |url=https://doi.org/10.1080/23311886.2023.2222572 |journal=Cogent Social Sciences |language=en |publisher=Taylor & Francis |volume=9 |issue=1 |pages= |doi=10.1080/23311886.2023.2222572 |access-date=27 March 2026 |quote=}}</ref> The outcome was the formation of the [[Right-wing politics|right-wing]] [[Perikatan Nasional]] (PN) coalition on 29 February, comprising BERSATU, PAS, and Azmin-aligned defectors from PKR. A new government was formed afterwards on 1 March by [[Muhyiddin Yassin]] of BERSATU, with a majority in Parliament obtained through the defections of eleven PKR members of parliament, including [[Azmin Ali]], [[Zuraida Kamaruddin]], [[Saifuddin Abdullah]], [[Kamarudin Jaffar]], [[Mansor Othman]], [[Mohd Rashid Hasnon]], [[Edmund Santhara]], [[Ali Biju]], [[Willie Mongin]], [[Jonathan Yasin]], and [[Baru Bian]].<ref>{{Cite web | last=Aziz | first=Adam | date=24 February 2020 | title=Azmin leads exodus of 11 MPs from PKR and PH | url=https://theedgemalaysia.com/article/azmin-leads-exodus-11-mps-pkr-and-ph | access-date=17 November 2025 | website=[[The Edge Malaysia]]}}</ref> All except Baru Bian chose to align with the Perikatan Nasional government formed afterwards, and they were later joined by [[Jugah Muyang]], an [[Independent politician|independent]] who only joined PKR after the 2018 election.<ref>{{Cite web | url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2020/06/05/pkr-veep-from-sarawak-quits-party-pledges-loyalty-to-muhyiddin-gps/ | title=PKR MP from Sarawak quits party, pledges loyalty to Muhyiddin, GPS | publisher=Free Malaysia Today | access-date=14 July 2020 | archive-date=27 June 2020 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200627222523/https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2020/06/05/pkr-veep-from-sarawak-quits-party-pledges-loyalty-to-muhyiddin-gps/ | url-status=live}}</ref>

==== Return to government (2022–present) ====
{{See also|Anwar Ibrahim cabinet}}
Ahead of the [[2022 Malaysian general election|2022 general election]], the party campaigned on its "Tawaran Harapan" manifesto, which emphasised tackling the cost of living, strengthening institutional independence, and pursuing anti-corruption efforts.<ref>{{cite news |date=2 November 2022 |title=Pakatan Harapan unveils Malaysia election manifesto, cost of living high on the agenda |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/ph-election-manifesto-and-cost-living-3038336 |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Channel NewsAsia |publisher=Mediacorp |language=en}}</ref> The election marked a slight setback for Pakatan Harapan, which won a reduced plurality of 82 seats. PKR returned only 31 MPs to Parliament from the 47 seats previously won, thereby losing its status as the coalition's largest component to DAP with 40 seats. However, the party returned to power through a negotiated coalition government, colloquially known as the "[[National unity government#Malaysia|Unity Government]]" ({{translation|''Kerajaan Perpaduan''|lang=ms}}), formed between Pakatan Harapan and its former adversaries, including Barisan Nasional, [[Gabungan Parti Sarawak]] (GPS), [[Gabungan Rakyat Sabah]] (GRS), and several minor parties, with Anwar Ibrahim appointed as the tenth [[Prime Minister of Malaysia|prime minister]].<ref>{{cite web |last=Lee |first=Stephanie |last2=Nadarajah |first2=Hema |last3=Siao |first3=Tim |date=7 December 2022 |title=Malaysian PM Appointed to Lead Divided Coalition Government |url=https://www.asiapacific.ca/publication/malaysian-pm-appointed-lead-divided-coalition-government |access-date=27 March 2026 |website=Asia Pacific Foundation of Canada |publisher=Asia Pacific Foundation of Canada |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |date=24 November 2022 |title=Anwar sworn in as Malaysia’s PM after 25-year struggle for reform |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2022/11/24/malaysias-anwar-named-countrys-10th-prime-minister |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Al Jazeera |publisher=Al Jazeera Media Network |language=en}}</ref>

The arrangement marked a departure from the party's pre-election opposition to Barisan Nasional, as stability and continuity in governance were prioritised over idealism due to sustained destabilisation efforts by Perikatan Nasional.<ref>{{cite journal |last=Ostwald |first=Kai |date=2 May 2023 |title=Why Malaysia’s Unity Government Will (Probably) Survive |url=https://www.iseas.edu.sg/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/ISEAS_Perspective_2023_36.pdf |journal=ISEAS Perspective |language=en |publisher=ISEAS – Yusof Ishak Institute |volume=2023 |issue=36 |issn=2335-6677 |access-date=27 March 2026}}</ref> Despite alignment at the leadership level, historical animosity between UMNO and PKR complicated cooperation within the grassroots, where distrust continued to persist.<ref name="Star20242">{{cite news |date=27 December 2024 |title=Umno-Pakatan animosity thawing but rift still remains |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2024/12/27/umno-pakatan-animosity-thawing-but-rift-still-remains |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=The Star}}</ref> The formation drew criticism from the party's hardliner faction led by Deputy President [[Rafizi Ramli]], particularly over unease that cooperation with UMNO would contradict earlier reformist positions, as the "Grand Old Party" embodied the political establishment that the party sought to challenge since its founding.<ref name="MalayMail20252">{{cite news |date=19 July 2025 |title=‘Not their home any more’: Rafizi warns PKR risks Umno-style exodus over grassroots anger after party polls |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2025/07/19/not-their-home-any-more-rafizi-warns-pkr-risks-umno-style-exodus-over-grassroots-anger-after-party-polls/184429 |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Malay Mail}}</ref><ref name="FMT20242">{{cite news |date=13 February 2024 |title=PKR grassroots unhappy with party’s handling of Najib’s commuted sentence |url=https://www.malaysia-today.net/2024/02/13/pkr-grassroots-unhappy-with-partys-handling-of-najibs-commuted-sentence/ |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Malaysia Today}}</ref>

Having emerged from a political crisis and the [[COVID-19 recession in Malaysia|COVID-19 recession]], Anwar's administration stressed political stability as the basis for economic recovery.<ref>{{cite news |date=23 September 2025 |title=PM Anwar: Stability and prudent financial management key to Malaysia’s growth |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2025/09/23/pm-anwar-stability-and-prudent-financial-management-key-to-malaysias-growth/192146 |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=Malay Mail |publisher=Malay Mail Sdn. Bhd. |language=en}}</ref> The cabinet was appointed in December 2022, with ministerial portfolios assigned to maintain support from Barisan Nasional and the [[East Malaysia]]n parties. With an ideologically diverse cabinet, stability efforts focused on coalition consensus and gradual policy implementation to prevent fragmentation. However, the incremental pace of reforms led to intensified criticism from party hardliners.<ref>{{cite news |date=14 July 2025 |title=Special Report: Anwar struggles with institutional reforms |url=https://theedgemalaysia.com/node/762831 |access-date=27 March 2026 |work=The Edge Malaysia |publisher=The Edge Media Group |language=en}}</ref> This culminated in a power struggle during the [[2025 People's Justice Party leadership election|2025 party elections]] between the hardliners, led by Rafizi Ramli under the HIRUK ({{literal translation|agitate}}) faction, and the party establishment, led by [[Nurul Izzah Anwar]] under the DAMAI ({{literal translation|peace}}) faction.<ref name="StraitsTimesHirukDamai">{{cite news |date=19 May 2025 |title=Two PKR leaders woo grassroots on nationwide tours amid power struggle |url=https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/two-pkr-leaders-woo-grassroots-on-nationwide-tours-amid-power-struggle-in-malaysias-ruling-party |access-date=18 March 2025 |work=The Straits Times |quote=Rafizi is defending his position as PKR deputy president through his Jelajah Hiruk (Noisy Tour) ...
