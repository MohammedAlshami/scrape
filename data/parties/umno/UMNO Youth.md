The '''UMNO Youth Movement''' ([[Malay language|Malay]]: {{lang|ms|Pergerakan Pemuda UMNO}}; [[Jawi alphabet|Jawi]]: {{lang|ms-arab|{{Script|Arab|ڤرڬرقن ڤمودا امنو}}}}), or simply known as the '''UMNO Youth''', is the [[youth wing]] of [[Malaysia]]'s former [[ruling party]], the [[United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO). The group's membership consists of UMNO members aged 18 to 40, and its establishment was rooted in championing the ethnic causes of the [[Malays (ethnic group)|Malay]] and [[Bumiputera (Malaysia)|Bumiputera]] youth. Since March 2023, the UMNO Youth has been led by [[Muhamad Akmal Saleh]], the [[Malacca]] [[Malacca State Legislative Assembly|state assemblyman]] (MLA) for [[Merlimau (state constituency)|Merlimau]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2023/03/12/umno-polls-official-results-for-youth-wanita-and-puteri-exco-yet-to-be-announced |title=Umno polls: Official results for Youth, Wanita and Puteri exco yet to be announced |work=The Star |date=2023-03-12 |access-date=2023-05-07}}</ref>

The UMNO Youth is organised separately from the women's youth wing (''Puteri UMNO'') and [[student wing]] (''UMNOSiswa'').<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.sinarharian.com.my/article/60390/khas/umno/siswa-umno-bakal-ditubuhkan-di-semua-universiti |title=Siswa UMNO bakal ditubuhkan di semua universiti |publisher=Sinar Harian |date=2019-12-04 |language=ms |access-date=2023-04-29}}</ref> The youth wing has been characterised as more outspoken and ideologically assertive than the party leadership, particularly under the leadership of Akmal Saleh, who has been associated with a more [[Nationalism|nationalist]] and confrontational political style. This approach has contributed to periodic tensions within UMNO regarding the party’s strategic direction, especially over participation in the [[Anwar Ibrahim cabinet|unity government]] and cooperation with [[Pakatan Harapan]], particularly its component party, the [[Democratic Action Party]] (DAP), which has historically been a political rival of UMNO.

== History ==
=== Establishment ===
The history of the UMNO Youth is closely linked to the broader development of the [[United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO) during the late colonial period in [[British Malaya]]. As part of the party’s efforts to strengthen its organisational structure and cultivate future leadership, UMNO established a dedicated [[youth wing]] to mobilise younger members and support its [[Nationalism|nationalist]] objectives against [[British Empire|British colonial rule]].<ref>{{cite journal|url=https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/modern-asian-studies/article/abs/formation-and-first-years-of-the-united-malays-national-organization-umno-19461948/C63C6723B1BB29F3D542122B781E7CA0|title=The Formation and First Years of the United Malays National Organization (U.M.N.O.) 1946–1948|year=1977 |doi=10.1017/S0026749X00000536 |access-date=13 May 2023 |last1=Stockwell |first1=A. J. |journal=Modern Asian Studies |volume=11 |issue=4 |pages=481–513 |s2cid=146363282 |url-access=subscription }}</ref> The UMNO Youth was formally founded on 26 August 1949 in [[Butterworth, Seberang Perai|Butterworth]], [[Penang]], during a conference attended by representatives from various state branches. 

=== Era of Hussein Onn (1949–1951) ===
The founding conference elected [[Hussein Onn]], son of party founder [[Onn Jaafar]], as the first UMNO Youth Chief, and the organisation became initially known as the UMNO Youth Alliance ([[Malay language|Malay]]: {{lang|ms|Perikatan Pemuda UMNO}}).<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.perdana.org.my/blog/2021/05/29/tun-hussein-onn-one-of-the-greats/|title=Tun Hussein Onn: One of the Greats|access-date=13 May 2023}}</ref> 

=== Era of Abdul Razak Hussein (1951) ===
Following Hussein Onn’s appointment as UMNO Secretary-General in August 1950, leadership of the youth wing was assumed by [[Abdul Razak Hussein]]  of [[Pekan]], [[Pahang]], who continued to develop its organisational role within the party.<ref>{{cite book|author1=Kee Beng Ooi|title=UMNO and Looking Back at History". Catharsis: A Second Chance for Democracy in Malaysia|url=https://www.degruyter.com/document/doi/10.1355/9789814818926-018/html?lang=en|date=2018|publisher=ISEAS|isbn=978-981-4818-92-6|pages=58|doi=10.1355/9789814818926-018 |s2cid=239358713 }}</ref> The slogan changed from ''"Merdeka"'' (Independence) to ''"[[Ketuanan Melayu|Hidup Melayu]]"'' (Long Live the Malays), reflecting the movement's evolving focus from the struggle for independence to the advocacy of Malay rights and socio-economic interests in the post-colonial period.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Ariffin |first=Omar |title=Melayu Baru dan Bangsa Malaysia: Tradisi Cendekia dan Krisis Budaya |publisher=Utusan Publications |year=1993 |location=Kuala Lumpur |pages=45–47}}</ref>

=== Era of Sardon Jubir (1951–1964) ===
During the era of Tun [[Sardon Jubir]], the slogans ''"Pemuda Tahan Lasak"'' (Tough Youth) and ''"Revolusi Mental"'' (Mental Revolution) were introduced. His wife was a [[Chinese people|Chinese]] woman. This period coincided with the [[Indonesia–Malaysia confrontation]] and the [[Independence of Singapore Agreement 1965|Expulsion of Singapore from Malaysia]].<ref>{{Cite news |last=Chew |first=Ernest |date=22 June 1998 |title=The Singapore Separation |work=The Straits Times |page=32}}</ref> In December 1955, as a result of amendments to the UMNO Constitution, the name of the UMNO Youth Alliance was changed to the UMNO Youth Movement. 

=== Era of Senu Abdul Rahman (1964–1971) ===
Senu continued the policies set by Sardon. A significant challenge he faced was when Singapore sought to exit Malaysia. Statements by [[Lee Kuan Yew]] questioning the special status of the indigenous Malays ([[Bumiputera (Malaysia)|Bumiputera]]) had angered the Malay community.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Lau |first=Albert |title=A Moment of Anguish: Singapore in Malaysia and the Politics of Disengagement |publisher=Times Academic Press |year=1998 |isbn=9812101345 |location=Singapore |pages=153–155}}</ref> The UMNO Youth passed a resolution urging the federal government to take action against Lee Kuan Yew. However, Singapore would subsequently leave the federation in 1965.

=== Era of Harun Idris (1971–1976) ===
Datuk [[Harun Idris]] served as the [[Menteri Besar of Selangor|Menteri Besar Selangor]] and was an aggressive leader of UMNO Youth. His strident Malay nationalism contributed to a loss of [[Malaysian Chinese|Chinese]] voter support for the ruling [[Alliance Party (Malaysia)|Perikatan]] coalition, weakening its position in [[Selangor]] and contributing to the racial tensions that culminated in the [[13 May incident]] in 1969.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Von Vorys |first=Karl |title=Democracy without Consensus: Communalism and Political Stability in Malaysia |publisher=Princeton University Press |year=1975 |isbn=0691075700 |pages=318–340}}</ref> He was revered by Malay nationalists for his efforts to uplift poor rural Malays by facilitating their migration to urban areas for work and residence alongside the [[Chinese people|Chinese]]. This period also saw the launch of the [[New Economic Policy (Malaysia)|New Economic Policy]] (NEP), aimed at enlarging the economic pie and ensuring a more equitable distribution of wealth among ethnic groups.<ref>{{Cite journal |last=Jomo K.S. |date=2004 |title=The New Economic Policy and Interethnic Relations in Malaysia |url=https://www.unrisd.org/80256B3C005BCCF9/(httpAuxPages)/F270E0C066F3DE7780256B65004D6AF5/$file/Jomo.pdf |journal=UNRISD |pages=7–9}}</ref>

=== Era of Syed Jaafar Albar (1976–1977) ===
[[Syed Jaafar Albar]], originally from [[Johor]] and of [[Arab Malaysians|Arab]] descent, was a fiery orator. He was often mocked by [[Lee Kuan Yew]] of the [[People's Action Party|PAP]] for also being of immigrant stock.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Ooi |first=Jeff |date=12 February 2006 |title=The 'Singa UMNO' Roars No More |work=Malaysiakini}}</ref> Jaafar Albar was so fierce in his advocacy that he was nicknamed the "Lion of UMNO," and he referred to UMNO Youth as the "Ginger Group." Nevertheless, his prominence was eclipsed by the charisma of Datuk Harun Idris.

=== Era of Suhaimi Kamarudin (1977–1982) ===
[[Suhaimi Kamaruddin]], the nephew of Datuk [[Harun Idris]] from Selangor, was expected to be as radical as his uncle. The slogan ''"Pemuda Bertindak"'' (Youth in Action) was adopted. In his bid to defend his position as UMNO Youth Chief against challenger Datuk Seri [[Anwar Ibrahim]], Suhaimi proposed raising the Bumiputera equity target from 30% to 53%. However, this was not well-received even within the UMNO Youth, as the 30% target had yet to be achieved. Consequently, members voted for Anwar Ibrahim, who was seen as the protégé of Prime Minister [[Mahathir Mohamad]].<ref>{{Cite news |last=Shamsul Akmar |date=12 November 1993 |title=Anwar's Rise: The End of the Line for Suhaimi |work=The Star |location=Kuala Lumpur}}</ref>

=== Era of Anwar Ibrahim (1982–1987) ===
The tenure of [[Anwar Ibrahim]] as the UMNO Youth Chief is widely regarded as a transformative period in the wing's history, marked by a significant ideological shift and institutional reform. Anwar's leadership represented a political synthesis of [[Islamic revivalism]], [[Malay nationalism]], and [[Progressivism|progressive]] [[Internationalism (politics)|internationalism]], fundamentally altering the movement's character and reach.

Anwar entered UMNO in 1982 at the direct invitation of Prime Minister [[Mahathir Mohamad]], a move seen as a co-optation of the Islamic intellectual movement into the ruling party's fold. Before this, Anwar was the president and a prominent leader of the [[Malaysian Islamic Youth Movement]] (ABIM), a nationwide Islamic youth organisation known for its social activism and critique of government policies, which led to his detention under the [[Internal Security Act 1960|Internal Security Act (ISA)]] from 1974-1975.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Abdullah |first=Firdaus |title=Radical Malay Politics: Its Origins and Early Development |publisher=Pelanduk Publications |year=1985 |location=Petaling Jaya |pages=152–155}}</ref> His recruitment into UMNO was seen as bridging the party's secular-nationalist base with the rising tide of Islamic consciousness amongst the educated Malay youth. 

Anwar's decision to accept Mahathir's invitation to join UMNO signified Mahathir's foresight in channelling Islamic revivalism into the ruling party and Anwar's pragmatic belief that substantive change could only be achieved from within the power structure.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Means |first=Gordon P. |title=Malaysian Politics: The Second Generation |publisher=Oxford University Press |year=1991 |isbn=0195889881 |location=Singapore |pages=241–243}}</ref> His entry slogan, ''"Masuk UMNO, Teruskan Perjuangan"'' (Join UMNO, Continue the Struggle), was aimed directly at his ABIM constituency, assuring them that his ideological mission remained intact.

Anwar's vision for UMNO Youth was a sophisticated synthesis of three core streams:

* Islamic Ethics and Governance: He infused the movement with the language of moral governance ''(siyasah shar'iyyah)'', anti-corruption, and social welfare, drawn from his ABIM background. He argued that development must be spiritually meaningful and ethically grounded.

* Reformist Malay Economic Nationalism: He firmly upheld the movement's traditional role in championing Malay economic advancement and the [[New Economic Policy (Malaysia)|New Economic Policy (NEP)]], but framed it within a context of creating a competitive, learned, and ethical Bumiputera community, not merely a recipient of quotas.

* Progressive Internationalism: He connected the UMNO Youth to global intellectual currents, engaging with debates on democracy, development in the Muslim world, and post-colonial discourse. Under his direction, the movement actively cultivated relationships with youth wings of ruling and opposition parties across the [[Organisation of Islamic Cooperation|Islamic world]] and in the [[Non-Aligned Movement]], elevating its international profile.

This tripartite ideology was encapsulated in his landmark slogan for the wing: ''"Generasi Penggerak Zaman"'' (The Generation that Moves the Times). It called for the youth to be proactive, intellectual, and morally principled leaders of change, rather than just political foot soldiers.<ref>{{Cite speech |last=Ibrahim |first=Anwar |title=Generasi Penggerak Zaman: Wawasan Pemuda UMNO |date=19 March 1983 |location=UMNO Youth General Assembly |url=https://www.anwaribrahim.com/1983/03/19/generasi-penggerak-zaman/}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book |last=Funnell |first=Victor C. |title=The Anwar Debacle: Culture and Democracy in Malaysia |publisher=Institute of Southeast Asian Studies |year=2001 |isbn=9812301125 |pages=89–91}}</ref>

The UMNO Youth's approach was also revolutionised from within. Anwar introduced regular Quranic study circles ''(usrah)'' and intellectual discourse sessions ''(fiqh al-harakah)'' into wing activities, emphasising the integration of religious learning with political activism. He also established closer ties with university students and graduates, launching programs emphasising academic excellence, critical thinking, and technological literacy, such as early initiatives on information technology. Consequently, he actively recruited doctors, engineers, lawyers, and academics into the wing, broadening its social base beyond traditional party apparatchiks and creating an internal think-tank culture focused on policy.

While Prime Minister Mahathir launched the "Buy British Last" policy, Anwar's tenure was itself marked by several assertive actions. In 1984, Anwar led the UMNO Youth in a highly publicised "occupation" of the British High Commissioner's residence, the [[Carcosa Seri Negara]], in a dramatic act of symbolic nationalism. Framed as reclaiming a vestige of colonialism, the action garnered significant popular support but was criticised by some as reckless populism.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Gomez |first=J. |date=22 September 1984 |title=The Carcosa Affair: Storming the Colonial Bastion |work=Asiaweek |pages=32–33}}</ref> Anwar also organised the 1985 Bumiputera Economic Congress (BEC), using the platform to deliver a landmark address where he pushed for a "second stage" of the NEP. He criticised rent-seeking and dependency, calling for a shift from "ownership" to "managerial control" and the creation of a genuine Bumiputera entrepreneurial class with "global competitiveness."<ref>{{Cite book |last=Shari |first=Ishak |title=Growth and Equity in Malaysia: The NEP and Beyond |publisher=Perspektif Publications |year=1986 |location=Kuala Lumpur |pages=112–118}}</ref>

By 1987, Anwar's influence had grown exponentially, becoming the de facto leader of the reformist [[:ms:Team Wawasan|Team Wawasan]] faction aligned with Mahathir. The faction pressured him to contest the UMNO Vice-Presidency in the [[1987 United Malays National Organisation leadership election|1987 UMNO General Assembly]], seeing it as a necessary step in his rapid rise. His agreement led to the handover of the Youth Chief post to his protégé and deputy, [[Najib Razak]], on 24 April 1987. The transition was staged to show continuity and the anointing of a successor from his own reformist camp, ensuring the succession remained within his ideological circle.<ref>{{Cite news |last=K. |first=Das |date=25 April 1987 |title=Anwar passes the youth baton to Najib |work=The Star |page=1}}</ref>

Anwar Ibrahim's era fundamentally altered the trajectory of the UMNO Youth. He successfully, if temporarily, merged Islamic moral discourse with modernist development agendas, creating a potent political language that appealed to an increasingly Islamic generation. The wing was transformed from a primarily communal pressure group into a movement with intellectual aspirations and a broader socio-religious agenda. His legacy is dual-edged: he is remembered as the most ideologically distinctive and transformative Youth Chief who expanded the wing's horizons, but also as the figure whose meteoric rise and later dramatic fall would, decades later, contribute to profound schisms within UMNO itself.

=== Era of Najib Razak (1987–1993) ===
Following the ascension of [[Anwar Ibrahim]] to the party vice-presidency in 1987, [[Najib Razak]], son of Prime Minister [[Abdul Razak Hussein]], took over the post of UMNO Youth chief. He had previously served as Deputy Youth Chief for three terms under Datuk [[Suhaimi Kamaruddin]] and two terms under Datuk Seri [[Anwar Ibrahim]]. His tenure coincided with [[Operation Lalang]], a major crackdown on dissent under the [[Internal Security Act (Malaysia)|Internal Security Act]]. In the lead up to the [[1987 United Malays National Organisation leadership election|1987 party elections]], UMNO's leadership had split into [[:ms:Team Wawasan|Team A]], led by [[Mahathir Mohamad]], and [[Parti Melayu Semangat 46|Team B]], led by [[Tengku Razaleigh Hamzah]]. After an election campaign marred by rigging allegations, a narrow victory for Mahathir by a majority of 43 votes saw his faction sweeping most contested positions, leading to the dissenting ''"[[:ms:UMNO 11|Kumpulan 11]]"'' (Group of 11) to challenge the party election results. On 4 February 1988, Justice Harun Hashim of the [[Kuala Lumpur Courts Complex|Kuala Lumpur High Court]] declared UMNO as unlawful due to breaches of the [[Societies Act 1966|Societies Act]], effectively rendering UMNO and its Youth wing defunct.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Hickling |first=Harold |date=5 February 1988 |title=UMNO Ruling Throws Malaysia into Political Turmoil |work=The Guardian |location=London}}</ref> UMNO Baru (New UMNO) was established on 13 February 1988, and the word 'Baru' was soon dropped. On 21 May 1988, Najib officially returned as the UMNO Youth Chief. <ref>[http://www.pemuda.com.my/modules.php?name=Sejarah5 Sejarah Pergerakan Pemuda UMNO]{{dead link|date=October 2021 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref>

=== Era of Rahim Thamby Chik (1993–1996) ===
The [[1993 United Malays National Organisation leadership election|1993 UMNO General Assembly]] saw the election of [[Abdul Rahim Thamby Chik]] as leader of the UMNO Youth following the ascendancy of [[Najib Razak]] to the party vice-presidency. His tenure saw a period of assertive Malay nationalism aligned with the modernising vision of Prime Minister [[Mahathir Mohamad]]. However, it was also marked by significant political controversy.

Abdul Rahim, who simultaneously served as the [[Chief Minister of Malacca]] (1982–1994), leveraged his dual roles to champion Bumiputera economic agendas and socio-political issues. Under his leadership, the UMNO Youth adopted a posture as the vigilant guardian of Malay rights and Islamic values within the framework of Malaysia's rapid industrialisation. A key policy moment during his era was the 1995 Bumiputera Economic Congress (BEC), where the UMNO Youth, under Rahim's guidance, pushed for a critical review of the [[New Economic Policy (Malaysia)|New Economic Policy (NEP)]]. He argued that the NEP's focus should evolve from a static 30% Bumiputera equity target to a more dynamic approach emphasising wealth creation, competitiveness, and the development of a robust Bumiputera commercial and industrial community (BCIC).<ref>{{Cite news |last=Johan |first=Jaafar |date=6 July 1995 |title=Rahim: NEP review vital for new challenges |work=New Straits Times |page=4}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book |last=Jomo K.S. |title=U-Turn? Malaysian Economic Development Policies After 1990 |publisher=Centre for East Asian Studies, James Cook University |year=1994 |location=Townsville |pages=41–43}}</ref>

His tenure was notably confrontational on social and political issues. Rahim Thamby Chik was a prominent advocate for moral policing and often spoke against what he perceived as "Western decadence."<ref>{{Cite news |last=Shamsul Akmar |date=15 November 1993 |title=Rahim's Guard: Morals and Malays |work=The Star |location=Kuala Lumpur}}</ref> However, his career was abruptly derailed by a major scandal in 1994. He was accused of statutory rape involving a 15-year-old schoolgirl. The allegations triggered a national uproar and a police investigation. Although he was never formally charged, with the Attorney-General's Chambers stating there was "insufficient evidence", the political damage was severe. He resigned from his position as Chief Minister of Malacca in October 1994 amidst the controversy.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Sukumaran |first=R. |date=5 October 1994 |title=Rahim Thamby Chik quits as Malacca CM |work=New Straits Times |page=1}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last= |first= |date=14 October 1994 |title=No charges against Rahim |work=The Straits Times |location=Singapore |page=1}}</ref> Despite the scandal, he retained the position of UMNO Youth Chief until the end of his term in 1996.

=== Era of Ahmad Zahid Hamidi (1996–1998) ===
Abdul Rahim was succeeded by [[Ahmad Zahid Hamidi]], who took over following the [[1996 United Malays National Organisation leadership election|1996 UMNO General Assembly]]. He inherited a movement that was politically forceful but also divided in the aftermath of controversy.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Khoo |first=Boo Teik |title=Paradoxes of Mahathirism: An Intellectual Biography of Mahathir Mohamad |publisher=Oxford University Press |year=1995 |isbn=983560035X |location=Kuala Lumpur |pages=289–291}}</ref> At the time, Zahid was known to be aligned with [[Anwar Ibrahim]], a relationship lasting to the present day when he brought UMNO to support Anwar as Prime Minister in 2022, himself serving as [[Deputy Prime Minister of Malaysia|Deputy Prime Minister]] in [[Anwar Ibrahim cabinet|Anwar's administration]].

During his tenure as Youth Chief, issues of cronyism, collusion, and nepotism (KKN) became common subjects of scepticism and jokes at UMNO assemblies. Following the schism between Anwar Ibrahim and [[Mahathir Mohamad]], the latter, as [[President of the United Malays National Organisation|UMNO President]], publicly listed UMNO members and companies that had received government tenders, shares, and board appointments. Names like Ahmad Zahid and other vocal critics of Mahathir were included, though the list was disputed for being incomplete with allegations of it being politically motivated.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Gomez |first=Edmund Terence |date=1991 |title=Money Politics in the Barisan Nasional |work=Far Eastern Economic Review |pages=11–12}}</ref>

=== Era of Hishammuddin Hussein (1999–2009) ===
Following the decision of Ahmad Zahid to contest for the party's Supreme Council, the young leader [[Hishammuddin Hussein]], son of Prime Minister [[Hussein Onn]], succeeded him in [[2004 United Malays National Organisation leadership election|2004]]. He previously held the prestigious cabinet position of [[Minister of Youth and Sports (Malaysia)|Minister of Youth and Sports]] (KBS) and [[Minister of Education (Malaysia)|Minister of Education]] (KPM) prior to his election. His tenure is notably remembered for his symbolic waving of the ''[[kris]]'' (traditional dagger) at the 2005 UMNO General Assembly, a controversial gesture interpreted as a strong assertion of Malay nationalism.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Abdullah |first=Zulkifli |date=22 July 2005 |title=The Kris: Symbolism and Controversy |work=New Straits Times |page=6}}</ref>

=== Era of Khairy Jamaluddin (2009–2018) ===
During the [[2009 United Malays National Organisation leadership election|2009 UMNO General Assembly]], [[Khairy Jamaluddin]], son-in-law of Prime Minister [[Abdullah Ahmad Badawi]], was elected as the new UMNO Youth Chief. He faced the heavy responsibility of restoring the confidence of young voters in the [[Barisan Nasional]] coalition, especially following the party's massive losses in the [[2008 Malaysian general election|2008 general election]]. Under his father-in-law's leadership, the party lost 58 seats and 12.86 percent of the popular vote in the election, including the fall of five states to the [[Pakatan Rakyat]] opposition. His biggest challenge was overcoming image problems stemming from [[Corruption in Malaysia|money politics]] allegations he faced during the 2009 Youth Chief election.<ref name="kj-rasuah">[http://www.harakahdaily.net/index.php?option=com_content&task=view&id=020051&Itemid=99 Pilih Khairy, Pemuda Umno hilang hala tuju] - from harakahdaily.net. Accessed 29 March 2009.</ref> Despite this, he was credited with modernising the wing's outreach and engaging more directly with youth issues.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Lee |first=Shi-Ian |date=28 March 2013 |title=Can KJ Deliver the Youth Vote? |work=The Malaysian Insider |url=http://www.themalaysianinsider.com |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130330063610/http://www.themalaysianinsider.com/malaysia/article/can-kj-deliver-the-youth-vote |archive-date=30 March 2013}}</ref>

=== Era of Asyraf Wajdi Dusuki (2018–2023) ===
[[Asyraf Wajdi Dusuki]] was [[2018 United Malays National Organisation leadership election|elected]] as the new UMNO Youth Chief on 30 June 2018, following Barisan Nasional's historic loss in the [[2018 Malaysian general election|14th General Election]]. A former [[Dewan Negara|Senator]] and Deputy Minister in the Prime Minister's Department (Religious Affairs), he had lost in his run for parliament during the election. Coming from a politically Islamic background and seen as aligned with Anwar Ibrahim, he led the wing from 2018 to 2023, succeeding Khairy Jamaluddin during a period of significant opposition and rebuilding for UMNO.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Zolkepli |first=Farik |date=1 July 2018 |title=Asyraf Wajdi is new Umno Youth chief |work=The Star |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2018/07/01/asyraf-wajdi-is-new-umno-youth-chief/}}</ref>

=== Era of Muhamad Akmal Saleh (2023–Present) ===
[[Muhamad Akmal Saleh]], the [[Malacca State Legislative Assembly|ADUN]] for [[Merlimau (state constituency)|Merlimau]] from [[Malacca]], was [[2023 United Malays National Organisation leadership election|elected]] to the position of UMNO Youth Chief on 11 March 2023. His leadership slogan is ''"Pemuda Cara Kita"'' (Youth, Our Way). He represents a new generation of leadership tasked with reinvigorating the wing ahead of the [[Next Malaysian general election|16th General Election]], focusing on grassroots engagement and issues affecting young Malaysians.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Yusof |first=Amir |date=12 March 2023 |title=Umno polls: Merlimau assemblyman Muhamad Akmal Saleh elected as new Umno Youth chief |work=CNA |url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/malaysia-umno-polls-muhamad-akmal-saleh-youth-chief-3332711}}</ref>

== Organisational structure ==
The UMNO Youth Movement was established over the party’s recognition of the need for a dedicated youth wing to support and develop future leadership within UMNO. The movement serves as a platform for grooming potential leaders, with members expected to demonstrate commitment and leadership capabilities at the youth level before assuming positions within the wider party structure. In this regard, UMNO Youth functions as an integral component of the party’s organisational framework. The wing has also been noted for its role in articulating sensitive or controversial issues, reflecting the comparatively assertive and activist orientation commonly associated with youth organisations.<ref>{{cite book|author=Ahmad Fawzi Mohd Basri|title= The United Malays National Organization (UMNO) 1981-1991: A Study of the Mechanics of a changing Political Culture|url=https://core.ac.uk/download/pdf/2731594.pdf|date=October 1992|publisher= Centre for South East Asian Studies University of Hull|pages=103}}</ref>

=== Hierarchy ===
The UMNO Youth is led by the youth chief, followed by the vice youth chief and 20 youth executive committee members or "EXCOs" which all are elected through UMNO party elections. The youth chief would then have the executive power to appoint the secretary, treasurer, information chief, executive secretary and up to 15 youth EXCOs.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=AaRUAAAAIBAJ&dq=%22UMNO+Youth%22&pg=PA2&article_id=6713,686789|title=Anwar to name five for Youth Exco|website=[[New Straits Times]]|access-date=1 May 2023}}</ref> There are other positions such as permanent chairman and deputy permanent chairman who holds less executive power within the youth wing where they are in charge of chairing the annual UMNO general assembly or ''Perhimpunan Agung UMNO'' (PAU) for the youth wing.

All of the positions mentioned are apparent in various levels, which it can be present at '''branch''' level ({{langx|ms|Cawangan}}), followed with '''divisional''' ({{langx|ms|Bahagian}}), to '''state''' ({{langx|ms|Negeri}}), and finally '''national''' ({{langx|ms|Pusat}}). Oftentimes, most national youth leaders hold divisional posts. For example, [[Muhamad Akmal Saleh]] and [[Nazifuddin Najib|Nazif Najib]] are both division heads of their respective constituencies, and various national youth EXCOs are also youth division heads. In order to hold divisional, state or national level positions, all youth leaders must first hold branch level positions beforehand.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2023/03/18/najibs-son-nazifuddin-narrowly-elected-langkawi-umno-chief/|title=Najib's son Nazifuddin narrowly elected Langkawi UMNO Chief|website=FreeMalaysiaToday|access-date=1 May 2023}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.astroawani.com/berita-politik/akmal-saleh-menang-selesa-di-jasin-ketua-umno-bahagian-termuda-penggal-ini-411470|title=Akmal Saleh menang selesa di Jasin, Ketua UMNO Bahagian termuda penggal ini|website=[[Astro Awani]]|language=ms|access-date=1 May 2023}}</ref>

== Leadership ==
=== Youth Chief ===
The Youth Chief of UMNO acts as the penultimate leader of the party's youth wing where it holds comparable authority to the [[president of the United Malays National Organisation|President]], only being limited to the youth wing. Throughout [[Politics of Malaysia#History|Malaysian political history]], four former Youth Chiefs of UMNO, which are [[Hussein Onn]], [[Abdul Razak Hussein]], [[Najib Razak]], and [[Anwar Ibrahim]], went on to serve as the [[Prime Minister of Malaysia]].<ref>{{cite book|author=Peter Searle|title=The Riddle of Malaysian Capitalism Rent-Seekers Or Real Capitalists?|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=XpuovMTBl2MC&dq=%22UMNO+Youth%22+-wikipedia&pg=PA181|date=1999|publisher=University of Hawaii Press|pages=181|isbn=9780824820534 }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://southeastasiaglobe.com/licence-to-killmalaysia-ahmad-zahid-hamidi-crim/|title=Malaysia's brazen Home Minister Ahmad Zahid Hamidi is determined to reduce the country's alarming gang activity at any cost|website=South East Asia Globe|date=November 2013 |access-date=14 May 2023}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.malaysianbar.org.my/article/news/legal-and-general-news/general-news/razak-drove-a-hard-bargain|title=Razak drove a hard bargain|website=Malaysian Bar|access-date=14 May 2023}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/opinions/21607|title=Najibs climb up the Umno ladder with a helping hand from Anwar|website=Malaysiakini|date=19 June 2002 |access-date=14 May 2023}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://thestar.com.my/news/story.asp?file=%2F2004%2F1%2F7%2Fnation%2F7055813&sec=nation|title=Najib is Deputy PM, Cabinet reshuffled|website=The Star|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20040112054121/http://thestar.com.my/news/story.asp?file=%2F2004%2F1%2F7%2Fnation%2F7055813&sec=nation |access-date=14 May 2023|archive-date=2004-01-12 }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://eresources.nlb.gov.sg/newspapers/Digitised/Article/straitstimes19590720-1.2.56|title=Alliance has secret poll weapon|website=NewspaperSG|access-date=14 May 2023}}</ref>
