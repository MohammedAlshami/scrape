The '''Socialist Party of Malaysia''' ('''PSM''', {{Langx|ms|Parti Sosialis Malaysia}}; {{zh|c=马来西亚社会主义党}}; {{langx|ta|மலேசிய சோசியலிஸ்ட் கட்சி}}), is a [[socialist]] [[political party]] in [[Malaysia]] and an offshoot of [[Parti Rakyat Malaysia]], which originally upheld the same ideology.<ref name=s>{{cite news |title=Leftist discourse adds to political debate |url=https://theedgemalaysia.com/article/leftist-discourse-adds-political-debate-0 |work=[[The Edge (Malaysia)|The Edge Financial Daily]] |date=Jul 5, 2013}}{{pn|date=December 2024}}</ref>

For ten years following its founding in 1998, the party was denied registration as a political party by the [[Government of Malaysia|Federal Government of Malaysia]], on the grounds that it was a threat to national security.<ref>{{Cite web |title=OUR STORY |url=https://partisosialis.org/our-story/ |access-date=2024-10-02 |website=Parti Sosialis Malaysia |language=en-US}}</ref> 

However, the Home Ministry gave them the green light in June 2008, shortly after PSM enjoyed its electoral breakthrough when it won its first parliamentary and state seats in [[Sungai Siput (federal constituency)|Sungai Siput]] and [[Kota Damansara (state constituency)|Kota Damansara]] respectively.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2011-06-22 |title=Not so happy birthday for Samy |work=The Star Online |url=http://thestar.com.my/news/story.asp?file=/2008/3/9/nation/20590882&sec=nation |access-date=2024-10-02 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110622035921/http://thestar.com.my/news/story.asp?file=/2008/3/9/nation/20590882&sec=nation |archive-date=22 June 2011 }}</ref>

== History ==
{{Politics of Malaysia}}

=== The end of PSRM (1990) ===
The last socialist party to exist in Malaysia, Parti Sosialis Rakyat Malaysia (PSRM), reverted to its original name to [[Parti Rakyat Malaysia]] in 1990 before merging with [[Parti Keadilan Rakyat]] in 2003.

=== Formation of PSM (1990–1998) ===
In 1991, several [[grassroots]] based organisations working with the urban and rural poor in Malaysia formed an alliance. These included the Jawatankuasa  Sokongan Peneroka  Bandar led by Dr [[Mohd Nasir Hashim]],  Alaigal led by Dr [[Michael Jeyakumar Devaraj]] and Pusat Pembangunan  Masyarakat, which was headed by [[Arutchelvan Subramaniam|S. Arutchelvan]].<ref>https://www.researchgate.net/publication/329268390_Parti-parti_Politik_Kiri_dalam_Pilihan_Raya_Umum_ke-14</ref>

In 1994, the groups staged a massive Labour Day [[Demonstration (people)|demonstration]] at the heart of [[Kuala Lumpur]], surprising many people. The last major demonstration called by the working class in the capital city was in the 1970s.

In 1995, these grassroots organisation who already had their strong support among the [[plantation]] workers, the urban poor, and industrial workers mooted the idea for a political party to represent the aspirations of the poor and the marginalised. The election results in 1995 gave a sweeping win to the ruling [[Barisan Nasional]] and hastened this process.

After years of discussion and consolidations, it was finally agreed that a party with socialist ideology was needed to liberate the masses from their current conditions. With this in mind, the groups took more than two and the half years to draft the party's constitution, which was ready by the end of 1997. 
After further consultation with their electoral base, on 1 May 1998, the new party known as the Socialist Party of Malaysia officially submitted its application  for registration. Former PSRM central committee member and ex-ISA detainee Mohd Nasir Hashim was named pro-tem party chairman during this time.{{citation needed|date=December 2024}}

==== Legal status ====
The Federal Government then rejected the application to register as a political party, alleging that PSM was a threat to national security. 

However, because the right to form a political party is guaranteed in the constitution, PSM took the government and the Home Minister to court for abusing their power. Although the [[Court of Appeal (Malaysia)|Court of Appeal]] dismissed the national security argument on 16 August 2006, it upheld a separate reason to deny the registration of the PSM as a political party. 

PSM then filed an appeal against the Court of Appeal's decision to the [[Federal Court of Malaysia]]. However, on 17 June 2008, the Home Ministry approved PSM's application as a political party just before Federal Court proceedings started, ending a 10-year tussle.<ref>{{cite news|publisher=Malaysiakini|date=17 June 2007|access-date=18 June 2008|title=PSM allowed to register as political party|url=http://malaysiakini.com/news/84578|format=fee required}}</ref><ref name="PSM registered">{{cite news|publisher=Official Socialist Party of Malaysia website|access-date=20 June 2008|title=PSM allowed to register as political party|url=http://www.parti-sosialis.org/?p=319}}</ref>

=== 1999 general election ===
In 1999, the party decided to contest in [[Malaysian general election, 1999|the year's general election]]. Since PSM was not registered, it had to contest under some other party's logo. In 1999, the candidates contested under a [[Democratic Action Party (Malaysia)|Democratic Action Party]] ticket. The main intention was to popularise the party, while it lost the contest, Jeyakumar managed to reduce his opponent's majority by 10,000 votes.

=== 2004 general election ===
[[Image:election 2004.jpg|right|thumb|PSM candidates fielded for the [[Malaysian general election, 2004|2004 general election]].]]

After the 1999 election, PSM opened its service centre in Sungai Siput. The party claimed that DAP was not happy with this and demanded that Jeyakumar join DAP if he wished to stand again.

He declined and sought a meeting with Keadilan leader Dr [[Wan Azizah Wan Ismail]]. As a result PSM was able to contest under the logo of Parti Keadilan Nasional (now known as [[Parti Keadilan Rakyat]]).<ref>{{Cite web |title=OUR STORY |url=https://partisosialis.org/our-story/#ideology |access-date=2024-10-02 |website=Parti Sosialis Malaysia |language=en-US}}</ref>

=== 2008 general election ===
Three PSM members contested in the [[Malaysian general election, 2008|2008 general election]] under the PKR banner while another ran as an independent. Two of these candidates won PSM's first ever seats. 

Candidate Michael Jeyakumar Devaraj won PSM's first ever [[Parliament of Malaysia|federal parliamentary]] seat by defeating then president of [[Malaysian Indian Congress]] and long-serving Minister of Works [[Samy Vellu]] in Sungai Siput.  

At the same time, PSM president [[Mohd Nasir Hashim]] won the Kota Damansara seat in the [[Selangor]] state legislative assembly. 

Although [[Arutchelvan Subramaniam]] lost, PSM's election campaigning resulted in an increase in membership in [[Semenyih]]. 

The remaining member who contested Jelapang as an independent was M. Sarasvathy.<ref>Peter Boyle, [http://www.greenleft.org.au/2008/745/38545 Electoral break-through to test socialists], Green Left Weekly, 29 March 2008</ref>

=== 2013 general election ===
The two PSM incumbents, Michael Jeyakumar Devaraj and Nasir Hashim, recontested their seats in the [[Malaysian general election, 2013|2013 general election]] under Keadilan.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Vengadesan |first=Martin |date=2013-04-17 |title=PSM Setuju Tanding Di DUN Kota Damansara, Parlimen Sungai Siput Guna Lambang PKR - Semasa {{!}} mStar |url=https://www.mstar.com.my/lokal/semasa/2013/04/17/psm-setuju-tanding-di-dun-kota-damansara-parlimen-sungai-siput-guna-lambang-pkr |access-date=2026-04-19 |website=www.mstar.com.my |language=en}}</ref> 

Jeyakumar retained the PSM's [[Parliament of Malaysia|parliamentary]] seat of Sungai Siput, but PSM president Nasir lost the Kota Damansara state seat to a [[United Malays National Organisation]] (UMNO) candidate. This came after a PAS member joined the contest to split the opposition vote.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2013-04-18 |title=PSM chief's seat not settled as PAS steps in |url=https://m.malaysiakini.com/news/227195 |access-date=2024-10-02 |website=Malaysiakini}}</ref>

S. Arutchelvan and M. Sarasvathy also recontested the same seats, this time under the PSM ticket, and lost heavily.<ref>{{Cite web |title=PSM candidate in Semenyih concedes defeat {{!}} The Malaysian Insight |url=https://www.themalaysianinsight.com/s/47954 |access-date=2024-10-02 |website=www.themalaysianinsight.com |language=en}}</ref>

=== 2018 general election ===
For the [[Malaysian general election, 2018|2018 general election]], PSM contested in five federal constituencies and 14 state constituencies. All PSM candidates for this general election competed under PSM name and logo, as opposed to the previous elections, meaning that its cooperation with parties like PKR and DAP came to an end .<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.thesundaily.my/news/2018/01/11/psm-contest-seats-using-their-own-logos-not-pkrs|title=PSM to contest seats using their own logos, not PKR's}}</ref> 

Unlike in previous elections, Nasir Hashim announced that he was not running in the 2018 election.

PSM did not succeed in winning any seats this election and lost the [[Sungai Siput (federal constituency)|Sungai Siput]] parliamentary seat that it had held since 2008.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Arutchelvan |first=S. |date=2019-07-23 |title=PSM crosses 20-year hurdle |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/484978 |access-date=2024-10-02 |publisher=[[Malaysiakini]]}}</ref>

Due in part to the decision to contest independently of the [[Pakatan Harapan]] coalition, PSM suffered a heavy defeat with all candidates, including Dr Jeyakumar, losing their deposits.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Vengadesan |first=Martin |date=2019-07-16 |title=Red doctor at the helm of PSM - 'It's time to reach out' |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/483953 |access-date=2024-10-02 |website=Malaysiakini}}</ref>

=== 2022 general election and 2023 state elections ===
In the 2022 General elections, PSM took a back seat, with Dr Jeyakumar declining to run in Sungai Siput.<ref>{{Cite web |title='Giant killer' Dr Jeyakumar prepared to step aside in Sungai Siput |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2022/10/13/giant-killer-dr-jeyakumar-prepared-to-step-aside-in-sungai-siput |access-date=2024-10-02 |website=The Star |date=13 October 2022 |language=en}}</ref>

On 15 July 2023, PSM and [[Malaysian United Democratic Alliance]] (MUDA) announced a political alliance for the [[2023 Malaysian state elections|2023 state elections]]. The elections were held in Selangor, Kelantan, Terengganu, Negeri Sembilan, Kedah and Penang on 12 August 2023, and the PSM/MUDA alliance attempted to position itself as the "third choice" for voters "who are unhappy and bored with the politicians now and want to protest."<ref>{{Cite web |last=Chan |first=Julia |date=2023-07-15 |title=Muda, PSM join forces for state polls to take a stand against racial politics |url=https://www.malaymail.com/news/malaysia/2023/07/15/muda-psm-join-forces-for-state-polls-to-take-a-stand-against-racial-politics/79891 |access-date=2023-07-15 |website=Malay Mail |language=en}}</ref>

In July 2024, the party held its 26th annual congress, at which Sivaranjani Manickam was elected secretary-general replacing A Sivarajan.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Vengadesan |first=Martin |date=2024-07-28 |title=Sivaranjani Manickam elected PSM sec-gen |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/713362 |access-date=2024-08-04 |website=Malaysiakini}}</ref>

Over the years, the party's three main front organisations established more than a hundred sub-fronts, and it still believes in grassroots work done with the masses especially the plantation workers, the urban poor, industrial workers, peasants and also collaborates with the progressive student movement.

PSM was also at the forefront of the successful battle to establish a minimum wage in Malaysia.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Vengadesan |first=Martin |date=2022-03-23 |title=PSM chair: Minimum wage raise laudable, still short of what's needed |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/615529 |access-date=2024-10-02 |website=Malaysiakini}}</ref>

Aside from electoral contests and grassroots campaigns, PSM is also known for organising its annual Labour Day march<ref>{{Cite web |last=Vengadesan |first=Martin |date=2022-05-01 |title=Labour Day: Fight for workers' rights will go on, says Arul |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/619880 |access-date=2026-04-19 |website=Malaysiakini}}</ref> and international socialism conferences held every November.<ref>{{Cite web |last=VENGADESAN |first=MARTIN |date=2018-11-21 |title=Prominent activists due in town for December's Socialism 2018 forum |url=https://www.thestar.com.my/news/nation/2018/11/21/prominent-activists-due-in-town-for-decembers-socialism-2018-forum |access-date=2026-04-19 |website=The Star |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |last=Vengadesan |first=Martin |date=2021-11-25 |title=Regional socialist conference kicks off this weekend |url=https://www.malaysiakini.com/news/600548 |access-date=2026-04-19 |website=Malaysiakini}}</ref>

=== Seven-Point Manifesto ===
PSM has a seven-point manifesto which lists the following policies:<ref>[http://www.parti-sosialis.org/manifesto Seven Part Manifesto], Official Socialist Party of Malaysia website. Retrieved 21 August 2010</ref>

# Workers' rights will be safeguarded (e.g. minimum wage, automatic recognition of workers unions and 90-day maternal leave).
# The eradication of neo-liberal policies (e.g. halting privatisation of health care, education and other public necessities).
# Stopping the Free Trade Agreement with western imperial powers.
# Provide comfortable and humane housing for both rural and urban inhabitants.
# Stopping racial and religious politics to foster greater unity among the people.
# Eradication of corruption and abuse of power.
# Stopping the destruction of the environment.
